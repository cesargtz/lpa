Partnering for Results

Partnering for Results

February 11, 2016
By
Dora Moscoso

The fast pace of information technology has penetrated every sector on the planet.  It has revolutionized medicine, architecture, art, the media, and has changed the way we communicate and express ourselves every day.  It is not surprising that now it is also becoming the cornerstone of the monitoring and evaluation (M&E) function of development projects.  No development project is conceived without a results-monitoring capacity, given its intrinsic nature; therefore, timely and effective achievement of development results is demanding an even greater scrutiny of project-generated data.

The good news is that a great variety of tools for data collection are now available in the market. Unfortunately, a single space to centralize all these tools, and what would be even better, to rate their efficacy has not been developed.  Well, not until the Impact Tracker Technology Catalogue was developed by Kopernik.  

The Catalogue’s audience is small and medium social enterprises and nonprofit organizations working in development and humanitarian assistance.  It provides an assessment of data collection applications and other IT platforms based on five criteria:  affordability, usability, rapidity (sending large volumes of data on a real-time basis), scalability (ability to handle multiple services and users), and transferability (flexibility of usage across sectors and contexts). The tools assessed range from digital data collection applications (for smartphones and tablets), SMS communications platforms (to communicate with clients and beneficiaries), geospatial mapping tools, and remote sensors (GPS-enabled to measure usage of devices such as cook stoves, water filters, toilets, etc.).

Despite the overwhelming usefulness of Kopernik’s assessment, the rapid pace of technology which involves the creation of brand new applications and faster and more sophisticated tools, requires that this effort be done continuously in order to serve its purpose.  Also, there is no such a thing as the “right tool” for one organization. Some organizations are tempted to develop their own tools in an effort to have complete control of the data.  Development agencies should strive to identify which of these technologies are more affordable and adequate to the needs of their partner agencies and clients, in order to facilitate reporting efforts.  

If there is one affirmation that we all hold true, is that development projects must yield impact.  To evidence these changes, the function of continuing project data gathering and analysis during project implementation should be done in the easiest and fastest way possible.  The objective of this data gathering effort is to learn, and to make needed changes during implementation, so that these interventions are effective and reach those who need them the most.  One of the strengths of these tools is its real-time application, which would allow us to identify challenges and obstacles that arise and need immediate attention.  

Technology is now on our side, and despite its fast pace, much can be accomplished if international organizations, global NGOs, and donors foster a concerted effort to continuously innovate in M&E by identifying the appropriate tools that our beneficiaries need for simpler and faster data gathering.   In fact, this is one other area in which the MIF can innovate, as information and communication technology tools are needed in every sector across the region.  The MIF’s innovative character allows it to provide this very specific type of support to its partners.  The MIF should identify, assess, and map the ICT tools currently available for data collection and reporting efforts, and play a facilitator role with its partner agencies on the most adequate ones that would be the best fit to their data collection and assessment needs.
