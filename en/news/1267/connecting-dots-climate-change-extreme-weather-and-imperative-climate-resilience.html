Connecting the Dots: Climate Change, Extreme Weather and the Imperative for Climate Resilience

Connecting the Dots: Climate Change, Extreme Weather and the Imperative for Climate Resilience

September 19, 2012
By
Steve Wilson
The great majority of climate scientists have avoided linking specific extreme weather events to global climate change. A typical disclaimer goes something like this: “We must emphasize that one cannot attribute the cause of any specific weather event, such as a heat wave, drought or other occurrence to global climate change.”
 
This convention may be changing.
 
A paper published in August 2012 in the early edition of the Proceedings of the National Academy of Science, “Perception of Climate Change”, argues that climate change is the only plausible explanation for several specific extreme weather events in the past two decades.   
 
Moreover, the authors, James Hansen, a noted climate scientist at the U.S. National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) Goddard Institute for Space Studies, and Makiko Sato and Reto Ruedy of Columbia University’s Earth Institute, base their conclusions exclusively on the statistical analysis of surface temperature data.
 
While Hansen and his co-authors clearly believe that humans are the main drivers behind global climate change, their analysis does not rely on any specific theory of climate change, or on a particular climate model, or any interpretation of meteorological phenomena, such as El Niños. 
 
Instead, this paper analyzes objective surface air temperature data collected by NASA satellites and by National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) meteorological stations on the ground. The authors model surface temperature variation using basic statistical tools such as standard deviation, within a normal distribution as displayed by the familiar “bell curve.” Using temperature data from a base period of 1951-1980, their paper compares the observed variability of seasonal mean surface air temperature in more recent decades. Based on this analysis, the authors reach several important conclusions. 
 
First, in the decades since 1980, extreme temperature variability has increased across the globe in ways that cannot be explained by natural variability alone. Temperature anomalies are increasing and widespread, from North America to Asia, and in the equatorial areas of the Pacific Ocean.    
 
Secondly, there has emerged in the past two decades a pattern of extremely hot summers (i.e. more than 3 standard deviations from the mean temperature of summers in the base period 1951-1980) in both the northern and southern hemispheres.  In addition, the surface area of the Earth affected by extremely hot summers has increased by more than a factor of 10 over the earlier baseline period.  Winters show similar trends, but with fewer extremes in variability.  
 
As is well known, higher average temperatures add more water vapor to the atmosphere. This excess will be worked out through the water cycle, in the form of excess rainfall or as severe drought. In winters, increased water vapor often translates into greater snowfall, a counter-intuitive and confusing event for many who may ask how rising average temperatures can cause more snow!
 
Third, the statistical significance of the shift in the number and degree of extreme temperature events across the globe leads the authors to conclude that climate change is loading the “climate dice” in ways that increase the probability of these anomalies:
 
“One implication of this shift is that the extreme summer climate anomalies in Texas in 2011, in Moscow in 2010, and in France in 2003 almost certainly would not have occurred in the absence of global warming… In other words, we can say with high confidence that such extreme anomalies would not have occurred in the absence of global warming.”
 
The authors note that many people now alive have experienced significant temperature variability in their own lifetimes, particularly those who remember and contrast any portion of the base period 1950-1980 with the last two to three decades.  
 
Joe Romm, in his influential climate blog Climate Progress, notes that recent studies corroborate Hansen’s conclusions. The growing support of other researchers, coupled with the accuracy of Hansen’s earlier climate predictions in the 1980 make this paper, in Romm’s words, a ”must-read.” We are inclined to agree.
 
Another recent publication, the 2012 Special Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPPC), Managing the Risks of Extreme Events and Disasters to Advance Climate Change, provides further evidence, albeit highly nuanced, that a warming planet is correlated with an increase in extreme weather events. 
 
This report, in the tradition of the IPCC, identifies emerging consensus within a large scientific literature and ties these findings to specific recommendations for managing climate risks in various contexts.  Both Hansen’s piece and this IPCC report make important reading for anyone interested in the issue of climate adaptation and resilience.
 
The growing scientific evidence connecting climate change and extreme weather, particularly in the form of drought, heat waves and heavy rains, confirms what many people across the globe already suspect: weather patterns are changing, and the world is generally a hotter, less meteorologically stable place. 
 
The empirical work of Hansen and others is also important because it demonstrates that extreme weather is increasing now, and thereby impacting livelihoods, communities and infrastructure. Over the longer term, climate change mitigation will be the key to solving this challenge. But in the shorter run, the political economy of climate negotiations, carbon policies, and regulations will be less important to the lives and businesses of many.
 
Unlike climate change mitigation, climate adaptation is everywhere local. To date, most work done to improve climate resilience in households, municipalities and businesses has been done by local MSMEs. These enterprises may describe this work as simply “doing business,” but they have been the first line in helping many people to rebuild, to improve irrigation methods, to protect supply chains, to protect personal and business assets, etc.
 
Today, the Multilateral Investment Fund is one of the few institutions piloting private sector adaptation and resilience projects on the ground with MSMEs and local communities. Over the past ten years, MIF has been providing technical assistance grants to spur adaptation and climate resilience in the private sector, and more programs are under development.
 
Working with MSMEs in Barbados, Belize, Jamaica and the Bahamas, the MIF is assisting enterprises (in tourism, agriculture, fishing and crafts), communities and local governments in implementing climate adaptation plans. Successful pilot experiences in this program will be extended to an additional 40 small island and coastal communities in the Caribbean region.
 
In Tabasco, Mexico the MIF has worked with the local center for entrepreneurship, the Centro Empresarial de Tabasco (CET) to help over 500 SMEs in the tourist, retail and food processing sectors to  improve their climate resilience. This project has also created an early warning extreme weather system for businesses using SMS texts, as well as a climate risk atlas for MSMEs in the Tabasco area.  Further, this project has put in place an innovative online assessment tool to evaluate company’s capacities to respond to extreme weather events and climate related risks. 
 
The MIF has also supported an Emergency Liquidity Fund that provided $5 million in emergency liquidity funds to microfinance institutions with micro and small business clients who were hit hard by local extreme weather events. This type of financing facility can help small firms and microenterprises to survive the disruptions caused by hurricanes and other damaging weather events.
 
Going forward, national adaptation planning and public sources of adaptation finance will also prove to be vital resources for developing countries in LAC and elsewhere. In the meantime, the practical research done by Hansen foreshadows the demand for products and services needed to respond to extreme weather. The MIF will continue to help many MSMEs to lower their own climate risks, while converting the risks of others into business opportunities.
