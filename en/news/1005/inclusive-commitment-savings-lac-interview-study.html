Inclusive Commitment Savings in LAC: An interview on the study

Inclusive Commitment Savings in LAC: An interview on the study

November 19, 2013
By
Avril Perez

La versión en español del blog está disponible en la página del programa ProAhorros

Early in 2013, the ProSavings team announced the launch of a study that set out to do what no one in the realm of savings and financial inclusion had done before, map and analyze the commitment savings product supply in 26 countries of Latin America and the Caribbean – thus contributing to filling the huge knowledge gap that remains in the region. In an interview with Andrea Reyes (ProSavings Program Coordinator) and Fermin Vivanco (MIF Sr. Specialist), who originally devised and supervised this study, they talk us through the process of conceiving this study and their reactions to its results. What was your reasoning behind launching this study on commitment savings? Andrea Reyes: We were mostly motivated by the lack of information regarding commitment savings products in LAC. Most of the projects under the ProSavings Program are developing commitment saving products for low-income people because these products share similarities with the methodologies that this niche uses to save. However, there are few experiences and data available about the different product designs, number of clients with commitment savings, best practices, etc., which are important to learn and develop a business strategy for microsavings.

Fermin Vivanco: Adding to what Andrea said, I think that financial intermediaries should be able to know in advance if a commitment savings product is likely to succeed. Currently there are many unknowns as to how to design commitment savings products that are both appealing to clients and profitable for financial intermediaries. Through this study we sought to identify concrete examples of successful applications.

What where your initial expectations as to the results you would obtain?

Andrea Reyes: Initially, I was expecting data about products in LAC including the number of institutions offering these savings products, number of clients, savings portfolios, and best practices. But what we have found so far with the study is beyond my expectations and I am sure this study will be an important tool for financial inclusion industry.

Fermin Vivanco: Absolutely, for the first time we have a solid framework to help financial intermediaries introduce changes to their products to make them better for the clients.

So tell me, why are commitment savings important to you?

Andrea Reyes: To me the most fascinating aspect about commitment savings is the motivation component these products incorporate to achieve a goal or a dream, no matter the level of income that you have, this is a powerful tool to save. Having a goal set in your mind, flexibility in the amount of money deposited periodically, access to an account, available channels and incentives - this is the perfect combination to save in an easier and safer way.

Fermin Vivanco: Commitment savings provide both the client and the provider with a framework for monitoring savings’ growth. Liquid savings products are essential to manage family budgets on a day to day basis, but commitment savings will help low-income people build resources to get out of poverty. To get out of poverty the key is not what you earn but what you save. Commitment savings is a financial instrument that really can fight poverty.

So according to the study, findings show that you managed to identify 3,315 regulated and unregulated financial institutions offering savings products in the region, did this number surprise you? Andrea Reyes: Yes definitely, it was a surprising result. It confirms the lack of data available about savings – for instance the MIX has 168 institutions reporting savings - but this also shows an opportunity to learn and deepen the knowledge on savings. What about you Fermin?

Fermin Vivanco: Same. I thought - many institutions are going to be very interested in learning about products that will increase their clients base, and savings among their current clients.

Looking back at the results of the study, what other knowledge gaps can you identify that still need to be filled in this subject?

Andrea Reyes: One thing is certain, there is a need for more research in this field. There is not a lot of evidence about how low-income people are saving. There is a lack of studies about the regulation that promotes financial inclusion for the poor, how it has been used by the financial institutions, and if it really helps both the client and the financial institution.

Fermin Vivanco: To this I would add- what are the niches being served and not served with commitment savings in LAC? Are new regulations on basic savings accounts being used to offer commitment savings

Thank you both. The initial results of this mapping exercise give us an idea of the immense opportunity that exists when it comes to savings, particularly targeted to the low-income demographics of the region. Nonetheless, for Xavier Martin, who conducted the study, and the ProSavings team, the task did not end there. The team went a step further into selecting from these institutions success stories of products being offered in the region that met both the needs of clients and the needs for profitability of financial institutions. This task required a laborious initial survey to over 400 institutions in the region and 14 field visits across 5 countries. The results of this work will be published soon through the ProSavings Program website and will be further discussed in a subsequent post. In the meantime you can read the first two articles that detail the results of this mapping exercise.
