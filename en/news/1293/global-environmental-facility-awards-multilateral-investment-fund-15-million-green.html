Global Environmental Facility awards Multilateral Investment Fund $15 million for Green Venture Capital

Global Environmental Facility awards Multilateral Investment Fund $15 million for Green Venture Capital

July 05, 2012
By
Georg Neumann

The Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF) has been awarded $15 million in reimbursable resources by the Global Environmental Facility (GEF), under an innovative new GEF program for Public-Private Partnerships. 

By leveraging and strategically applying these resources, this endeavor will bolster green business projects and ventures in Latin America and the Caribbean, improving access to finance for entrepreneurs in a budding, yet underfunded, industry. 

Through venture capital funds, the MIF will invest in private sector projects that combat climate change, preserve biodiversity and encourage the sustainable use of natural resources. These will include clean and renewable technologies, such as wind, solar, small scale hydro, waste to energy, and other sources of clean energy. 

Investments will also be made in projects that improve energy efficiency, thereby reducing greenhouse gas emissions and offering attractive returns. The MIF venture capital funds will target smaller companies with business models and technologies that allow for the sustainable use of natural resources and the preservation of biodiversity. This has the potential to create opportunities for local microenterprises, small businesses, and low income populations, including women and indigenous groups.  

Venture capital is a powerful vehicle for promoting innovation, entrepreneurship, and risk-taking in promising new technologies and markets, including the environmental space. The MIF is the leading investor and promoter of smaller venture capital funds in Latin America and the Caribbean, having approved 60 funds with over US$250 million committed across the region. 

The GEF Public-Private Partnership (PPP) program is a new initiative that supports green investments in the private sector. Programs are executed by multilateral development banks, and use non-grant instruments to improve access to financing for companies pursuing green technologies and innovative business models. Non-grant instruments include equity fund investments, contingent grants, credit guarantees, concessional loans, and performance risk guarantees, revolving funds, and risk sharing funds for loan provision. These instruments offer private sector partners a range of financing options to support new approaches and technologies, and can be helpful in attracting significant private sector co-financing.

The Global Environmental Facility is the largest source of public funding for projects that seek to improve the global environment. It provides grants related to climate change, biodiversity, international waters, land degradation, the ozone layer, and persistent organic pollutants. Since 1991, the GEF has provided $10.5 billion in grants and leveraged $51 billion in co-financing for over 2,700 projects in over 165 countries.
