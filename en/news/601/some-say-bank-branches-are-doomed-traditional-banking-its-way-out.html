Some say bank branches are doomed; is traditional banking on its way out?

Some say bank branches are doomed; is traditional banking on its way out?

December 14, 2015
By
Maria Luisa Hayem

Solución Asea’s clients conducting transaction at a small business in Mexico 

In my recent visits to two branches of two major banks in the United States, I witnessed the discontent of bank clients waiting to be served by a single teller. The rest of the teller windows seemed abandoned.

At one of the branches, the bank’s staff tried to help clear the line by suggesting people use the automatic teller machine (ATM) instead, but few took them up on that offer. Perhaps they didn’t hold a debit card from that bank, or needed to make high-volume transactions, or simply wanted to be helped by a person and not an ATM.

Whether we like it or not, we aren’t likely to see the return of multiple tellers in every bank building anytime soon. Bank branches require high levels of investment and involve significant operative costs. In Latin America and the Caribbean they are not viable in remote areas with dispersed low-income populations. So the challenge–not only in the United States, but also in Latin America and the Caribbean—is developing business strategies to replace the traditional bank branch system that will be both convenient for clients and cost-effective for financial services providers. 

Many financial intermediaries in Latin America and the Caribbean have decided to bring their products and services closer to the clients through agents, which partner with existing small businesses so they don’t need their own facilities, and offer services such as payments, deposits, and withdrawals. In México, for instance, between 2010 and 2013, the number of bank branches grew by 5.5%, while the number of agents increased by 40.7%. In some countries, regulation allows for bank accounts to be opened through these channels. The MIF’s project “Financial Inclusion of Beneficiaries of the Solidaridad Program,” implemented by the Asociación La Nacional de Ahorros y Préstamos (ALNAP) in the Dominican Republic, is an example of client relations occurring outside of bank branches. 

Financial institutions and telecommunication companies are also betting on the use of mobile technology as a way to facilitate sending and receiving e-money. Through mobile phones, clients can also access information on their balances, and make payments, withdrawals, and cash deposits through ATMs and/or agents. According to GSMA, an organization that represents the interests of mobile operators worldwide, in 2014, 37 initiatives were being carried out in 19 markets in Latin America and the Caribbean, including new operations in  Brazil,  Colombia, the Dominican Republic, Ecuador, Panama, and Peru. 

In the coming years, making contact with the financial system for the first time might not require contacting a bank employee or visiting a branch. New clients might only need to go to a bank branch once or twice a year, or maybe never. But banks that want to succeed will have to make sure that those few branch visits are not as frustrating as what I experienced recently! 

So how are financial institutions going to stay close to their clients and become aware of their needs, preferences, and behaviors? How are they going to help their clients understand how to make the best use of available products and services? Data generated by transactional behavior will certainly play an important role, but will this be enough to provide access to quality financial products and services that improve individuals’ livelihoods?

Financial institutions and telecommunications companies will have to continue their efforts to better understand clients, and use marketing campaigns, agent banking, and mobile technology effectively to achieve financial inclusion that both benefits clients and is financially sustainable for the various market players. We at the MIF are testing new ways to make this happen.
