Dignity in the dump

Dignity in the dump

December 06, 2012
By
Elizabeth Terry

We had a visit from a movie star at the IDB this week! Sebastiao “Tiao” dos Santos is one of the principal subjects of “Waste Land,” a 2010 documentary that won many prestigious film festival prizes and was even nominated for an Oscar. The film depicts the community of catadores, or “waste pickers,” who eke out a meager living collecting and selling recyclable materials in Jardim Gramacho, a massive landfill on the outskirts of Rio de Janeiro. Renowned artist Vik Muniz spent time at the dump creating striking multimedia portraits of some of the catadores, and the documentary shows the process of the artistic collaboration and how the artist and his subjects inspired each other.

Tiao spoke of how his mother and older siblings supported their family by working as catadores, and how even though he felt stigmatized in childhood by school bullies who called him a “trash eater’s son,” he didn’t fully realize the inequality in which he’d grown up until he was a bit older and had the opportunity to take a class in youth leadership. His voice softened as he recalled how he lashed out at the teacher on the last day of class, feeling that he’d been happier before he became aware of poverty and injustice around him and began alienating his friends and family with political rants. “Who said you’d be happy coming here?” responded the teacher, and challenged young Tiao to channel his anger and frustration into making a difference for his community. He did just that, educating himself further by reading the writings of revolutionary thinkers like Che Guevara and Machiavelli, and eventually forming and leading the Association of Recycling Pickers of Jardim Gramacho.

“I am not a trash picker. I am a professional who works in waste collection.”

The economists and specialists in the audience chuckled as Tiao related an anecdote about being interviewed on a leading Brazilian talk show. The host referred to Tiao as a “trash picker,” and although it embarrassed him to challenge a prominent TV personality, Tiao corrected him, knowing that he’d get an earful from his mother and siblings if he didn’t. “I am not a trash picker. I am a professional who works in waste collection,” he said. Tiao is passionate not only about raising awareness among his fellow catadores about the importance of the work they do, but even more so about raising awareness among all Brazilians about the vital role of catadores and about the responsibility we all have to recycle. He shared a video from the “Limpa Brasil” (“Clean Up Brazil”) campaign that has gotten over 100,000 people to volunteer to pick up trash in their communities, and talked about new laws on the books that are closing large landfills like Jardim Gramacho and setting new recycling policies for national and local governments and for industry.

As inspiring as Tiao’s story is, I was even more touched by a short clip from the documentary that featured another catador named Valter, who explained that he was proud of his work because he knew that every bottle and box he recycled was one fewer that might end up polluting a river. I was really moved by the contrast between this humble man standing on an ugly mountain of trash, and his admonition about the need to protect the beauty of nature.

In her introduction to the Q&A with Tiao, MIF General Manager Nancy Lee talked about the work we are doing in the region on behalf of informal recyclers, creating opportunities for them to increase their incomes and formalize their work. Last year, the MIF, in partnership with the IDB’s Water and Sanitation Division, the Avina Foundation and the Coca-Cola Company, launched The Regional Inclusive Recycling Initiative, which will contribute to the integration of “waste pickers” into formal recycling markets, thereby improving their incomes, working conditions and standards of living. This initiative builds on the MIF’s experience working with informal recyclers in Argentina, Bolivia, Brazil, Colombia, and Peru, and projects we are just starting in Chile and the Dominican Republic. She said, “We know recyclers have a clear role as economic and environmental actors. They are not the problem, but are a key part of the solution.” 

The problem she speaks of is huge—according to Tiao, Brazil alone generates 240,000 tons of waste every day, only 2% of which is recycled. So the solution will need to be equally huge, calling on major players like the IDB, large companies and national governments, and small (yet essential) actors like Tiao, Valter, and the millions like them around the world who spend their days sorting through trash.
