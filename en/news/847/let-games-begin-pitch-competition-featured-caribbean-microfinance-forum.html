Let the games begin: Pitch competition featured at Caribbean Microfinance Forum

Let the games begin: Pitch competition featured at Caribbean Microfinance Forum

June 12, 2014
By
Guest

  Facilitators Lisa Harding of the Caribbean Development Bank and Yvonne Chin Irving, a media consultant, counting ballots to determine the winning youth track project proposal.

 

By Wayne Beecher and Trang Ho 

Over 132 participants and 32 presenters were in for a special treat at the Fifth Caribbean Microfinance Forum (#CaribForumV) in Montego Bay, Jamaica, last June 2-5, 2014.  The conference, titled “Building an Environment for Entrepreneurs to Excel,” focused on three tracks: youth entrepreneurship, rural economies, and technology and innovation.  On the conference’s second day, Jamaica-based MIF senior specialist Wayne Beecher presented a “boot camp” session on entrepreneurial design from a development business model. Wayne explained how to consider the value architecture, value delivery, value chain, and value proposition of a project proposal. This model also examines the inputs, activities, results, impact, and systemic impact of the project as well as the cost structure and revenue streams. Using what they learned in boot camp and during the conference’s other sessions, teams from the three tracks then created project proposals and took part in a pitch competition. Energy ran high in the breakout sessions as innovative project ideas took shape. In the competition’s first round, one proposal from each of the tracks was selected, and then in the final round, the three finalists battled it out before judges from the IDB, the European Investment Bank, and the Development Bank of Jamaica. In the youth track, the selected proposal was a program that would use a specialized evaluation process to increase the number of young entrepreneurs who receive funding from microfinance institutions. The program would use a whole array of channels for outreach and training, ranging from direct mentorship to social media platforms.  The proposal from the rural track involved technology solutions that would create value for small farmers by helping them to better serve the supply needs of the hotel market. This market-driven project would support agricultural production forecasts, upfront payments to participating farmers, access to finance and technical support, and more.  The tech track proposed a mobile money platform that would create a branchless agency network to provide rural and youth entrepreneurs, as well as the unbanked, with a range of secure and affordable financial services like payments for purchases and microfinance transactions.   After much deliberation, the judges chose the rural track’s proposal as the grand prize winner, since their proposal was easy to understand, implementable, and addressed the key constraints facing rural farmers. At the same time, all participants benefited from having had the opportunity to work with this kind of project model, and now can start applying it to their own daily work.  The Forum was hosted under the auspices of the CARIB-CAP II project with the support of the MIF, the European Commission, the Caribbean Development Bank, and Citi Foundation. This year’s conference also received the support of the Development Bank of Jamaica, the National Youth Service, and HEART Trust/NTA in Jamaica.
