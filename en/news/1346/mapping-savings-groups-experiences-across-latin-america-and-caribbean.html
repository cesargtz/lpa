Mapping the Savings Groups’ Experiences across Latin America and the Caribbean

Mapping the Savings Groups’ Experiences across Latin America and the Caribbean

May 08, 2013
By
Avril Perez

By Xavier Martin and Avril Perez

The use of savings groups is not a recent practice. This model has been around for centuries and is currently frequent among societies lacking access to formal financial services. The benefit of participating in these groups is clear – if ten people each save $5 every month to purchase an item valued at $50, everyone will have to wait ten months to accumulate enough money to make such purchase. However, if these ten individuals gather up and form a savings group, most will be able to purchase the item prior to the ten month mark – one member will have access to $50 during the first month, another during the second month, and so on until the last member receives the money during the tenth month. The simplicity and immediate benefits offered by this model explain the popularity of these groups among low-income individuals and those living under extreme poverty.

In Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC), there is a long standing tradition of informal, decentralized, community savings and credit mechanisms. Most of the experiences with informal savings groups consist of very simple structures, in which every member of the group contributes the same amount and obtains credit by taking turns, without incurring interests. The names given to these informal savings models vary from country to country.

 

Despite the popularity of these informal saving models, their potential as a financial inclusion mechanism is limited. These savings models are not very flexible, and are more targeted towards short-term expenses. As these groups grow and their demand for larger loans increases, the system reaches a limit that can only be overcome through a greater flexibility in terms of the products being offered.
 
Likewise, the most common scenario is that not all individuals have the same savings capacity or the same opportunities for investing. Therefore, introducing interest rates provides a greater flexibility in the types of services that can be offered such that, within the same groups each member can function properly despite their different appetite for savings and credit. This increase in flexibility goes hand in hand with a more complex management, which makes it difficult for these types of initiatives to develop naturally without a greater level of institutionalization. 
 
Taking root on the experiences of informal savings groups, several cooperatives and other microfinance entities in LAC have promoted the development of savings and credit groups through different models and approaches. The underlying idea is that keeping a simple management structure can introduce a degree of flexibility to these groups and offer financial services at a low cost, thus enabling the accumulation of benefits for its members and promoting a savings discipline. Under this lens, savings groups are no longer just individuals gathering to purchase an item; rather they become more stable groups that can increase the access to formal financial services and can serve as a platform for delivering other types of non-financial services.
 
Within the LAC region, the MIF has supported several projects promoting savings groups based on different methodologies. Likewise, around the world, self-help groups have been populated in India and other south Asian countries; community savings groups projects have been successful in African countries, and several initiatives by local organizations have been working for decades in alternative methodologies for organizing credit and self-managed savings groups (solidarity funds, community banks, popular investment entities, credit companies, rural banks, local financial structures, etc.).
 
After decades of work with plenty of institutions in the promotion of savings and credit groups in Latin America and the Caribbean, a knowledge gap remains in regards to the learnings obtained from these types of interventions. Nowadays there is a wide array of detailed literature on successful savings groups’ experiences in other regions, particularly Africa; yet very little studies capture this same information for Latin America and the Caribbean. Therefore, through the ProSavings Program, the MIF is conducting a study to identify and document these experiences, analyzing their role within the current financial landscape. With this study the MIF expects to contribute to a better regional understanding of the opportunities, challenges and lessons learned from this type of interventions.
