Last week I spent a day on the road with Mateo, a mobile field officer from the Evangel Credit Union in Belize. He collects deposits and loan payments, as well as other cash transactions, for up to 200 credit union members per day while driving a truck through BelizeÂ´s countryside. His clients, most of them women with small scale family farms and businesses, see the truck stopping on the road and run out from their houses with crumpled bills in their hands, happy to make a deposit in a matter of seconds, instead of losing two to four hours in travel just to go to a credit union branch in Belize City. Mateo could be considered a human ATM, but he makes quick conversation and motivates people to keep up with their savings in a way that no ATM could. By recording each transaction in two places he is able to resolve any disputes which may arise. He also promotes credit union products on the road and enrolls about 40 new rural savings clients per month For many years I have observed the differences between recording microfinance client information electronically in handheld devices, and doing it manually. Contrary to conventional thinking, I am convinced that taking information manually is the best option, as itâs a more natural process and helps keep field officers aware of what they do while visiting clientsâ microbusinesses. Instead, loan officers with handheld devices are frequently fixated on the tablet or smartphone screen, worrying about reception or screen reflection. Many times they get frustrated with software imperfections and bugs or batteries that run out. Â Â  Is the implementation of solely manual systems the solution to rural finance? Â Not necessarily. The MIF, along with the Belize Credit Union League and participating credit unions, are planning to develop a shared rural agent network which will complement the role of the mobile field officers. While technology on the road can be a hassle, it can come in handy at stores. Credit union shared agents will be able to perform cash transactions at small shops in rural areas. By sharing retail space and the time with existing business, the transactions can be paid for through commissions and could become less costly. This way the Credit Union members can make transfers, deposits, withdrawals, and loan payments in small stores operating as service points for the Credit Union League. The big question is how to efficiently combine the roles of field officers and rural agents, which is something that this MIF project will try to help address.
