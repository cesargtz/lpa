How to do business in Haiti? Invest in women and in partnerships

How to do business in Haiti? Invest in women and in partnerships

June 20, 2013
By
Nara Meli

 

 

A few days ago, I attended the 4th Annual Haiti Business Investment Expo and Conference, here in Washington, D.C.

There was a large Haitian turnout among the participants: The Expo hosted no less than 48 Haitian businesses and associations, providing networking opportunities for the participants between the discussion sessions. Other NGOs, enterprises, financial institutions and donors also participated on panels such as “registering a business in Haiti”, “Haiti’s investment opportunities”, and “partnering to grow the manufacturing and textiles industry”.

Looking over my notes, I noticed two words I circled several times. The first is “partners!” “Partners are everything. Of course, you need lawyers, accountants. But without partners, you will be lost in Haiti. Partners will help you find your way,” said Philippe St. Cyr from the American Chamber of Commerce (AmCham) - Haiti. In a later session, similar words were spoken by Josef Leitmann, Fund Manager of the Haiti Reconstruction Fund (HRF), World Bank: “We managed difficulties by working with our partners – 19 donors and the Haitian government.

I had the opportunity to meet with Kit Miyamoto and Daniel O’Neil, of Miyamoto International, a global engineering company that specializes in disaster prevention and response. Kit arrived in Haiti right after the 2010 earthquake, and set to work through the World Bank, to help the Ministry of Transport and Public Works (MTPTC) to identify and grade (through a color-scheme) damaged buildings. Later, Miyamoto International established a branch in Haiti and hired and trained local Haitian companies to help with reconstruction. Miyamoto International is one of the investors in dloHaiti, which I mentioned here, when it became the first recipient of the Leopard Haiti Fund’s investment. When I asked him if he thought partnerships were important, he was emphatic in his response: “Good, strong partners are key. You can’t do anything in Haiti - or anywhere else, for that matter - without partners.” When I asked him where he found his partners, he said he got lucky: “I was initially looking for someone for translation purposes, and she happened to know everyone I needed to know!”

Where can one find Haitian partners, if not by luck? The Haitian Embassy in D.C. is one place. Another is the AmCham-Haiti. There is also the Center for Facilitation of Investments (CFI), and the Société Financière Haïtienne de Développement S.A (SOFIHDES), both in Haiti. And of course, the government: Mr. Eddy Clemens from the Ministry of Finance talked about the importance and the variety of public-private partnerships – particularly where the public sector “fails” - the private sector can step in and provide what is needed to fill the gap.

What was my second word? “Women!” The panel on their role in economic development should have been called an ignite event. The level of engagement and passion that emanated from the panelists was unparalleled. Three out of the four - Danielle St Lot, Haiti’s Ambassador at Large for Women Investors, Eveline Pierre, Campaigner of Empowerment, Yve-car Momperousse from Kreyol Essence, and Leigh Carter, CEO of Fonkoze USA - were Haitian women, and all presented strong cases for empowering women in Haiti. “Investing in women is not a question of entrepreneurship, but one of economic sense” said Ms. St Lot. Indeed, data shows that women entrepreneurs invest 90 percent of their incomes into their families, compared to 35-40 percent for men. “This isn’t a time for charity. That was after the earthquake. Now is the time for business.” Thankfully, she added, the conditions were ripe at the political level: “The president thinks like a woman! He has created programs to help women”, programs such as Ti Manman Cheri.

Due to its importance in Haiti’s development, the Diaspora is often involved in the discussions. What did it think of investing in Haiti? Opinions were split. Some Haitians had gone back and successfully started their business. Others found it too hard: “I tried opening a business in Haiti,” said an elderly lady, “but I was overwhelmed by the lack of infrastructure. [People living in Haiti] tell us to come back, but there are bad roads, the electricity is unreliable, the buildings are not good.” While coming back to Haiti is likely challenging, “you cannot wait for the infrastructure to get started” said Ms. St Lot, “you can’t wait for charity anymore.” “When I first started, I bootstrapped!” said Ms. Momperousse, “I didn’t ask for donations.” “Donations are nice,” said Nicolas Bussenius, an entrepreneur whose family founded the Hotel Mont Joli in Cap Haitien, “but they are not essential.” “You cannot grow a country with grants,” said Charles Clermont, the CEO of SOFIHDES. “Haiti needs investment, it needs to grow its private sector” and for that, it needs partners.

How did you find the right partner in Haiti? How can we, from the donor side, work to target more women in our projects? What kinds of interventions would most benefit Haitian women?
