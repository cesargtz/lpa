The Progress Out of Poverty Index: A solution to measure poverty reduction?

The Progress Out of Poverty Index: A solution to measure poverty reduction?

March 12, 2014
By
Claudia Gutierrez

Measuring poverty is a challenge in itself; just defining the word “poverty” could be subject to hours of debate in any international development organization. Let’s imagine that we want to “simplify” things, so we define poor as having an income level of less than $2 a day. Then, the task becomes how to estimate how much the people in a given community or group earn daily, since in general poor people have various and very unstable sources of income. So to learn more about their poverty levels, you have to ask them many questions, starting with where they live, how they live, what they do as a principal activity, as a secondary activity, as a temporary job, how many children they have, whether they receive remittances, if they borrow money... and so on and so forth. Imagine how difficult is to ask all of these questions, and even worse, how difficult it would be to answer them.

Now imagine that there is a tool that summarizes all of these questions so that with just 10 questions you can calculate someone’s probability of being poor. That would be great, wouldn’t it? Well, that is what is proposed by the Progress Out of Poverty Index (PPI), which was developed by Grameen Foundation in 2005.

As we were genuinely curious about learning more about how this tool is being implemented and how useful it is in measuring and tracking poverty, the MIF recently conducted a study that explored these aspects in more detail with institutions already implementing the tool. Our study found that the tool is indeed highly appreciated for its simplicity, but implementing it and interpreting the results require additional support, which is not always readily available. The tool has the potential to provide useful information, as long as it is implemented correctly. Grameen Foundation has done an admirable job providing guidelines and toolkits to help institutions interested in applying the tool. However, more hands and minds are needed; our study recommends that national or regional networks and national governments combine efforts and work together for the common purpose of measuring and tracking poverty.

The study also mentions some limitations regarding the tool’s accuracy. Ten questions to predict the probability of being poor requires one to accept many strong assumptions, which, as noted above, can be subject to endless discussions. But, for some purposes where high precision in estimates is not needed, the balance between accuracy and the simplicity and low cost offered by the tool could be enough to consider it a reasonable solution. The developers of the tool are very transparent about what the tool can and cannot do; the methodology and the details regarding its construction are available on their web site, and all the tool’s users can analyze this material to see if the information the PPI provides is ultimately useful.

Our study is intended to be a source of information for people interested in tools for measuring poverty; it provides real life examples of institutions applying the tool, the challenges they faced and what advantages and disadvantages they find in its application. I think simplicity should be the main quality of any poverty measurement tool. This study explores one approach, but perhaps there are other tools that have the same benefits, or there may be other experiences from institutions that have used this tool or others that will be valuable to share. Let’s take advantage of the knowledge others have and use this space to learn. If you have been asking the same questions about how to measure poverty and have ideas, let us know, by leaving a comment here on the blog. Perhaps we do not need to reinvent the wheel every time… we can build on what already exists and enhance it.
