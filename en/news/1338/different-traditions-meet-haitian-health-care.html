Different traditions meet in Haitian health care

Different traditions meet in Haitian health care

April 24, 2013
By
Nara Meli

Where do you go if you get sick? Do you call your doctor? Does someone drive you to the emergency room? Is it far? Can you afford it? Does the hospital have the medicine that you need?

What would your life look like if you had limited access to health services?

If you were Haitian, in all likelihood, you would call on the houngan or mambo (male/female voodoo priest), and he/she would perform some “spiritual power” to cure you – 90% of the population, rich or poor, practices Voodoo and believes in its healing powers. Many will go to their priest before seeing a doctor. Failing the voodoo remedy, if you live around Port-au-Prince, you would climb aboard a tap-tap – a local bus – and head over to the Hôpital de l’Université d’Etat, or Hôpital Général, the main public hospital.  

The General Hospital was severely affected by the 2010 earthquake; several wings collapsed, including the nursing school. Even now, over three years later, it is overwhelmed, and cannot provide services to all who need them. One of the reasons is the lack of funding: According to the World Health Organization, the Government spends about $76 per capita on health care (2010 constant dollars), around 1.5-2% of GDP in 2010.

Low spending on health means low quality and limited access to healthcare. According to the Pan American Health Organization, only 25 percent  of births in Haiti are attended by a skilled health professional, and about half of the population has no access to health services at all (PAHO 2007).

And limited access to health care means a higher chance of suffering or dying from treatable diseases. One indicator is mortality of children under five. Despite a falling trend, Haiti’s rate is still much higher than those in Cuba and the DR. The spike seen in the chart below is due to the earthquake, which caused an estimated doubling in the infant mortality rate.

So is there room for the private sector to intervene?

 

The answer is yes, there is a lot room for the private sector to be actively engaged in health care for low-income people – the sector just hasn’t yet been widely recognized as an area with investment potential. Nonetheless, the private sector is already intervening to fill the gaps in the provision of health care. The chart below shows  the % of GDP that is spent on health from the public and private sectors, indicating that the private sector spends over twice as much as the public sector on health.

                                                                     

 

Expenditure on Health, as % of GDP, 1995-2010 
(Source: World Bank Development Indicators) 
 
This is where DASH adds value. DASH is a 30-year-old health care provider that caters to informal and formal workers in and around Port-au-Prince, and has plans to expand to other major cities in Haiti. DASH offers DASHProcare, an HMO-type plan where individuals receive information on family-planning, HIV/AIDS, nutrition and immunizations.    
 
The MIF is partnering with DASH to design a pilot for providing pre-paid basic healthcare services around Port-au-Prince. The model works with a health card called Carte Avantage Sante. People can sign up for a six-month period, and gain access to a basic health package that would include exams, labs and medications. Patients can expand their plan to include hospitalization and surgery. 
 
The pilot is also geared toward improving the quality of care provided, and incorporates indicators to measure this throughout the initiative. Yet another challenge in Haiti is the lack of information available to the low-income population about health care practices and services available, so part of the initiative will fund educational and awareness programs, including outreach and customer service at the various DASH locations. Asked if he had any competition in the private health care sector, Dr. Laroche, who heads DASH exclaimed, with a smile: “The voodoo priest is our biggest adversary!”   (As a side note, this MIF blogger did meet a voodoo priest on her most recent visit to Haiti. However, she did not receive any health treatments.)
