MIF launches project to revive cocoa value chain in Haiti

MIF launches project to revive cocoa value chain in Haiti

November 15, 2013
By
Nara Meli

On October 5, the MIF launched the project “Creating Alliances in Cocoa for Improved Access and Organization in Haiti” in Dame Marie, in the Grand’ Anse department. The event, which gathered over 200 farmers from most of the cities of the Department of Grand’ Anse, represents the first step in organizing the cocoa value chain in to improve access to market.  With the activities of the project scheduled to begin in January 2014, the enthusiasm of the participants suggests that the expectations are high, the synergy guaranteed, and the results promising. 

A Value-Chain Project

The project, a 3-year, $5 million grant, has been in preparation since early this summer, and tackles some of the toughest challenges in the cocoa market: low production volumes, poor quality due to inadequate post-harvest processing, and fragile farmer cooperatives.  It directly targets 7,000 small farmers and cooperatives, – 4,000 farmers and six cooperatives in Grand’Anse and 3,000 farmers and one federation of cooperatives in the North department – strengthening their link to the value chain. The project is focusing on the two main cocoa production zones in the country (Grand’Anse and the North departments), and supports nearly 35% of all Haitian cocoa producers.

The project has three main components. First is improving cocoa farms’ productivity and natural resource management for the healthy regeneration of farmland. The project will develop a new cocoa agroforestry system will be developed based on organic practices, with the introduction of high-yielding plants. Furthermore, extension agents will be trained on pest management, grafting techniques and soil conservation, and a genetic mapping of local cocoa varieties will be conducted, with the establishment of “clonal gardens”.  

The second component is to add value through upgrading products and processes. Here, the focus is on improving processing techniques: reaping, fermentation, drying and cocoa transformation for better efficiency and bean quality. Activities include developing business plans and training for cooperatives to gradually transition from bulk cocoa to processed cocoa (higher value), capacity building in commercialization of processed cocoa and improvements in infrastructure for fermentation and drying. 

Third, the project will work on strengthening the capacity of cooperatives for long-term growth, in particular in enterprise management, marketing and commercialization, and good governance. 

The potential for national impact

Cocoa is an important source of primary income generation for the poor in Haiti. The sector consists of approximately 20,000 small producers, at or below the poverty line. The typical cocoa producer is 53 years old and lives in a basic housing structure, without access to electricity or running water, and households average four and eight children. 

The projected goal is to increase the incomes of small cocoa farmers by 25%, using the improved practices, based on the successful transition from unfermented cocoa to higher value fermented cocoa, higher yields per acre and better cocoa quality. Furthermore, cooperatives exporting fermented cocoa are expected to receive a price premium of 20%. With a more efficient supply chain and improved negotiating power, producers are expected to receive a 70% and 60% share of the world price for fermented and unfermented cocoa respectively. The project is also expected to create 250 new jobs in regeneration (temporary), grafting, and extension services. 

Some of the expected results include: a) 4,000 producers trained in sustainable cocoa agroforestry systems through farmer field schools; b) 16 clonal gardens with high yielding genetic material established; c) 2,500 hectares under improved management practices; d) 180 extension officers trained in farm management and grafting; and e) 13 cooperatives with an established member database and basic traceability system.

 
Sustainability
 

Sustainability is based on the ability of cooperative enterprises to successfully export greater volumes of cocoa through long-term commercial relationships with buyers and local exporters. Products and services developed by the project such as nurseries and grafting services will be embedded in the cooperatives as income- generating activities in order to ensure that they are market-driven and can be sustained by the cooperatives’ business model after the project ends. 

It couldn’t be done without…strong partners. MIF is collaborating with Root Capital, the Ministry of Agriculture, Natural Resources and Rural Development (MARNDR), Catholic Relief Services in Grand’Anse, and Lutheran World Relief in the North Department, channeling these organizations’ extensive experience in agro-enterprise projects in Haiti. This cross-country alliance will promote learning and collaboration between cooperatives, making the project a platform for producers and other cocoa sector stakeholders. 

In time, the project also therefore seeks to create a National Cocoa Alliance, which will help strengthen market connections, address challenges in the value chain and create an environment of knowledge-sharing and market development.
