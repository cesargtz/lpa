Two Steps Forward: Leopard Haiti Fund “spots” its first investment opportunity / Committee forms to coordinate external aid to Haiti

Two Steps Forward: Leopard Haiti Fund “spots” its first investment opportunity / Committee forms to coordinate external aid to Haiti

May 14, 2013
By
Nara Meli

If you read any report on Haiti or participate in any group discussion about the country these days, you will frequently hear these two words: Partnerships and coordination (for long-term development). In the case of Haiti, partnerships stands for improved private investment. Coordination, for more effective aid.

I have written about the balance between aid and investment in previous posts, and the discussion has moved forward in the past few months. While the Martelly administration had made it clear it wanted to tip this balance toward more business investments (particularly bringing larger foreign companies to Haiti such as SAE-A, Heineken, Marriott and Best Western), it has also advanced another message: the need to better coordinate international aid. The impetus for this is of course, the earthquake of 2010, which prompted an unprecedented and largely unchecked torrent of relief funds from the international community. Many critical stories were told about the consequences of this situation.

Incidentally, two events occurred in the past three days providing a good indication that things are moving in the right direction.

On the investment side, the Leopard Haiti Fund (LHF) formally announced its first equity investment in dloHaiti – which stands for water of Haiti, “dlo” meaning water in Kreyol – a business that provides affordable drinking water to underserved communities. The LHF, a private equity/venture capital fund, was launched in July 2012 with an initial closing of $20 million through the partnership and contributions of the World Bank’s IFC, the Netherlands Development Finance Company (FMO), and the MIF. The purpose of the fund is to provide risk capital (through quasi-equity and equity instruments) to up to ten Haitian SMEs, including start-ups.  DloHaiti, a finalist in Imagine H2O’s annual international competition for the world’s most promising start-ups, joins a growing cohort of local businesses on the fast-track to development. It is. Says Guy Balan, LHF Associate Partner in Port-au-Prince: “We believe dloHaiti brings world-class entrepreneurs, technology and know-how to address some hard, persistent problems in providing basic services in Haiti. We like the market-driven model and would like to see this type of investment expanded in Haiti and eventually in other parts of the world.” For more on LHF’s first investment, see its press release.

On the aid side, Friday, May 10 was the first meeting of the new Committee on Coordination of External Aid for the Development of Haiti (CAED), chaired by Prime Minister Laurent Lamothe, which replaces the Interim Commission of the Reconstruction of Haiti (ICRH). The CAED’s 32 members – 16 Haitians, 16 foreigners – include international organizations, South-South partners, donors, the Haitian government and other stakeholders in the development of Haiti. The principles governing the CAED are similar to the ones developed by the successful and widely recognized Agency for the Rehabilitation and Reconstruction of Aceh and Nias (BRR), following the Indian Ocean tsunami in 2004: “to coordinate the ongoing reconstruction efforts, and to match donor funds with specific community needs […] through a process that is rigorous, sensitive to local concerns and priorities, and well-monitored.”(Brookings, 2008). The principal aim of the two entities is simple: empower the receiving country to manage reconstruction on its own terms.

Looks like the week is off to a good start.
