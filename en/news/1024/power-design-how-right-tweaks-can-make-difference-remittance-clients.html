The power of design: how the right tweaks can make a difference for remittance clients

The power of design: how the right tweaks can make a difference for remittance clients

November 01, 2013
By
Rebecca Rouse

You may think you’ve seen it all, but you’ve never seen anything like Living Wallet. This product concept from a team of Japanese designers is taking savings and control to a new level.    

The idea behind Living Wallet is that even though it’s difficult to save, a well-designed product can help you maintain your savings goals when temptation calls. The wallet is linked to a phone app that tracks your bank account balance. When your balance dips and you reach for your wallet to purchase something, the wallet literally runs away from you and evades your hand. If you’re quick enough to catch it, it begins to scream and cry for help. Finally, if all else fails, the wallet places a phone call to your mother, who will hopefully guilt you into keeping that money in your savings account. As the website notes, it’s always hard to go against your parents. 

Sadly, Living Wallet isn’t on the market yet, but even if smart wallets don’t end up being the next big thing in savings promotion, the designers make an interesting point about the ways that product design can work to improve client outcomes. 

The importance of smart product design and control over savings was one of the main topics discussed during the Remittances and Financial Inclusion workshop at Foromic in Guadalajara. For transnational households, there can be radical differences between the financial goals of the remittance sender and those of the recipient. At Foromic, economist Diego Aycinena presented the results of his study with Nava Ashraf, Claudia Martinez, and Dean Yang about savings product design for remittance clients. In this study, Salvadoran migrants in the United States were randomly offered one of three savings accounts, each providing them with a different level of control over the funds. The first account gave access only to the remittance recipients in El Salvador, the second account was a joint account shared by both the recipient and the sender, and the third account gave exclusive control to the sender. After six months, migrants with exclusive control over a savings account had accumulated an average of US$211.19 in savings, compared to an average US$64.21 in savings for in joint accounts. Accounts owned solely by the remittance recipients displayed an even lower average savings balance of US$41.76.

The results of this study were corroborated by the experience of Banco Familiar in Paraguay. Diego Balanovsky, Financial Manager at Banco Familiar, explained how his bank has experimented with accounts for both remittance recipients and senders, finding that recipients demand more transactionality from their accounts, while senders use them primarily for savings and making online payments on behalf of their families.  Nearly 90% of remittance senders with savings accounts at Banco Familiar maintain a balance, compared to 60% of remittance recipients. Senders also maintained slightly higher average balances: US$869 vs. US$692. Banco Familiar was also surprised to learn that 70% of deposits made by remittance recipients were not drawn from remittances, but rather reflected households’ multiple sources of income. 

Aycinena and Balanovsky both stressed the importance of first understanding the needs and preferences of your clients, and then creating custom products that are designed to work for them.  For example, would Living Wallet be a good fit for a client who wants a tool to manage day-to-day transactions? But for someone who is looking for a tool with which to accumulate savings, the innovations that Living Wallet offers could potentially make an important difference in their financial outcomes.
