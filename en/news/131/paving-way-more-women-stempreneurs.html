Paving the way for more Women STEMpreneurs

Paving the way for more Women STEMpreneurs

July 21, 2016
By
Susana Garcia-Robles

Another year…another WeXchange event approaching!

As I reflect on what we have accomplished since we started WeXchange back in 2012–months of consultations with the “wisdom of the crowd”, brainstorming on ideas, the first events in Miami–to what we have accomplished today: annual gatherings that have inspired other initiatives in many countries in Latin America and the Caribbean, the proclamation of December 12th as the day of the LAC Woman Entrepreneur in partnership with NXTPLabs, the creation of a vibrant Facebook community that keeps posting and making us aware of the progress made by entrepreneurs from the region; I see clear progress in the region for women’s economic empowerment.

Last year, as we took WeXchange from Miami to Mexico, we decided to shape the event using a clear inspirational motto: “Fearless Facing Failure” or, in Spanish, “Sin Miedo a Soñar, Sin Miedo a Fallar”. We wanted to address head-on our fear of failure, so entrenched in our Latin American culture, and so opposite to the entrepreneurial culture seen in developed economies where innovation, entrepreneurship and VC flourish.

This year, we decided to take WeXchange to a country that is taking a huge step in favor of innovation and entrepreneurship: Argentina. The new government recently approved in Congress an SME Law that will facilitate the road for entrepreneurs. Argentina has always been a cradle for innovation and entrepreneurs but improvements at the regulatory and business environment were needed.  Selecting the city of Buenos Aires as our next hub signals a strong support for the new environment in place. This time, WeXchange opens a week of events under the IDB Demand Solutions initiative that will also happen in Buenos Aires.

When we thought of the theme for this year’s WeXchange, Women Entrepreneurs in STEM Careers (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math), STEMPreneurs, came to mind.  While there are a number of initiatives teaching girls to code, this is just the tip of the iceberg: we need to make sure that the ratio of graduates from STEM careers is more gender balanced, as these are the skills most useful to creating innovative and “sought after by investors” companies. A research by EY shows that men tend to venture more in STEM related startups (software, Internet, business services), while women tend to stay in the food and beverages, business services, and manufacturing. Let’s change this!

So, what defines a STEMpreneur? A STEMpreneur is someone who wants to solve things using technology, math, science, or engineering skills. Many combine their STEM education with the skills and passion for entrepreneurship, creating highly innovative companies that are very attractive to venture capitalists who see their potential. They create algorithms to come up with a financial solution to provide loans to underserved populations, apply data analytics to decision-making tools that can both improve agricultural yield and manage risk, or build nano-satellites that provide groundbreaking data streams with the ability to watch borders and vehicle movements, detect developing forest fires, follow the number of cars in a supermarket parking-lot to estimate revenue, identify the expanding wave of a tsunami... Yes! All these companies exist! 

So this is everyone’s territory, not only men, not only women; although traditionally has been dominated by men.
Luckily, there are great examples in LAC and in the world of women using these skills in their startups. And there are challenges as well…

When Victoria Alonsoperez started Chipsafer–a startup that tracks and early detects anomalies in cattle health, remotely and autonomously, and also helps the farmer prevent epidemics and combat cattle rustling–she was faced with the assumption that she was the marketing person at the company: young, pretty, well spoken. She’d laugh and say; “Nope, I’m the engineer who developed the technology!” The company started in Uruguay and moved to Brazil a few months ago.

Silvina Moschini, an Argentinean living in the US, is another example of an unstoppable entrepreneur using technology in her venture: she is the founder of Yandiki, an employment platform for the connected workforce generation. It provides immediate access to curated creative talent from around the world, in the cloud, on demand, transparently, with no hassle. She was selected as Endeavor Entrepreneur in 2016.

Gabriela Leon from Mexico, started her venture out of a health problem in her family, creating Gresmex, a company at the forefront of the nanotechnology research and development. She speaks of times when her brother, also a co-founder, needed to be the one initiating the meetings with potential investors, for them to be taken “seriously”.
Barbara Silva from Chile, is the CEO of BeSTinnovation and the Chile Director at SingularityU Global. BeSTinnovation is a platform that fosters innovation in the public and private sectors through the transference of knowledge, models and technologies, connecting Latin America with the global market.

Liza Velarde, while pursuing her undergraduate studies in international business three years ago, founded Semka together with a few classmates in biotech, industrial physics and electronic engineering. Semka developed a medical device that monitors cancer patients in a detailed way, providing them with personalized treatment. Her goal? To revolutionize the cancer therapy monitoring industry, and more immediately, to obtain Series A financing to accelerate the growth of her business.

Alice Brooks and Bettina Chen met and became friends in the master’s engineering program at Stanford. As two of the few women in the program, they compared stories on why they had pursued engineering. They were both inspired by their childhood toys. In the US, less than 15% of women enter college intending to major in a STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math) field. Alice and Bettina believe the gender gap starts young. Many girls’ toys lag behind in the development of spatial skills, hands-on problem solving skills, and confidence with technology. These are exactly the skills that Roominate, their company, aims to develop. Roominate’s unique blend of building blocks, circuits, designs, crafts, storytelling, and creativity teaches kids while they play. Using motor and light circuits, modular furniture, building pieces and walls, Roominate empowers kids to build endless amazing creations.

All these real women with real stories are bringing down the myth that studying–and venturing–in science is only for “the boys”. Focusing on STEM in WeXchange 2016 will help keep strengthening the case for many more women to feel inspired to pursue their dreams. Will you be part of the movement?
