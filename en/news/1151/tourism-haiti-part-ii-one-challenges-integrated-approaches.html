Tourism in Haiti (Part II): One of the Challenges of Integrated Approaches

Tourism in Haiti (Part II): One of the Challenges of Integrated Approaches

March 08, 2013
By
Nara Meli

Last week, I wrote about what tourism is and has been like in Haiti. Today, I want to tell you about what the MIF is doing on the topic.

In 2011, the MIF approved a three-year project aimed at positioning the North Department as Haiti’s prime tourism destination. The idea was to use an integrated approach to promote tourism based on the historical and cultural attractions unique to Haiti, involving stakeholders from the public and private sectors. The project would particularly organize and facilitate the management of the tourism destination with the participation of rural and urban micro, small and medium-sized enterprises, local and national government and other actors along the value chain. 

MIF financing of the project was $1.7 million, local counterpart provided $711,250, for a total of $2,431,416. The executing agency is Canadian organization CECI, the Centre for International Studies and Cooperation.

Principally, the project aims to establish an independent, public-private Destination Management Organization (DMO), which will organize and improve the provision of quality tourism products, and structuring private and public sector stakeholders around the development of the North Department Destination. Among other activities under this component, the business and operational plans will be developed, as well as a model for the management of the national park, including a permanent staff structure.

You can find more details on the project here.

So, where are we now?

The MIF signed an agreement for the project with the Ministry of Tourism in December 2011. So far, the project has accomplished three activities out of 28 planned, one of them being strengthening the Information Desk in Milot.

A few unplanned activities were also carried out, at the request of stakeholders, such as supporting crew training, supporting a social engineering campaign on the importance of exhibiting a clean city (Cap Haïtien), and participation to tourism events. But the main element of the project, the establishment of the DMO, has not yet been tackled.

Why? Well one reason is because there are a lot of stakeholders involved:

Association Touristique d’Haïti branche Nord (ATH-Nord), Chamber of Commerce, Industry and Professional Activities for Northern Haiti Ministry of Tourism ISPAN (Institut de Sauvegarde du Patrimoine National) Ministry of Transportation Tourinord (local organization) FONDTAH (Fondation pour le Développement du Tourisme Alternatif à Haïti) USAID Inter-American Development Bank Royal Caribbean Cruise Lines Global Heritage Fund US National Park Service World Monuments Fund UNESCO ESRI GAP Adventures Planeterra Solimar DIAGEO National Geographic Maps Representatives from main local governments in Cap Haïtien, Labadie, Milot, Dondon, Limonade and Fort Liberté.

And I don’t even think this is everyone…

When we talk about coordination and “integrated approach” in the development industry, we often underestimate the amount of partners that can be involved in a project – particularly if it is high-visibility and with high-potential. Case in point, the Martelly Administration has chosen the North as a development “pole” for Haiti. This means there is a flurry of big initiatives in close proximity, the most obvious examples being the Caracol Industrial Park ($300 million), the University Roi Christophe in Limonade ($30 million), the Feed the Future Partnership North Corridor ($75 million), the Infrastructure and Institutions Emergency Recovery project ($35 million) and the International airport of Cap Haïtien ($33 million). 

People working in development know they have to collaborate in order to have the most impact. Development for the majority does not happen in silos, but when lessons, knowledge and skills are widely shared, and when relevant interests are well-represented. While everyone can agree to coordination in principle, things get trickier in reality, mostly because – coordination takes time.

So, getting it right is a slow process, but what could be some ways to speed things up? I went around some of our team leaders here at the MIF and here are a few ideas.

  • Assign a point-person for each stakeholder group, and make sure the person can speak and make decisions on behalf of the whole group.
  • Set up a good communication channel with all the stakeholders, with regular exchanges that involve all the point-persons. For example: weekly calls or emails.
  • Agree on a common agenda aligned with results (can be monthly, quarterly, etc.).
  • Lay out an incentive structure and ensure accountability by assigning each point-person with responsibilities to move the agenda forward.

While these tips are no-nonsense, they become more complicated to apply when a large private sector firm is sitting at a table flanked by micro entrepreneurs, or if there are fifteen different partners involved. Or two parties can’t stand each other.

In many cases, successful projects must be negotiated as well as fought for.

“What needs to happen, as soon as possible is to set up the DMO, as a credible and representative organization, with an advisory council that takes into account all points of view. The DMO also has to show how it can serve the interests of the partners involved” Says a team member in Port-au-Prince. If all goes well, the core structure of the DMO will be set up in March. What follows is the establishment of the rest of the organization, including its staff and standard of operation.

I will keep you posted of the further developments!
