Early risk, big rewards: A sweet story of success for smallholders in northern Peru

Early risk, big rewards: A sweet story of success for smallholders in northern Peru

June 30, 2016
By
Laura Fernandez

Small-scale farmers in northern Peru are finding that success is sweet. They are exporting Fair Trade organic panela (unrefined brown sugar) to France, Italy, Germany, and Canada thanks to the entrepreneurial acumen, creativity, and continual support of their cooperative, CEPICAFE, and an impact investment from the IDB/MIF Social Entrepreneurship Program (SEP). While previously dependent on coffee as their principal income source, this investment helped hundreds of farmers to diversify into panela, and then cacao and other products, allowing them to improve their resilience to climate change, increase their incomes, and become direct suppliers of high quality organic and Fair Trade products.

The panela produced by these farmers used to be very low quality and was sold at extremely low prices to local factories as an input for “agua ardiente,” a local liquor. In 2001 and 2002, CEPICAFE worked with a small group of 70 member farmers  to experiment with production technologies and simple processing equipment to improve their products for export to niche markets in Europe.  During this pilot, CEPICAFE’s annual exports increased from two to nine tons of panela.  Inspired by the success of this experiment and the market opportunity,, CEPICAFE requested a loan from the IDB/MIF to expand its efforts and support more member farmers.

In 2003, when CEPICAFE had 1,650 members and only US$1 million in total assets, the IDB/MIF provided the cooperative with its first long-term loan of US$480,000, plus US$145,000 in technical assistance. The loan funds were used to expand CEPICAFE’s loans to members for coffee and panela production, improve its warehouse, and invest in panela processing infrastructure.  As buyers ordered more panela, CEPICAFE encouraged more of its members to plant and harvest organic sugar cane to complement their coffee crops.  They installed additional community processing stations in strategic areas to boost the supply, and improved their centralized packaging capacities and techniques. By the end of the project in 2008, CEPICAFE had surpassed all project production and export goals, and was exporting 500 tons of panela. The project’s final evaluation found that 400 panela farmers had increased their panela incomes fivefold. 

But this was just the tip of the iceberg of scale and sustainability. As demand for its panela grew, CEPICAFE continued to support its members in expanding production and improving quality, by investing in infrastructure by accessing local and international funders. Over time, CEPICAFE was transformed into the NORANDINO Agrarian Cooperative, and it transferred its credit operations to the NORANDINO Credit Union, of which it is a founding member. 

By the end of 2015, the NORANDINO Cooperative had 4,790 active members. During the same year, panela exports reached 1,260 tons, which is 140 times the volume exported when CEPICAFE received its loan from the IDB/MIF!  These panela purchases alone pumped more than US$830,000 in income to hundreds of small farmers in the region.   A small farmer with a single hectare of sugar cane can reap panela profits of US$2,700 in a year, which is more than 10 times the minimum wage in Peru. Now over 800 farmers are producing and exporting organic panela through NORANDINO.

In November 2015, NORANDINO inaugurated its new, modern panela packaging facility with cutting edge technologies and certifications, and a production capacity of 3,000 tons per year and 34 employees. The US$2 million plant (click for a video) was financed by public programs of the Peruvian Government (Agroideas), the European Union, and several international funders, together with NORANDINO’s own funds. NORANDINO has grown significantly and now has almost US$ 9 million in total assets. With this newly installed capacity, and a fast-growing distribution network in Europe that reaches over 4,000 supermarkets in France alone, NORANDINO estimates that the cooperative can potentially increase its exports by a factor of 10 in the coming years.

However, the flavor of success is not only sweet. With 2,575 tons of coffee exported to 10 countries in 2015, coffee is still NORANDINO’s main product.  Its small-scale farmers even became linked to a 30-second television commercial aired during Super Bowl 50 that was worth an estimated US$5 million, by having supplied coffee beans to Death Wish Coffee, the small roaster which promotes its product as “The World’s Strongest Coffee.”

IDB/MIF took a risk by supporting CEPICAFE at an early stage with its first long-term loan for panela production. CEPICAFE has repaid the entire IDB/MIF loan without delays. CEPICAFE, and then NORANDINO and its member farmers did the rest on their own. The returns on that investment have already been significant, and will continue to multiply in the future.
