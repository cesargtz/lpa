Investment in integrated health services model leads to better health for nearly 50,000 people

Investment in integrated health services model leads to better health for nearly 50,000 people

December 04, 2013
By
Guest

By Lara Diaconu, vice president of Health Services Fund, Global Partnerships and IDB investee

Nearly two years ago, the IDB-MIF Access to Basic Services unit approved a three-year grant to Global Partnerships (GP) to help improve “access to quality health services for low-income women in Latin America.” The grant’s specific objective was “to develop and expand sustainable business models providing health services through microfinance institutions in Nicaragua, Honduras, Ecuador and Haiti.” 

As a direct result of GP’s technical assistance and grant capital to four microfinance partners, almost 50,000 low-income women now have improved access to health services (data as of September 30, 2013). All four partners – including Pro Mujer International, Fundación ESPOIR (Ecuador), Fonkoze (Haiti), and COMIXMUL (Honduras) – had implemented some kind of health services package prior to GP’s engagement. However, we worked with them to shift to a new way of thinking about health services delivery, targeting the nexus of impact, scale and sustainability. This meant:

  • a new focus on chronic disease;
  • leveraging staff and infrastructure in new ways;
  • conceiving health as marketable products and services more than a social program; and 
  • new tools in place to monitor costs and revenues that contributes towards building sustainable solutions in healthcare. 

We are encouraged by the progress to date (latest progress report) and are thrilled to be learning alongside our investors such as the IDB-MIF.

How do our investments serve people who need it most?

Ana Joaquina Martinez is a member of Comixmul, and has been a community pharmacist in the El Rincon community in Honduras for the last 3 years. When Comixmul began including her in the training sessions to become a community pharmacist, she says she realized that she wanted to know more and more in order to help people better, and it opened up her mind to pursue more formal nursing studies. There are approximately 4-5,000 residents served by this pharmacy, and in September 2013 her pharmacy sold 5,300 lempiras ($265), one of the highest performing community pharmacies in the Comixmul network. She attributes her success to the low prices offered on the medicines themselves, but mostly to the special care she takes to help everyone who seeks her out. Clients come to her most frequently with high blood pressure, cough, temperature, fungal infections, and seeking vitamins such as iron and pre-natal vitamins. She wants to offer people a more formal “clinic”-type experience, and would like to have a proper clinic bed, scale, injection medicines and needles, and more robust medicines to respond to pneumonia and other respiratory issues.
