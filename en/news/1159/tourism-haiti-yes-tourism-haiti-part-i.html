Tourism in Haiti? Yes, tourism in Haiti! (Part I)

Tourism in Haiti? Yes, tourism in Haiti! (Part I)

February 28, 2013
By
Nara Meli

 

There has been a lot of buzz in the media lately about Haiti as a tourist destination – such as here and here. The push for this is coming mainly from the country, where the Martelly administration is embarking on a mission to raise the profile and branding of the country, and reinstate it as a unique destination in the region. More on this here. After all, Haiti used to be known as the “Pearl of the Caribbean.”

I was curious to learn more about the historical trends for tourism in Haiti, so I did a bit of research. This is what I found. Between 1995 and 2007, international tourism averaged roughly 150,000 arrivals a year.  In 2007, however, that number more than tripled, to 386,000, and then 387,220 in 2009. These figures do not include cruise passengers, of which the industry brings about 400,000 every year, mostly in the summer.

(Source: Ministry of Tourism, Haiti)

Don’t let the surge from 2007-2009 mislead you: 2007 is the year a new regulation was introduced in the way visitor arrivals are counted at the airports. The Caribbean Tourism Organization notes that as of that year, the total number of tourists started including Haitian residents abroad (the Diaspora), previously discounted. As such, the series is not comparable with data from previous years. The chart shows clearly, however, the marked effects on tourism of the hurricane season of 2008, with storms Fay, Gustav, Hanna and Ike, and the earthquake of 2010.

Leisure visitors to Haiti 2006-2011

Source: Ministry of Tourism

As you can see, the level of stated ‘leisure’ visits comprises roughly a third of all visits – an average of 2006-2011 gives 36.8% of all international visitors to Haiti, and 42% for 2011 alone. Those numbers also reflect the drops in 2008 and 2010. Not only that, but an estimated 72% of visitors came from the USA in 2010, and 76.7% in 2011, proving again the importance of the US as a partner in the development of this industry in Haiti.

Next, I did a comparison for international visitors between Haiti, Jamaica and Cuba for 2000-2011. This is a more sobering reality.

Number of international arrivals in Haiti, Jamaica and Cuba

(Source: for Haiti and Jamaica, Ministry of Tourism. For Cuba, World Bank.)

It is true that Haiti does not strike one as the first choice for a Caribbean-style (or not) type of vacation. Even this blogger will admit to expressing initial surprise when a couple of friends told her that they were heading to Port-au-Prince for their spring break. However, no one can deny that the island has a lot to offer. In the north of the country, near Milot is the Palais Sans-Souci, and nearby is the Citadelle Laferrière, both inscribed in the World Heritage List since 1982. Both structures are located in the National History Park, created in 1978, and stand as powerful symbols of liberty, as the first monuments built by black slaves who had gained their freedom. To the south of the country lies the colonial city of Jacmel, founded in 1698, itself placed on the World Heritage’s “Tentative List” since 2004. Not only that, but Haiti also has unique arts and crafts, festivals, 1,722 kmof coastline, and mountain ranges.

(Image Credits: MIF)

How is this for a change in your vision of Haiti?
