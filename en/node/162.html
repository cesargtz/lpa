As remittances to Latin America and the Caribbean continue to grow, how do we ensure they benefit households in the long run?

As remittances to Latin America and the Caribbean continue to grow, how do we ensure they benefit households in the long run?

November 30, 2016
By
Lukas Keller

Photo: Victor Casale

2015 was a good year for remittances to Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC). They grew to a new record high of US$65.7 billion, as shown in a new report published last Friday by the Center for Latin American Monetary Studies (CEMLA, for its abbreviation in Spanish). 2015 also brought confirmation that the growth of remittance flows to Latin America and the Caribbean (LAC) is sustained. The previous year, they had already reached an all-time high of US$62 billion, signaling a recovery from the global financial crisis.

The report, which was financed by the MIF, highlights that the growth in migrant transfers was particularly strong in the Caribbean and Central America, where remittance inflows grew by 7.6% and 6.6% respectively in 2015. As a general trend, remittance growth was more pronounced in countries and sub-regions where the United States represents the primary destination and host country of migrants due to a stronger economic recovery in that country vis-à-vis other major host nations, especially Spain.

As the growth of remittances outpaced that of the region’s economies overall, the relative importance of these transfers continued to increase in a majority of LAC countries in 2015.  The share of gross domestic product (GDP)  made up of remittance inflows has grown to more than 25% in Haiti, for example, and is more than one-sixth of economic output in El Salvador, Honduras, and Jamaica. Behind these figures stand many millions of households who depend on regular money transfers from abroad not only to cover basic needs such as food and clothing, but also to pay for health and education services. What is more, there is little indication that the significance of remittances for Latin America and the Caribbean will abate in the near future: CEMLA’s report suggests that 2016 will see another record high of these transfers, as remittances are projected to grow by nearly 6%, to a total of US$69.5 billion.

As the importance of remittances to the well-being of households across the region continues to grow, families also become increasingly vulnerable to economic downturns in the major migrant receiving countries. Arguably, the best way for remittance recipient households to cushion against the negative effects of such downturns is to use part of these transfers for long-term asset building, including financial assets such as long-term savings as well as non-financial ones such as investments in real estate or small businesses.

The MIF, through its Remittances and Savings program, has been supporting financial institutions across LAC to devise and expand access to savings products tailored to the needs of remittance clients, which in some of the poorest countries represent a fifth of the population. Our work with these institutions as well as original research on the financial behaviors of remittance senders and recipients has revealed promising avenues for improving the financial resilience of remittance clients. For example, our studies of LAC migrant communities in the United States, Spain and Chile (forthcoming) suggest that there are significant business opportunities in providing convenient ways for migrants to channel remittances into retirement savings accounts or to purchase property in their countries of origin. Technological and regulatory progress will make these business cases even more promising.

2015 brought good news about remittances. Let’s work together to ensure that the region benefits from these transfers in the years to come. More than ever, we are learning how to do so.
