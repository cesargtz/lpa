New technology for the Costa Rican coffee sector will be good for the environment and for productivity

New technology for the Costa Rican coffee sector will be good for the environment and for productivity

October 24, 2013
By
Claudia Moreno

 

The Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF), a member of the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) Group, recently approved a $2,240,000 project with Fundecooperacion (a Costa Rican NGO that supports socio-productive projects) that seeks to reduce the environmental damage caused in coffee production and boost the efficiency of small farmers and coffee processing plants in Costa Rica. In order to achieve this, the project will adopt new technologies and best production practices by coffee producers and processing plants.

In Costa Rica, coffee farming is a dynamic activity, and its magnitude is reflected in the fact that it generates 25% of agricultural greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and 9% of the country’s total emissions. In 2007, the government announced its climate change mitigation policy, in which it pledged to achieve carbon neutrality by 2021.

This project will contribute to this goal by helping to reduce greenhouse gas emissions generated throughout the coffee production cycle. Specifically, it will help reduce the impacts of fertilizers and coffee processing waste, and contribute to more sustainable use of land, water and natural resources that serve as fuel for production. Additionally, the project will seek to increase the profits of 3,000 coffee farmers living in rural areas of the country who depend on the crop as their main source of income. At the same time, the initiative will strengthen the operational processes of three coffee processing plants and assist coffee organizations in approaching markets and buyers interested in low-carbon products.

“This is a very innovative project that brings together MIF experiences in helping small farmers increase their productivity with the application of new technologies to reduce their GHG emissions,” said Carrie McKellogg, Chief of the MIF’s Access to Basic Services and Green Growth unit.

The project’s technologies will be tested by coffee farmers and processing plants. This “technology package” will include improvements in fertilizer application, the adoption of denser agroforestry systems, spraying systems to reduce gas emissions, use of gasification technology  as a substitute for firewood to fuel the coffee dryers, and climate adaptation activities including introduction of coffee varieties adapted to climate change, increased tree cover, and soil conservation practices.

“The initiative demonstrate’s the MIF laboratory role, testing innovative technologies for coffee cooperatives and small farmers, which when proven will contribute to the project’s systemic impact by informing national protocols on carbon neutrality for the coffee sector,” added McKellogg.

The project will receive the support of the World Resources Institute (WRI) as a strategic knowledge partner that has developed the
GHG Protocol Agricultural Guidance, as well as the GHG Protocol Policy and Action Standard. The latter will be validated through this operation before being launched as an international standard. The German Agency for International Cooperation (GIZ) will contribute counterpart funds, and provide support to obtain NAMA Facility funds for implementing the NAMA in the coffee sector.

In the long term, the project aims to define a new development model through which the Costa Rican coffee sector will establish new technologies that can be adopted throughout the country to mitigate GHG emissions. The Government of Costa Rica will define a Nationally Appropriate Mitigation Action (NAMA) —a climate mitigation model—that it will promote as a voluntary measure for reducing the coffee sector’s carbon footprint. This is the first NAMA to be developed in the world for an agricultural product, and it covers the process from planting to the drying of green coffee beans.


About the MIF 

The Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF), a member of the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) Group, is funded by 39 donors and supports private sector-led development benefitting low-income populations and the poor - their businesses, their farms, and their households. The aim is to give them the tools to boost their incomes: access to markets and the skills to compete in those markets, access to finance, and access to basic services, including green technology. A core MIF mission is to act as a development laboratory - experimenting, pioneering, and taking risks in order to build and support successful micro and SME business models. More information at www.fomin.org.

About Fundecooperacion

Fundecooperación is a Costa Rican nongovernmental organization that includes representation from five key sectors of Costa Rican society: NGOs, civil society, government (national and local), private sector, and academia. It provides technical assistance and has a lending program that finances green initiatives. It has managed national and international projects, and has experience with South-South cooperation  in sustainable development and climate change, among other topics. More information at http://fundecooperacion.org/
