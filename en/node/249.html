It's all about mergers in Jamaica and the Caribbean

It's all about mergers in Jamaica and the Caribbean

June 06, 2017
By
Elena Heredero Rodriguez

The NEO Alliance in Jamaica

A few weeks ago, I was in Jamaica to visit the NEO Jamaica alliance and to speak at the 3rd International Conference on Technical Vocational Education and Training (TVET) in the Caribbean about public-private partnerships. It is always nice to see progress firsthand and to meet with our partners after a year since the NEO alliance was launched. This visit was also timely given the major changes in the Ministry of Education of Jamaica with the merger of the Human Employment and Resource Training Trust, National Training Agency, known as HEART Trust NTA, the National Youth Service (NYS), and the Jamaican Lifelong Learning Foundation. Both HEART Trust NTA and NYS had been founding members of the NEO Jamaica alliance and key players in the youth employability ecosystem. HEART is the largest TVET in the country with a strong reputation among employers and youth alike. NYS, now the National Youth Division, provides personal and professional development services through training, work experience, entrepreneurship activities, mentorship and volunteer opportunities to youth ages 17-24 with a particular focus on females, rural youth and youth with disabilities.
 

The merger has slowed down implementation as the alliance needed to decide which centers were going to benefit from the NEO Quality System and most of the centers were from HEART Trust NTA. But it looks like the decision will be made soon, and some centers from the Ministry of Labour and Social Security and YUTE have already received orientation about the quality system and the NEO standards by our regional partner, the International Youth Foundation, and this critical part of the project will speed up in the summer. The goal is to monitor and evaluate quality services of 11 public and private providers, implement new practical tools, train teachers, counselors, job placement staff and impact 10,000 disadvantaged youth with better youth employability services.
 

The NEO Jamaica alliance has also been building some groundwork in the country to spur a debate about the youth employability ecosystem, its governance, the results metrics to measure progress, the employer demands, the challenges for unattached youth to gain decent employment, etc. The plan is to disseminate the findings through the Jamaican press -The Gleaner- and social media and to organize a public-private dialogue by the end of the year to raise more awareness and visibility. The first article about NEO Jamaica can be found here.
 

The 3rd TVET Conference in the Caribbean
 

This merger was certainly discussed at the 3rd TVET conference though the headline was more about a different type of merger - a “marriage” between TVET and formal education. Yes, this is what the Minister of Education, Youth and Information of Jamaica called it, and it really resonated among the more than 300 participants and TVET specialists from the Caribbean and around the world. In what sense? By infusing into the formal school system more experiential learning, learning by doing projects, fostering creativity, collaboration, communication and problem solving skills. The national curriculum K-12 has changed to make school a much more attractive learning place, help students acquire demand-driven skills and support teachers in this transition. It is an exciting development for the country as the official education statistics report that 31.6% of students drop out of school before completing secondary education qualifications and employers continue to rank soft skills such as communication, self-efficacy, and teamwork, as more important skills than academic ones.
 

This conference in the Caribbean is done every two years by the University of the West Indies and the organization committee is large and includes the Inter-American Development Bank. Apart from the marriage of TVET and formal education, I learned about the skills road map and action plan in The Bahamas, the new International Center for Technical and Vocational Education and Training (UNEVOC-UNESCO) in Trinidad and Tobago, the heated and amusing discussion about rebranding TVET in the Caribbean with the example of the grave diggers transformed into “soil removal technicians”, the importance of life skills for unattached youth, quality systems and international best practices from the European Union, etc. You can find many materials presented here.
