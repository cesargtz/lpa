Inspiring the private sector: from agricultural small farmers to rural entrepreneurs

Inspiring the private sector: from agricultural small farmers to rural entrepreneurs

April 22, 2016
By
Fernanda lopez

Small farmers in Latin America deal with knowledge and technology access barriers whenever they try to adapt their productive systems to climate change: irrigation, pest-resistant seed varieties, and best practices are sometimes unknown or out of reach of rural areas. In other cases, additional barriers are present, such as access to land and entry into high-value markets. For countries like Guatemala, these issues become particularly challenging: the country has 12% of its total agricultural area made of land holdings smaller than one hectare (the highest percentage in Latin America, according to the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization). In addition, 51.5% of the country’s young and predominantly female population (66.5% of the population is younger than 30 years old and 51% are women, according to Guatemala’s National Institute of Statistics) still lives in rural areas and is mostly dedicated to agriculture.

Since 2012, our MIF-funded project “Horticultural Financing in Guatemala for High Value Markets” has been piloting a model to help small farmers increase their productivity, comply with market standards, and secure access to global value chains for their fresh vegetables. Through the model,  600 farmers were trained and are currently applying climate-smart agricultural practices in 600 acres of land that are being managed as small businesses. In addition to market access, the model also generated an additional 30,000 temporary jobs in rural areas of Guatemala’s Central Highlands, providing finance and training through a platform of services readily available to both women and men farmers. Collectively, they have already exported 6 million pounds of fresh vegetables to mainstream wholesalers in North America.

From the MIF’s perspective, the key pillars of this model worth highlighting are:
 

  • Having a committed private sector local partner: Guatemalan partner Unispice had a committed senior management with a clear vision of creating a win-win solution focused on growing the business by securing and managing its supply chain while at the same time helping small rural farmers to get out of poverty in six months. Unispice provided basic training and technical assistance that allowed small farmers to implement agricultural production practices that responded to market requirements and addressed climate change challenges, such as extended dry seasons, pests ,and diseases. As a result, farmers were able to achieve international certifications, increase their productivity, and standardize their quality for international markets. Moreover, since farmers had access to a validated business model whose market access was guaranteed by Unispice, small producers were able to access finance.
     
  • Enabling demand-driven approaches to access high-value markets: Most small farmers in Latin America realize that they are experienced in agriculture, but lack specific knowledge, technology packages, and finance to produce using the standards required by more sophisticated markets. The project prioritized the provision of tailored training, technology, and finance, which was welcomed by the participating small farmers and surrounding communities. Nowadays they acknowledge the project’s contribution by recognizing themselves as more prepared to deal with climate variability.
     
  • Our interventions produce valuable intangible assets that create meaningful impact:  The technical assistance provided by Unispice had a powerful impact on the beneficiary farmers, who no longer define themselves as small farmers, but rather as business women and men. By receiving training and technical assistance from a counterpart directly related to the business, they were able to learn and understand how their field work has a direct impact on market access, final prices, purchase volumes, and profits. People who once were small farmers from poor communities in Northern Guatemala are now empowered rural entrepreneurs who share and understand the requirements needed in their productive processes to ensure quality and productivity, and are generating employment and creating a better life for themselves and their families.

 

We really hope that more private sector companies in Latin America get inspired by Unispice and start implementing climate-smart agriculture models that allow more small farmers to become successful rural entrepreneurs.
