Effective solutions for youth that don’t want to be NEET anymore.

Effective solutions for youth that don’t want to be NEET anymore.

August 09, 2017
By
Francisco Larra Bujalance

Alejandra is a Mexican young woman whom dropped out of secondary school when she was 16 years old due to her pregnancy. After a long period out from school and the labor market, she would like to work but she perceives that her situation neither allows her to enroll in a job training nor look for a job. Daniel decided to drop out when he was studying his last year of secondary school. He thought that school didn’t contribute anything to his life and he decided, in his own words, “to the monotony of doing nothing for living” because there was not hope in Uraba, his home region in Colombia.    

According to the Labor Markets and Social Security Information System of the Inter-American Development Bank, one out of 5 youth between 15 and 24 years of age are not in education, employment, or training and they are known as NEET youth. The unemployment and job informality are challenges that persist in the labor panorama for youth in Latin America and the Caribbean, despite of the good economic performance during the first decade of this century.

In this context, the Alliance NEO Mexico is leading the implementation of a comprehensive attention model for NEET youth in Nuevo Leon (NL) named Modules of Comprehensive Attention Services  for Youth (MÁS JOVEN for its acronyms in Spanish). MÁS JOVEN gathers stakeholders’ efforts with the Government of the State of Nuevo Leon and organizes the programs of the Secretary of Social Development (SDS), the Secretary of Economy and Labor (SET), the Secretary of Education (SE), the Institute of Job Training and Education (ICET), CONALEP NL (an upper secondary technical educational institution), with COPARMEX (business association), Alianza Educativa, Caritas de Monterrey and Servicios a la Juventud (ONG).

This intervention model consists in the accommodation of comprehensive youth attention centers where services are coordinated based on employability and job placement efforts. The goal is to include all youth, women and men, within the 4.0 industrial momentum and fulfill the more than 21,000 vacancies for technical and operational jobs that are annually needed in the productive sector in the State. This effort is based in five pillars:

1.      Coordinated approach. To resolve complex social problems, we can’t continue working in an isolated manner. But in Nuevo Leon the stakeholders are not inventing anything new, they are just starting to work in a different manner. This new way of working aims to polish the relationship among businesses, governments and civil society.  Working together will result in triggering new solutions, making processes more efficient, optimizing resources, generating innovation and increasing the capacity to answer to the new challenges.

2.      Motivating youth. We must reach out to youth that are completely detached from the training and employment systems and meet them where they are. The SDS in partnership with Alianza Educativa, Caritas de Monterrey and Servicios a la Juventud share their knowledge about youth problems and their outreach experiences to youth and their families. They perform a collective effort working with youth in the areas where they usually congregate, serving as liaison with the employment and job training public services. During the outreach component, it is crucial to work on the believes that youth and their families may have, showing them an inspiring vision of future and helping them to realize that a job is more accessible than they think.

3.      Improving employability. Under this pillar, the SET and SE, together with the training institutions such as CONALEP NL and ICET, coordinate their training services aligning the job training courses with the local productive sectors necessities. They implement effective teaching methodologies for youth, include technical skills, life skills, internships, and make scholarships more accessible. Together, they have developed career guidance services to provide relevant information adapted to youth in order to help them identify their interests, skills and resources to make effective decisions about their education, labor experiences, and developing a life plan. 

4.      Obtaining a formal employment.  Increasing the youth competencies and job qualifications shouldn’t be enough.  In Nuevo León, the private sector now more than ever is in coordination with the job training providers and with the SET to improve the job placement services, update the employment demands information and vacancies database.  The goal is to assure internships and a first successful job experience for the most vulnerable youth. The youth job placement and keeping them in the labor market is described as indicator of success, therefore all stakeholders are responsible to comply with this indicator.

5.      Supporting and following up. We cannot allow neither gaps nor short-circuit in the services offered to youth. To provide the youth with support along the intervention is crucial for the equation:  A youth = An employment.  Lastly, a monitoring system is needed to be committed to the ongoing improvement process, once the youth are placed in jobs, by compiling the feedback from the youth, the job services providers, and the employers.

After a six-month-pilot-phase, the first results were analyzed and more than 280 detached youth, who were away from the job training and employment market, were placed in jobs. And, there is still much work to do! Latin-American and the Caribbean cannot afford the risk of losing human capital if it wants to improve the productivity and keep the long term economic growth. Our youth can’t continue dropping out of school without the indispensable skills of finding a job.  This scenario will probably result in a worst employment perspective, lower salaries, a possible at-risk behavior and social exclusion.   

Becquer, the poet, stated: “In the dark corner of the hall, perhaps forgotten by her mistress, silent and dusty, laid the harp. […] Alas!  I thought; how often does genius likewise sleep in the deepest of the heart, and a voice, like Lazarus, awaits to be told: Rise and walk!”. There are 148 million youth in the region that, like Alejandra and Daniel, are already speaking about goals and self-improvement. They are the I-want- and-I-can youth who have been able to obtain a job, and who encourage us with illusion to keep working together to generate employment opportunities to allow young people to develop their full potential and fulfill their dreams.

 

*NEO  is an initiative were more than 140 business, government and civil society of 12 countries in Latin America and the Caribbean have committed to work together to find solutions to complex social problems such as youth employment.
