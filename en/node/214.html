Tigo Money – A lesson in mobile financial services, one partnership at the time

Tigo Money – A lesson in mobile financial services, one partnership at the time

August 01, 2017
By
Fermín Vivanco

When the Luxemburg-based telecommunications provider Millicom started its service Tigo Cash in Paraguay nine years ago, few expected this to be the starting point of Latin America’s most successful mobile money story.

Fast forward to 2017 and the service, functionally adapted and renamed to Tigo Money, is used by more than 2.2 million Paraguayans – or about half of the country’s adults – to conduct different kinds of basic financial transactions, such as sending money and taking out small business and consumer loans. Tigo Money’s foothold in the Paraguayan payments market is such that the equivalent of 10% of the total low-value transfer volume of Paraguay’s payment system is channeled through its mobile wallets, which are serviced by more than 4,000 agents spread out over 97% of the national territory. As in Kenya and other countries, mobile money transactions in Paraguay usually occur at small stores where users either top up or cash out wallet balances. A widely distributed agent network is thus the key to the function of the service.

Observers have pointed to various unique factors for Tigo Money’s success, including an underdeveloped traditional financial sector in Paraguay that, together with a high penetration of mobile phones, created a fertile environment for their SMS-based money services.

Another success factor has arguably been Millicom’s pursuit of strategic partnerships with both private and public actors to continuously improve its products and commercial strategy over the years. Now, a report (in Spanish) by the MIF and the IDB’s Connectivity, Markets and Finance (CMF) Division provides a unique account of the development of two of these partnerships and the key factors that brought them to fruition. The two alliances had distinct objectives, respectively: On the one hand, to pilot the possibility of offering loans through Tigo Money’s platform, and on the other, to tackle liquidity problems among its agents, a problem that often haunts mobile finance providers that rely on its own agents for cash deposits and payouts.

With regards to Tigo Money’s incursion into micro loans, starting in early 2012 Tigo partnered with two established Paraguayan Banks - Visión Banco and Banco Familiar - to develop Credimóvil, a highly successful loan product that allowed Tigo Money users to apply for micro loans of between $US100 and $US400 using only their national ID. Specifically, to apply for a loan, users would simply send their ID number via SMS to Tigo, which in turn ran an automatic check of the users’ creditworthiness using transactional and other available data. Individual credit decisions would be made in an average of 20 minutes, much faster than through conventional methods. As the IDB/MIF report highlights, the key to the success of Credimóvil lay in the commitment to the product, especially on the part of Banco Familiar, which saw Credimóvil as an excellent way to expand the reach of its loan operations beyond the 50 districts (19% of all the districts in Paraguay) it had branches in at the time.

To address illiquidity problems among Tigo Money agents, the company in December 2011 joined forces with Visión Banco, which set up a special type of checking account for Tigo agents. The account granted agents an overdraft option of $US1,000, to be repaid in 25 days, as well as immediate access to credit to quickly address cash shortages that agents might face in their daily operations. The highly successful model allowed for an effective synergy between Tigo Money and Visión Banco in that it enabled Tigo to circumvent the option of financing its access points through a more risky “prepaid” option, and Visión Banco to find a new client base for their loan products. The key to success for this partnership can be seen in the significant presence of Visión Banco across Paraguay, bringing the bank as close as possible to Tigo Money’s service points.

The ascent of Tigo Money to one of Latin America’s most successful mobile money service providers can be told from many perspectives. Until now little has been written about the details of the strategic partnerships that helped it become such an important player in Paraguay and an increasing number of additional markets in the region. In that sense, our new report provides some valuable lessons on how to make digital financial inclusion happen.
 

Note: Millicom’s partnerships with Visión Banco and Banco Familiar were supported by the Technologies for Financial Inclusion (Tec-In) Program of the MIF and CMF/IDB. Under the framework of the program, from 2010 to 2015 Millicom and the MIF/IDB implemented a technical cooperation project entitled “Mobile Financial Services Provision under a Last Mile Business Model”. The objective of this project was to promote the use of Tigo’s mobile wallet as a way to increase financial inclusion in Paraguay.
