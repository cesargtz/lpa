Ecatepec, Mexico: Here you can save for retirement

Ecatepec, Mexico: Here you can save for retirement

October 27, 2015
By
Fermín Vivanco

Photo by Richard Cawood / CC BY-NC 2.0

Low-income, self-employed workers in Latin America and the Caribbean have been out of the spotlight of pension funds and the institutions that promote retirement savings. But now there are new initiatives that aim to change this. A story to analyze at Foromic in Santiago, Chile.

The houses in Ecatepec, on the edge of Mexico City, look identical from the outside. The residents who live in them also have something in common: no one, or almost no one, is making regular contributions to their pension fund, or their AFORES as they are called in Mexico. “What is an AFORE?” asks Tania Alvarado, a 22-year old mother of four, as she folds clothing in her store. The 1.7 million people who live in Ecatepec have not been a focus of the majority of the AFORES in Mexico. Nor have 36 million of the 53 million Mexicans who are registered with AFORES, who aren't making contributions to their account.

Although today AFORES promote the participation of independent, low-income workers, the system was originally designed thinking of salaried employees. Tania, like many of Ecatepec's working age residents, is self-employed, and doesn't have a fixed wage. For her, it is not possible to automatically deduct her pension contribution from the payroll as can be done for salaried employees. Furthermore, having a low and unstable income complicates her ability to contribute regular amounts, even if she is able to make voluntary contributions during peak sales periods.

Unless she starts to save, reaching retirement age will not mean stopping working and collecting a pension. She will not have enough saved. And at that age income generation will become increasingly difficult. Obtaining formal employment will be almost impossible as will be obtaining financing for her business. For 130 million Latin Americans who work and don't save for retirement, old age will be a time of increased economic vulnerability.

Ensuring that people like Tania can save for retirement is a priority for the National Commission of the Retirement Savings System (CONSAR) in Mexico and so it is launching several initiatives. One such initiative is to enable voluntary contributions to the AFORES to be made at convenience stores, starting with 7-Elevens; very small contributions, without commission, in one's own neighborhood. Another is an online portal to automate voluntary contributions that can be deducted from a debit card or bank account, products that reach 30 million Mexicans.

"I very much want to have voluntary savings, that way if something happens to me I have financial support for my children," says Tania. When I explain the new options to facilitate saving, she listens with interest, but hearing the challenges she faces just to make ends meet, I realize that besides facilitating channels to promote savings it will be necessary to find a way to raise awareness and even provide training on the importance of savings, and to develop tools so that household budgets can be managed in such a way that leaves space for retirement savings.

 

 

To learn more about what CONSAR in Mexico, Colpensiones in Colombia, the IDB, Grupo Sura, Ideas 42, Metlife Foundation and other private and public actors are doing to promote voluntary retirement savings in Latin America and the Caribbean, don't miss the Foromic panel "Retirement Savings: A new opportunity for financial inclusion", which will be livestreamed at www.foromic.org.
