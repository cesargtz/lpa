Must social enterprises grow to have a large-scale impact on society?

Must social enterprises grow to have a large-scale impact on society?

July 30, 2015
By
Nobuyuki Otsuka

By Nobuyuki Otsuka and Claudio Cortellese

There is a proliferation worldwide of “social businesses” that generate goods and services that benefit society as well as financial profits. Likewise, we’re seeing more social business accelerators—specialized intermediaries such as Socialab, Njambre and UnLtd Spain that provide social entrepreneurs with business tools and approaches to accelerate the growth of an idea that is assumed to create a positive impact. Development professionals who work on building up the private sector are curious about what accelerating a social business means. But perhaps a more fundamental question should be: is it necessary to accelerate a social business in the first place? 

Every day many social businesses are born, and just like in the case of any other commercial business, investors focus on the growth of the enterprise. However, we have not discussed in depth what expanding a social business should look like to achieve the ultimate goal of all social entrepreneurs—“systemic” social impact that affects the broader society. An example from around the world in Japan shows that obtaining broad social impacts may not depend on the growth of a social enterprise. 

Japan’s Carepro 

Health check-ups in Japan used to take place only during office hours. Then entrepreneur Takashi Kawazoe met a man who had had his leg amputated because he ignored the symptoms of diabetes. Kawazoe’s response was to start Carepro, a service that allowed people to self-administer simple blood tests in public places on weekdays and weekends for a nominal fee of $5, providing instant results on indicators such as blood-sugar level, cholesterol, and bone density. 

Kawazoe started the service in 2007 on street corners and expanded it to locations including famous department stores, and more recently, busy railway stations in Tokyo. In 2013, he became one of only three Japanese members of Ashoka, the largest network of social entrepreneurs worldwide. However, he faced one serious obstacle to scaling up his business further: self-drawn blood tests taking place outside of authorized medical facilities could be deemed extralegal, in the absence of regulations that explicitly permitted the tests. Kawazoe lobbied and negotiated with the Japanese government to create such regulations, in addition to fighting a strong backlash by the country’s medical associations. 

After his persistent advocacy for 7 years, the government issued explicit guidelines in 2014, clearing his business of any potential legal trouble. Since then, Kawazoe has become a nationally recognized social entrepreneur in Japan, appearing in newspapers and on TV programs, and has received awards from the World Economic Forum and other organizations. 

Paradoxically, the new regulations created another hurdle for Kawazoe: overnight, more than 1,000 new competitors sprang up when pharmacies replicated the quick medical self-check service in stores all over the country. Moreover, about 10,000 stores operating throughout Japan could become formidable competitors in the near future, as they would need only minor investments to add this new business, in addition to providing advice about over-the counter medicines and supplementary nutrients. In response, Kawazoe has formed a partnership with the giant Japan Railways Group—the country's largest railroad conglomerate—and developed a plan to operate in 50 railroad stations for the next 5 years. 

Impact on society vs. growth 

Has Kawazoe revolutionized the field of medical care in Japan? Emphatically yes. He almost single-handedly changed general health awareness in the entire country, while the model he developed was instantly proven as the best way to do it. Yet his company grew modestly—from 1 to 70 employees in the last 7 years. As this example shows, a tough, courageous and brilliant social innovator can have a widespread impact on his society without his enterprise experiencing major growth. 

For social business to win support from “impact investors” and international development organizations, we need to urgently address several key questions, including how to define elusive “social” returns, and how to exit from an investment in a social enterprise. But first, we should ask ourselves whether it is ever necessary to accelerate a social business. In the social sector, narrowly focusing on the growth of an individual enterprise may not be the most effective way to affect society as a whole—which  constitutes the core of social innovation. Rather, development organizations and investors that seek a measurable social impact, together with a financial return, should aim for a private initiative that is so influential that it ends up shaping new government policies.
