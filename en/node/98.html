By walking together youth can go farther

By walking together youth can go farther

August 11, 2016
By
Francisco Larra Bujalance

It was in the month of August, exactly 20 years ago, when I started the task to provide vocational counseling to more than 75 senior high school students from a public school in a marginalized neighborhood from Guatemala City. It was my first professional experience as a vocational counselor. While I was trying to provide them with professional profiles information and career paths that may be useful for them, I knew that very few would achieve enrollment in a public university in the next few months: the majority of them wouldn’t graduate and, in general, they would have to face the labor market directly without enough skills and low salaries.

While recently the UN reminded us, within the International Youth Skills Day context, that youth are almost 3 times more likely than adults to be unemployed and to faces inequalities in the labor market, memories from my first days as a vocational counselor, come back to my mind. 

Still, youth keep their convictions and they don’t lose hope. For example, Jennifer, a student of Vocational Technical Education in Industrial Electromechanics, whom is only 15 years-old, she wants “for women to realize that they can be good at electromechanics as men”. The dreams are many, but the existing educational and training systems do not respond to the learning needs of youth, who cannot find employment, and at the same time, the companies in the region face difficulties to find qualified workers, especially for operational and technical positions. 

Cristian, 17 years-old, and Mariel, 19 years-old, state that the internships from their trainings connected directly with their first job experiences. But they are the exceptions to the rule. According to The McKinsey Center for Government (MCG), employers, educators and youth live in parallel universes, because they work in isolated silos and they have different understandings about the youth employment challenge. While educators believe that graduates are ready for the labor world, the youth and employers all think the contrary. Thus, the interventions to improve the school-to-work transition require structural improvements. 

Currently, many agencies and institutions give recommendations to close the gap between the youth skills and labor market demands. Among them: a) developing labor competencies through a shared vision and co-responsibility of different actors; b) motivating communication mechanisms to give answer to the rapid technological changes; c) implementing regulations that allow uniformity in contents, quality criteria and relevancy among different educational options; and d) motivating integrated information and monitoring systems. However, there are very few experiences in Latin America and the Caribbean that experiment and put into practice these recommendations. NEO is one of them. 

NEO is a pioneering initiative that joints efforts from businesses, governments and civil society, working in education- training, labor market and youth areas, to tackle these problems in a structural manner. Sharing what is working or not and measuring the same indicators closes the gaps of coordination, dialogue, knowledge and efforts, previously isolated, to build a consensus about youth employability programs and public policies that work and are highly valuable. 

Among NEO allies, CEMEX has visualized the youth employability not only as social responsibility but as strategical part of community development and their business. Martha Herrera, director of CEMEX Corporate Social Responsibility, states: “The youth employability issue is everyone’s responsibility. NEO helps us to develop a sustainable model to achieve systemic changes in the employability in Mexico through the opportunity to work in alliance”. But working in partnership and in an articulated coordinated manner is not easy. It is always a great temptation to work in isolated and uncoordinated agendas, increasing expenses, efforts and reducing the common good. 

Latin America and the Caribbean is home to approximately 148 million youth, ages 15 to 29. However, according to researches, 1 in 5 youth do not study or work; 1 in 3 is at risk; the youth unemployment rate is as much as 3 times higher than the adult rate; and 6 in 10 jobs held by youth are informal. But something is changing. Angel, 20 years-old, had a second opportunity and he was able to receive coordinated services such as career guidance, job training, internships, socio-labor support and labor intermediation that allowed him to get a job as a soldering technician, immediately upon graduation. 

An African proverb says: “Alone we advance quicker, but together we can go farther”. My main wish for this August 12, as we celebrate International Youth Day, is to be a witness of governments, productive-private sectors and civil society working together with determination to demolish the walls that stop millions of youth from reaching their dreams. 

* NEO is an initiative led by the IDB, through its Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF) and its Labor Markets Unit (LMK), the International Youth Foundation (IYF) and partners: Arcos Dorados, Caterpillar Foundation, CEMEX, Fondation Forge, Microsoft, SESI and Walmart.
