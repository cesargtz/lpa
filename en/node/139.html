Latin American and Caribbean migrants in Chile – better off in the neighborhood?

Latin American and Caribbean migrants in Chile – better off in the neighborhood?

December 13, 2016
By
Lukas Keller

To most people, Jean Beausejour is known for playing on the Chilean national football team, where his latest successes include winning this year’s Copa America. A lesser-known fact about this son of a Port-au-Prince-born father and a Mapuche mother is that that he is arguably the most famous representative of one of the fastest-growing immigrant communities in the country: the Haitian-Chileans.

When we think about migration in the region, what typically comes to mind are the South-North journeys of those in the pursuit of new opportunities in North America and Western Europe. Since the global financial crisis of 2008/2009 diminished the relative economic appeal of these two regions, however, migration within LAC has been growing steadily. According to a 2015 study by the Organization of American States (OAS) and the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD), the number of people moving within LAC increased 17% per year between 2011 and 2013, compared with a migration increase of 5% per year to the Americas as a whole over the same period. In absolute terms, the region currently hosts 4 million LAC migrants and this number is projected to more than double over the next four years.

As one of the most dynamic economies in the region, Chile is a particularly popular destination for Latin American migrants. According to data from the National Statistics Department, the number of foreign-born persons in Chile was 410,998 in 2014 (2.3% of the total population), which is about twice as many as in 2002. While citizens of neighboring Peru, Argentina, and Bolivia make up the great majority of migrants, the number of people from more distant countries like Colombia or Haiti is also significant and rising.

The acceleration and changing patterns of migration to Chile present the government, private sector actors, and migrants themselves with both challenges and opportunities. To better understand the reality of Latin American and Caribbean migrants in Chile and help decision makers address their needs in an effective way, the MIF recently partnered with the Catholic University of Chile, J-PAL, and the Jesuit Service for Migrants and Refugees to carry out a survey among Latin American migrants in Chile. The results of this survey, which contained questions about different aspects of migrant’s integration into Chilean society, are available in a new report launched this week during an event at the Center for Public Studies (Cento de Estudios Publicos) in Santiago.

The report shows that while LAC migrants in Chile are well integrated in social and economic terms, this integration depends to a large extent on their access to quality employment. For example, while those migrants who do have work report salary levels that are comparable to those of the Chilean population in general – a fact probably due to migrants’ relatively high levels of educational attainment – there is also a large share of migrants who are unemployed and who live in financially precarious situations, characterized for example by a lack of any kind of savings. 

In line with the objectives of the MIF’s Remittances and Savings facility, the survey also inquired about the state of financial inclusion of Latin American migrants in Chile. The corresponding results suggest that similar to what we found in the context of LAC immigrant communities in Spain and the United States, there is still a significant share of migrants to be brought into the formal financial fold. Almost half (48%) of all migrants who report saving, for instance, do so at home and not in a bank, thereby forgoing the benefits – for example in terms of safety and diversification - of accumulating assets in a formal financial institution. A particularly promising avenue for transforming more migrants into clients of basic financial products such as savings accounts might consist in leveraging their remittance transfers. Of the 95% of migrants who regularly send money to their families in their countries of origin, only 5% currently do so via a bank channel, an indication of the scant involvement of Chilean banks in the remittance market.

There is a lot of welcome news in our report on the situation of LAC migrants who decided to move to Chile. However, additional steps should be taken to expand economic well-being and long-term financial security to those who currently lack access to a stable source of income. For financial institutions such as banks and credit unions, such steps include innovating in marketing and product design so as to provide more migrants with savings accounts and other long-term financial products. To the extent that this is achieved, Chile could provide valuable lessons for stakeholders in other LAC countries welcoming increasing numbers of their regional neighbors in the years to come.
