Building resilience to climate change in Sertão

Building resilience to climate change in Sertão

October 19, 2016
By
Guest

By Daniele Cesano, Technical Coordinator, Adapta Sertão

Bacia do Jacuipe is a small county in the semiarid Sertão region in Brazil. Characterized by low precipitation and high deforestation rates, the region has suffered the impacts of climate change, in particular increased temperatures and drought.  Already facing socioeconomic challenges, the incomes and livelihood of farmer families are at risk of further decline.

Since the year 2006, the coalition of agricultural cooperatives "Adapta Sertão" has been researching and developing climate smart production systems to make small-scale family farmers more resilient to climate change. The focus has been on finding practical technical standards to improve access to water, upgrade farming technologies, and process plants and markets for agricultural products. Through ongoing field-testing and experimentations, a training manual has been developed, Smart and Sustainable Agro-Climate Model (MAIS for its Portuguese acronym), with the concepts and principles for adapting agriculture to climate change. 

 Figure 1. Bacia do Jacuipe county and its location in Bahia state.

Climate Smart Agriculture as way to build resilience 

Climate-Smart Agriculture (CSA) is an effort to transform agricultural systems to effectively support development and ensure food security in a changing climate, based on three main objectives: sustainably increasing agricultural productivity and incomes; adapting and building resilience to climate change; and reducing and/or removing greenhouse gas emissions, where possible.  As climate change impacts are likely to increase over time, it is reasonable to claim that CSA is the new frontier in agricultural innovations to ensure food security and a low carbon economy in both the developing and developed world.

When looking at CSA, the number of issues that shall be tackled simultaneously is very large, from drought-resistant crops, livestock-pasture integration, and soil and water conservation, to surveillance and monitoring systems, pests and disease management, and financial incentives and funding mechanism. The development and dissemination of a comprehensive CSA approach requires the integration of several different areas that span from engineering, natural science, technological innovations, financial mechanisms and policy-making.

Adapta Sertão, CSA, and the MAIS production system

The MAIS includes more than 20 different CSA consolidated technologies and practices, and is currently being implemented and tested at a few hundred farms in Jacuipe Basin. It starts from the observation that the local rural economy in most parts of semiarid Brazil focuses mostly on animal breeding for milk and meat production. 

The MAIS distinguishes between specialized and diversified farms based on farm area and farmer profile. For example, bovine milk and ovine meat production require specific training, a good understanding of animal management procedures and also larger plots (min. 20 hectares) because the aggregated value per hectare is relatively low compared to areas cultivated with vegetable and fruit production. Farmers with less than 20 hectares of land under rain fed settings need to diversify their production to have products with a higher aggregated value like fruits and vegetables.

For the MAIS Milk and MAIS Lamb Meat productions, 100% of the pasture land is in silvopastorial settings based on the methodology of “MAIS Pasto com Caatinga”, which is the reforestation and pastureland restoration technique associated to the MAIS system. 

The MAIS production system has developed a very clear set of technical standards that aim to make farms and farmers resilient to climate change. Within the MAIS system, climate resilience is defined as the ability of a farm to reach its production potential, regenerating the environment and minimizing oscillation in the face of climate change.

This means:

1. Achieving maximum production efficiency within the farm and farmer's resources: Prevent the drought
2. Regenerating the environmental features of the farm: Prepare for the drought
3. Minimizing the seasonal fluctuations and medium-term productivity and profitability: Pass the drought

The result of this process automatically implies social improvements. In this context, the MAIS was developed to help farmers achieve this definition of climate resilience. The words Prevent, Prepare and Pass synthetically summarize the principles of the MAIS.
