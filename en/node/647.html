The NEO challenge: look outward to make an impact on youth employment

The NEO challenge: look outward to make an impact on youth employment

April 09, 2015
By
Elena Heredero Rodriguez

By Elena Heredero Rodriguez and Claudio Cortellese

The focus of the initiative New Employment Opportunities for Youth (NEO) is simple: to help 1 million young people in Latin America and the Caribbean prepare for the job market and find a first good job. The initiative sets out to achieve this by creating multi-sector partnerships with stakeholders in education, job training, and businesses. The theory is that together, they will address problems of youth unemployment, young people’s lack of work skills, and employers’ need for skilled workers.

The goal is simple but the strategy to achieve it is not; the problem is complex, and difficult to address from a single perspective. It requires a systemic approach.

To put together the puzzle, it is vital to have the perspectives of young people, employers, training institutions, job counseling and employment agencies, and those who implement youth employment programs and public policies. Only in this way will we understand where to find the failures, disconnections, disincentives, duplication, and mistrust, and little by little proceed with connecting, creating new incentives and relevant information, developing processes of dialogue, and building the confidence necessary for the youth employability system to be effective, yield results, and be efficient.

"The interview went very well because NEO showed us how to express ourselves, what to say, and how not to be nervous. I am in a 45-day trial period, and I think they will hire me because the owner of the plant, Mr. Juan, said my performance has been excellent. This job will help me contribute at home. I live with my Mom and two brothers. I also have a son, and we all feel part of NEO.”
Joel Andrés Mosquera Mazo (Colombia)

NEO was launched three years ago and in this period of time has managed to build five multi-sector partnerships: one in the state of Nuevo Leon in Mexico; another in the subregion of Urabá in the   department of Antioquia in Colombia; and three at the country level in the Dominican Republic, Panama, and Paraguay. Each partnership involves 10 to 15 actors and there is always a combination of businesses, the public sector, and civil society/academia. All of these partnerships set out to collaboratively develop and implement a youth employment plan or project for three years, with the members providing resources, knowledge, and skills.

This process of different sectors participating in the projects has been one of the most challenging for the NEO implementation team, which consists of representatives of the International Youth Foundation, the Multilateral Investment Fund, and the IDB’s Labor Markets and Social Security Unit.

In each of these countries, when we held the first workshop and proposed the NEO challenge, that is, to apply a systems approach and to work in partnership with complementary experiences and skills, the answer was always that we were welcomed—enthusiastically by some, more skeptically by others.

However, what we noticed is that there are almost no references for this type of approach to the issue of youth employment in the region. The actors are not used to thinking this way, seeing not only the tree but also the entire forest. The logical reaction for everyone is to think only about my project, my beneficiaries, my goals. Meanwhile, what we propose with a NEO partnership is a comprehensive approach. It is almost revolutionary to encourage the actors to look at what another entity does, what it learned from running a project, and the results obtained.

Why this gap? In the countries where we ventured there are not enough opportunities for reflection, dialogue, and knowledge about this specific topic. It is difficult to get away from everyday projects and stand back and look at the system as a whole. It is also complex, because the system does not change because of a single intervention, but rather because of coordinated action, with a common vision that is maintained over time.

"A systems approach begins when first you see the world through the eyes of another," the philosopher and systems scientist West Churchman said. This is the first step in changing a system: see the world with different eyes and imagine that another world is possible.
