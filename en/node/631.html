Can we invest intelligently in microfinance?

Can we invest intelligently in microfinance?

May 01, 2015
By
Yuri Soares

Microfinance has grown from an outfit financed by a few donors and philanthropic foundations, to a large and profitable industry: a multi-billion-dollar worldwide endeavor reaching 2 billion clients in low- and middle-income countries. The amount of international public and private funding to microfinance institutions has likewise increased dramatically—I estimate by a factor of 14 over the past decade, based on this source and this source—reaching $31 billion in 2013. Given this success, and the amount spent by impact investors (including development finance institutions such as the Inter-American Development Bank), are we investing intelligently?

From a financial point of view, I would argue that we are. The industry has adapted instruments from finance to better predict risk, and today we assign funds accordingly. The amount of analytic brainpower and technology used to predict financial returns in the overall financial industry is astounding, and even in the microfinance segment, the application of standard financial evaluation tools has become commonplace.

The development finance industry has experimented with ways to prioritize investment based on certain characteristics, such as the extent to which microfinance institutions are focused on fighting poverty, or, more recently, how well they apply standards of good corporate behavior (see for instance, the Smart Campaign). But these instruments have not adopted the sophistication used in risk analysis that allows you to “score” programs based on their expected effectiveness. This is mostly due to technical limitations. There are many development outcomes, and there is lack of consensus on their relative importance. There is also no consensus on the extent to which market and commercial success are “sufficient” to achieve broader economic and social objectives. Often, outcomes are not precisely defined and are difficult to measure. And finally, we do require high quality evidence in order to make intelligent investment choices. Given these challenges, we have not been able to develop instruments that would systematically score investments based on their potential for “development” success.

However, conditions are right for us to experiment with ways to overcome these barriers. This is why the IDB is partnering with the U.S. consulting firm Mission Measurement and a coalition of industry and development actors to try to develop a systematic way to predict microfinance outcomes in advance, using tools that are already commonplace in areas such as finance. By adapting the “Impact Genome” approach developed by Mission Measurement for the microfinance industry, we hope to be able to build an instrument that—together with the financial analysis already used—can score potential investment opportunities. This approach will attempt to codify all the available evidence on microfinance, and then to relate that evidence to the different characteristics (“genes,” in Mission Measurement lingo) of each potential investment, allowing us to invest with the best available evidence. For example, an investment in a microfinance institution with a proven track record, which deploys microfinance products that have previously proven to be effective, would score high under this approach.

The particularities of microfinance make this an attractive experiment, for two reasons. 

First, there has been a remarkable amount of research on the effectiveness of microfinance, and the use of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) has provided a much more precise measurement of what does and does not work. The accumulation of more experimental evidence over time is providing more facts, which are allowing us to deal with the issue often raised in the microfinance debate about the extent to which results can be generalized to other situations. Recent overviews of microfinance (see a 2013 article by Banerjee and a more optimistic note published by CGAP) show just how far we have come. Also, there is a large amount of “gray” evidence that is simply unexplored: high-quality case studies, or manuscripts about specific microfinance investments that are not publishable for various reasons. This gray data is unceremoniously shunned by “meta-evaluations,” which aggregate information from several individual evaluations of microfinance investments. But the gray data can be used to both provide evidence about the effectiveness of investments, and to provide additional facts about the characteristics of potential investments (although it would carry a lower weight than evidence provided by an RCT).

Second, microfinance is an industry that continues to attract the particular kind of investors for whom development is important. And at the end of the day, the investment channels and instruments will respond to the demands of those who put up the cash. I believe that a more rigorous and structured technology that helps to prioritize investments, and that more closely parallels the technology used for financial decision making, will resonate with this industry. After undergoing harsh criticism the past three years, the industry itself is receptive to new ways of looking at microfinance as a financial inclusion tool.

By linking program characteristics to results, we are forced to take a hard look at what microfinance can realistically achieve. Today, microfinance has expanded beyond basic microcredit products. By systematically codifying and rating the evidence associated with different flavors of microfinance, and using this tool for decision making, we can help to shape tomorrow’s industry.
