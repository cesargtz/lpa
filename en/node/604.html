A big step toward the first social impact bond in the developing world

A big step toward the first social impact bond in the developing world

November 25, 2015
By
Zachary Levey

Over the last decade, Latin America and the Caribbean have seen unprecedented development gains. Over 60 million people escaped poverty and over 100 million people entered into the middle class. For the first time in the history of the region, there are more people in the middle class than in poverty.

Yet, the tailwinds of the global commodity boom have begun to subside and most regional economies are expected to slow down. In these times when government has to try to more with less, there is a need for innovation around social problems more than ever. In a digital world of technology and many sources of data, there is an opportunity for LAC governments to change and become models for government of the 21st century: smarter, more accountable and more outcomes-focused. 

Over the last year, a new financial product called social impact bonds (SIBs) has garnered excitement throughout Latin America as a way for government to pay for social services in the event  that a results is achieved through a partnership with impact investors.  Around the globe, a growing number of OECD countries, such as the U.S. and U.K., have begun to develop social impact bonds to solve a variety of different social problems. 

On November 4th, 2015, Mexico took a big step toward launching the first social impact bond—not only in Latin America, but indeed in the entire developing world. The Governor of the State of Jalisco, Mexico, Jorge Aristóteles Sandoval Díaz, and the Executive Vice President and Chief Operating Officer of the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB), Julie T. Katzman, co- signed a letter of support between the IDB, its Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF), and the Government of Jalisco that authorized funds for the final design phase for a social impact bond that could be completed as soon as first quarter of 2016. 

The social impact bond in Jalisco would be used to fund services that would seek to increase the income and resilience of poor, female-head households with the goal of a permanent transition out of poverty.  In Mexico, one in four households is led by a single parent, and 86% of these are led by women. While the responsibility of single parenthood is a challenging situation for both men and women, women who are single working mothers generally face systemic social and economic disadvantages. Single mothers have a higher likelihood of experiencing poverty, food insecurity, and homelessness, as they are often are left without support networks and other means of aid as they have little disposable time to involve themselves in family networks or community organizations. The social impact bond will enable to the State of Jalisco to promote innovation around this problem by paying for services for women as they rise out of poverty. 

Globally, the social impact bond model is still in its infancy. There are only around 40 social impact bonds that have launched and only three have reached maturity. These first experiences suggest that this is a model that is still a prototype, with high production costs and subject to potential design flaws. In the U.S., Salt Lake County, Utah, which recently announced that it will make success payments for a SIB used to finance pre-school education, has fallen under scrutiny. A recent article in the New York Times suggested that the way success was measured may be flawed and that the children were not helped as much as was claimed. The case of Salt Lake County underscores two key points for Latin America: it is important that these first transactions are as well designed as possible and, even so, there will be bumps along the way. 


The news of collaboration between the IDB and the government of Jalisco is an important milestone for the state and for global efforts to encourage governments to switch to outcomes-focused funding. But there remains a tremendous amount of work to be done by all partners involved to make this into a reality.  

Mexico is one of the most promising locations for the development of a SIB market in Latin America. Should this SIB develop fully, it could have ripple effects in the way public services are funded and evaluated, benefiting vulnerable populations throughout Mexico and the developing world. We are looking forward to being part of this exciting new possibility and will keep you updated on its progress.
