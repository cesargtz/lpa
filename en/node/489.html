Looking behind the picture of mobile money

Looking behind the picture of mobile money

July 06, 2015
By
Fermín Vivanco

The argument goes like this: “Most people in developing countries have mobile phones, so voilà, they could use those phones to access financial services”.

It sounds reasonable.  For many, mere use of a mobile phone for financial services seems to be exciting enough. These days, some people even think that mobile financial services will eventually make banks obsolete.  If you visit many of the donor-sponsored websites that pay homage to mobile financial services, such as Better than Cash, the Alliance for Financial Inclusion, Financial Access Initiative, Mobile Money for the Unbanked, and others, you are likely to find photos of entrepreneurs with a mobile phone and a smile.  The pictures are powerful, but what do they mean? What, exactly, is the positive effect of using of mobile phones for financial services?

Now, let´s talk about the basics of mobile financial services, or “digital finance,” as is the preferred term nowadays. Let´s take G2P digital payments, for example. In essence, the “digital” part of the transaction just consists of a unique password and instructions which are sent by SMS or a similar messaging system, which the recipient then uses to withdraw their payment at an ATM, or at a banking agent. To make G2P digital payments convenient, piles of cash have to be physically sent anyway to ATM´s and bank agents.  The same process has to happen to make mobile remittances, or mobile PSP. 

Governments can use a “digital finance” mechanism to pay Conditional Cash Transfers to millions instead of using prepaid cards, debit cards, or cash payments. But a digital payment, by itself, does not constitute a value proposition for the recipient unless she or he uses linked services such as a mobile wallet,  or a virtual debit card that is convenient for the recipient to use at stores, ATMs, and bank agents. That is the part that has been the difficult to achieve.  Recipients of CCTs often think that leaving a balance in their accounts may lead to the loss of the subsidy, or may signal a lack of financial need, regardless of the way the subsidy is delivered. This fear may discourage a more multifaceted use of these tools.  In Colombia, most people who receive payments from the Familias en Accion program in a Daviplata mobile account end up withdrawing the entire subsidy at ATMs or agents as soon as they receive it, rather than maintaining a balance. That experience is repeated over and over, from country to country. 

There are potential cost savings benefits to paying a CCT subsidy digitally, but for the recipient the benefit usually lies in being able to withdraw their money from an ATM or at an agent in a more convenient location and with a smaller wait time, not in any financial inclusion benefits. 

Perhaps, instead of putting so much emphasis on a mobile money versus cash debate, we should focus on more substantial issues surrounding the improvement of the lives of these payment recipients, such as helping people to save for the future needs instead of having to borrow, helping them to manage their family budgets, and creating opportunities for them to invest in a business or housing.   

Improving the use of savings products among CCT recipients, which is the focus of the MIF´s Prosavings program, may be achieved by leveraging the way the payments are made. For example, you can design a commitment savings product that caters to CCT by offering extra incentives to make savings deposits the day after a subsidy is received, or by decreasing the cost of the affiliation to a credit union for a CCT recipient. 

The channel of the payment is important (and it can be done by mobile phone!), but we need to analyze how the channel actually contributes to financial inclusion. Perhaps mobile payments are a means, but not an end in and of themselves. What do you think?
