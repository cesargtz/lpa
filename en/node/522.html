The Business of Next – News from the Global Entrepreneurship Congress 2016

The Business of Next – News from the Global Entrepreneurship Congress 2016

March 24, 2016
By
Monica Pina Alzugaray

Since my first visit to this city back in 2006, many things have changed. At the time, there was little startup activity and only a few pioneers –incubadora Creame and Promotora– establishing angel networks and the first Venture Capital fund of Colombia. Today, the city is home to other funds, co-working spaces, accelerators, and Ruta N: an initiative that turned around a “suspicious” neighborhood in Medellin and made it into a flagship for innovation and entrepreneurship. Medellin has been a good example of how the public and private sectors can join forces to create a vibrant environment where entrepreneurship can flourish. No surprise that in 2013 Medellin was declared “Innovative City of the Year,” beating out fellow finalists New York City and Tel Aviv. Three years later, Medellin was the perfect place to host the Global Entrepreneurship Congress (GEC) a global encounter of the brightest minds and leaders in innovation and entrepreneurship to discuss the “Business of Next”.

For us at the MIF, the timing of this event couldn’t have been more perfect.  At GEC 2016 the main topics centered around entrepreneurial cities, ecosystem metrics and mapping, creating entrepreneurial mindsets, attracting global talent, early stage investing, startup accelerators,  global data sharing, and youth entrepreneurship.

As it happens in this type of events, there were lots of people attending sessions, networking, pitching their startups … Lots of creative chaos going on! 

We share with you some of the main takeaways that we found inspiring and practical at the same time.

When it came to enhancing the quality and performance of metropolitan areas, the role that mayors and municipalities have in seeking ways to make entrepreneurs important allies in improving their cities was highlighted. For entrepreneurs to stay in their cities, they have to find that they provide the right habitat for their creativity.

Efforts to map ecosystems at the local, national and even global levels were described as a critical first step, since decision makers need more and better data to guide their actions. Endeavor Insight presented its Network Maps initiative: these maps identify and track relationships between entrepreneurs, investors, and support organizations in five cities and industries in LAC, and its analysis shows enlightening conclusions about these ecosystems and provides recommendations on how policymakers and other key stakeholders can work with the private sector to foster thriving entrepreneurial communities. We will be posting a blog and links to these studies soon, so stay tuned!

The role education plays in creating an entrepreneurial mindset, was the topic of another section by MIT Professor Bill Aulet . It is well-known that in innovation-driven economies, education plays a central role shaping the minds of future entrepreneurs. Focusing on creating  entrepreneurs, not just companies, is an extremely inclusive force because, as Prof Aulet put it, “entrepreneurship is a merit-based system, nobody cares where you come from”. Little by little, in LAC we’re catching up and efforts are underway to foster an entrepreneurial mindset rooted in creativity, adaptability, persistence, communication, leadership, and strategic thinking. The sooner we start working with organizations focused on creating entrepreneurs, the better. 

In another fascinating panel by Priceline co-founder Jeff Hoffman and VivaColombia co-founder William Shaw, we heard ideas on what it takes to disrupt traditional markets. Their first recommendation was to look at other industries. Did you know that the first drive-through was invented for a bank and not for a fast-food restaurant? It was the owner of a fast-food restaurant who, looking to bring some innovation to his business visited a few banks and bumped into some construction workers as they were building a drive-through for the bank. Like the panelists put it: “good ideas are everywhere, you just need to look outside your industry”. In this spirit of venturing outside our comfort zone, Jeff Hoffman introduced us to the concept of “info-sponging” or taking 5-10 minutes of your time every day to read a story that has nothing to do with you or your business and write in one sentence what you have learned. Although this may seem just another exercise to rest your mind from the daily-routine, each sentence is actually the piece of a puzzle, one that may not have a clear shape today but as you look at it one day you’ll be able to connect the dots. You can hear Jeff Hoffman here. 

The role played by early-stage investors in helping entrepreneurs unleash their ideas and reach their full potential was another important panel. A timely infusion of capital can be the difference between failure and success—helping young companies start and scale while driving innovation and economic growth. Angel investing, the rapid rise of crowdfunding, cross-border impact investing, and policies to promote early stage investment took central stage. The rise of startup accelerators was discussed. Initial successes by some of them show they are promising models that can unleash the entrepreneurial force and, as a side effect, support more gender diversified teams! 

Finally, youth remain as a largely untapped source for large-scale economic growth. Youth unemployment remains a global challenge and accelerating entrepreneurship among millennials would be a large step in the right direction. 

These and other inspiring quotes all come from the different sessions we attended at the Global Entrepreneurship Congress/GEC in Medellin this week:

“The role of governments is to help create a conducive environment for entrepreneurship, but they can’t produce the entrepreneurs.”

“Climate change is calling for adaptation through entrepreneurship.”

 “Run away from people who tell you it can’t be done. Find the 3 out of 97 that made it and take that as your model.”

“Shame in life is in not trying, not in failing!”

These days have been intense: tons of networking, learning from others, understanding new trends, and passing our own experiences and lessons learned. Now, back at the MIF, with a list of potential ideas and new partners to work with, we feel tired but happy.
