SIBs and PPPs: Expanding cross-sector partnerships to solve social issues

SIBs and PPPs: Expanding cross-sector partnerships to solve social issues

April 16, 2015
By
Guest

Social Impact Bonds are being discussed at this year's PPPAmericas conference in Uruguay. 

Over the past year and a half, a financial mechanism called social impact bonds (SIBs) has garnered excitement throughout Latin America as an alternative way to fund social programs – from governments and private investors, non-profits and social entrepreneurs from Mexico to Brazil, Colombia to Chile.

While SIBs may be new, they are, in many ways, like Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs), familiar to governments around the world and in particular to governments in Latin America and the Caribbean.

This is not to say that PPPs and SIBs are the same; in fact they are used to accomplish very different goals. While PPPs generally finance infrastructure and maintenance outputs, SIBs fund and reward changes in behavioral outcomes which can be much more difficult to achieve.

However, both conceptually and in their implementation, SIBs and PPPs share 3 main similarities:

  • 1)      Transfer of risk: PPPs and SIBs harness private sector innovations for the public sector, yet new solutions to long-term social issues can be risky. Just like a PPP, a SIB transfers the risk of investment in an innovative social intervention from the government to the private sector, ensuring that public funds are only used when a social program actually delivers on its intended results.
  • 2)      Implementation: Both PPPs and SIBs are implemented in similar ways as well, using the principles of project finance in their design conception and financing. Instead of a “special purpose vehicle” to manage and attract investment from the private sector with a PPP, SIBs utilize an “intermediary” to attract private sector funding and manage the non-profit service providers.
  • 3)      Regulation: When looking build legal structures and regulations for a SIB, policy makers can easily draw from lessons learned from current PPP regulations that were developed to frame infrastructure projects.

SIBs should be viewed not as a completely new, but rather as a tool that can build off of the policy and legal structures that both governments and private investors are already familiar with from PPPs. By looking at SIBs in this way, all stakeholders can search for a variety of ways that PPPs and SIBs can be used to tackle similar issues.

While they are intended for very different purposes, what are the areas in which PPPs and SIBs can be used to work together? For what kinds of social issues could a PPP and a SIB be used to tackle two separate needs of the problem? What is the intersection of infrastructure and social programs?

These are the kinds of questions that policy makers familiar with PPPs can ask as they look to understand new ways to address high-priority and systemic social problems. 

By Alexander Honjiyo. Alex is a part of the MIF’s Social Impact Bond program team and a graduate student at Georgetown University’s Walsh School of Foreign Service.
