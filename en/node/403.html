Step No.1 to win low-income customers: discard your stereotypes

Step No.1 to win low-income customers: discard your stereotypes

September 23, 2015
By
Guest

By Susan Olsen

An unwillingness to overcome stereotypes about people at the economic base of the pyramid (BOP) could easily be the most important explanation of why some BOP-focused companies aren’t successful. Shattering stereotypes before starting an inclusive business model was the basis for a provocative panel discussion that took place during the Inter-American Development Bank’s BASE III Forum in Mexico City this summer.

“Poor people cannot pay. Let’s sell them something cheap,” is a perception held by companies when they develop BOP-targeted strategies, according to the panelists who participated in the session, which was moderated by Olivier Kayser, managing director and founder of Hystra, a global consulting firm based in France. The panel of four marketing experts from Brazil, Peru, and Mexico discussed how getting to know the desires, needs, and tastes of a company’s customer base determines the success of a business.

Aline Ross, founder and partner at Lexia Insights Solutions in Mexico City, lamented that the “poor you” attitude is a barrier to a sustainable business. That mindset blocks the ability of companies to develop good products and services that BOP populations can buy with their disposable income. Ross reinforced the message with an example of Diconsa, a Mexican state-owned store: “In rural areas, a family will pay three or four more Pesos to get the cooking oil they want and know is good quality, passing over the cheapest Diconsa brand. People simply don’t often want what is cheapest and don’t value what they get for free.”

Luciana Aguiar, from Plano CDE in São Paulo, Brazil, stressed that “the lowest cost may mean a high price” for low-income consumers, if they spend their hard-earned money on a product that is of poor quality. Studies have shown that for BOP consumers, the economic return must be tangible. This applies not only to traditional consumer goods, but also to services and more upscale products in financial services, such as savings or insurance instruments, which support accumulation of wealth.

Rolando Arellano, from Arellano Marketing in Lima, Peru, took a more dramatic stance on why companies should leave behind their BOP stereotypes. He called for an “exorcism” of company views that marketing to the BOP must be socially focused.

“There’s a lot of room in the middle (of the market),” according to Arellano, who urged companies to see the BOP as another business decision, using market intelligence and proper targeting to reach this large base of consumers more effectively.

Saide Chávez, the manager of Mexican cement producer Cemex’s Patrimonio Hoy program—which targets the housing needs of the low-income population—deepened the discussion by sharing the benefits of lessons learned from the last 17 years of running Patrimonio Hoy. Companies succeed when they provide BOP consumers with what they “like” and what they “want,” more than what they “need,” he said. This is in line with research on market creation and strategy that says that although the BOP has needs, that doesn’t mean there is an actual market of ready customers.

“It’s not just a segment,” Saide insisted. “It’s a vast market made up of many individual preferences and purchasing goals. So even talking about the BOP as if it were a single market is a misnomer.”

Leaving stereotypes aside and actually getting to know the clients’ desires is key, and must be complemented with a good quality product. This can even be the best marketing strategy: If a company builds trust among its BOP market clients, the friend-to-friend referrals that are used extensively in the highly dynamic social networks of the BOP can accomplish the rest of the sales marketing.

Selling to the BOP doesn’t mean selling cheap products in response to what we think are basic needs that need to be met. BOP consumers seek a tangible attractive return on investment for their purchases, and to be understood like customers in any other market.

A version of this post appeared originally on the Inter-American Development Bank Group’s Sustainable Business blog.
