A different approach to crediting smallholder farmers

A different approach to crediting smallholder farmers

April 15, 2016
By
Guest

By Leo Tobias, Group Manager, Software Development, Grameen Foundation 

It’s hard for a smallholder farmer to get a loan. According to the Initiative for Smallholder Finance, the $450 billion global demand by smallholders completely outstrips the global supply of $20-$30 billion.  A major factor limiting farmers’ access to finance is lenders’ perception of the high credit risk associated with farming.

But are all farmers high risk?  And is there a way to evaluate a farmer's ability to repay their loans based on who they are and how they farm, rather than on their credit histories or collateral? 

Grameen Foundation has long collected data to help farmers improve their yields and connect them to markets. Now, through the SAFE Platform, Grameen Foundation is joining forces with Starbucks, Cooperativa de los Andes (COOPERAN), COEX and the MIF to pilot a new way to assess risk: one that objectively and rigorously evaluates a farmer’s ability to pay back his/her loan.

Based on its long track record in agricultural lending, Grameen Foundation led the creation of the tool—named Agricultural Risk Evaluation Tool (ARET)—based on mobile-enabled collection of farm-level data by community workers to remedy the information imbalance between lenders and farmers, helping lenders understand the actual risks of lending to smallholder farmers.

Through a short series of questions, Grameen Foundation was  able to provide insight into the farmer’s likelihood of defaulting on their loan. A rigorous analytic process informed this straightforward tool.

ARET draws on data from more than 1,500 smallholder farmers in Colombia who have received loans. More than 150 variables were evaluated —ranging from fertilizer use and water access to the number of temporary workers they hire at harvest and certifications they achieved. From this data,  ten key correlated variables were identified to segment farmers into eight groups representing increasing risk of loan defaults ranging from 0 percent to 17.1 percent. 

Piloting ARET

Over the next year, Grameen Foundation will pilot ARET with Cooperativa de los Andes (COOPERAN), a large coffee cooperative in Antioquia, Colombia. With funding from the Multilateral Investment Fund, Starbucks and COEX, COOPERAN will establish a Community Knowledge Worker (CKW) program through which a network of trained field workers from the community will provide technical assistance to cooperative members.  The CKWs will develop individualized farm management plans that incorporate good agricultural practices for farmers and identify key areas for investment, such as equipment or replanting. ARET will help identify which farmers are lower risk and which ones will require additional support services.

During the pilot, Andes will access a dashboard to monitor its risk distribution and analyze the impact of specific variables on their overall portfolio (right).  The dashboard will also be able to visualize the risk of lending to specific populations, such as those displaced from their homes, victims of violence or members of minority groups that face discrimination. This data will have the power to debunk damaging preconceptions.

At the end of the loan cycle, Grameen Foundation will evaluate the accuracy of the tool to predict defaults and refine the methodology for risk evaluation. Ultimately, use of ARET will not only improve the efficiency of the credit process within COOPERAN but it will also enable lenders to support the success of Colombian farmers.
 

Leo Tobias

Leo Tobias is the Group Manager, Technology and Products at Grameen Foundation.  He leads the development of the Agricultural Risk Evaluation Tool (ARET) to raise the visibility of smallholder farmers to potential lenders.  Leo has architected and implemented digital financial services in the Philippines, India and Uganda enabling access for the rural poor.  In Kenya, he led the technology effort in the eWarehouse initiative which provided an important proof of concept for supporting the smallholder farmers’ ability to optimize the sale price through bulking, connection to market and access to loans.  Prior to joining Grameen Foundation, Leo was a principal consultant for a technology firm providing innovative solutions to banking and manufacturing corporations.  He is a PMP with a Business Administration degree in Accounting from Kent State University and a Certificate in Customer-Focused Product and Service Design from Cornell University.
