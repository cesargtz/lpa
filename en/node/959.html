Mayan farmers in Guatemala’s central region to increase incomes and diversify production with MIF support

Mayan farmers in Guatemala’s central region to increase incomes and diversify production with MIF support

July 30, 2014
By
Claudia Moreno

July 30, 2014.

 

The Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF), a member of the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) Group, approved a $2 million technical cooperation grant for a project that will benefit 15,000 small farmers in Guatemala’s central region. A Canadian nonprofit organization, the Centre for International Studies and Cooperation (CECI), is the MIF’s co-financing and executing partner in the project with an investment of over $877,000.

 

The Organization, Productive Diversification, and Marketing for Small Producers in Guatemala’s Mayan Communities Project will improve the quality of the farmers’ products, increasing their access to higher value markets, and diversifying their crop production, all with the goal of increasing their incomes. It will benefit the areas of Alta Verapaz, Baja Verapaz, El Quiché, and areas surrounding the Chixoy River basin.

 

Farmers in this region rely almost exclusively on a single commercial crop for their incomes: cardamom, which is exported to European, Middle Eastern, and Southeast Asian markets. However, given a highly fragmented production chain, poor road infrastructure, pests and plant disease, and other problems, these farmers remain poor and isolated. These indigenous Mayan communities experience high levels of illiteracy and poverty, with household incomes averaging less than $2000 per year.

 

High-quality cardamom can sell for as much as $9 per kilogram, but poor production quality and lack of access to post-harvest production facilities mean that the farmers targeted by this project earn as little as $2 per kilogram. The farmers also grow subsistence crops such as beans, corn, and potatoes for personal consumption, and do not grow other crops with commercial potential such as tomatoes and herbs. 

 

The project seeks to address this range of challenges on several fronts. The project will initially focus on community organization to strengthen coordination between the farmers and communities participating in the project, and build their business capacities. One group of activities will then provide technical assistance and training so that farmers can boost their productivity and capture higher values from their existing cardamom crops. Another group of activities will introduce additional crops and activities (such as animal husbandry) with commercial potential and cleaner production.

 

Guatemala’s National Association of Cardamom Exporters, a group of 10 cardamom export companies, and Unispice, a large horticulture export company, will participate in the project.

 

About the MIF

 

The Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF), a member of the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB) Group, is funded by 39 donors and supports private sector-led development benefiting low-income populations and the poor—their businesses, their farms, and their households. The aim is to give them tools to boost their incomes: access to markets and the skills to compete in those markets, access to finance, and access to basic services, including green technology. A core MIF mission is to act as a development laboratory—experimenting, pioneering, and taking risks to build and support successful micro, small, and medium business models. More information can be found at www.fomin.org .

 

About CECI

 

The Centre for International Studies and Cooperation (CECI), a nonprofit organization based in Canada, works in projects in 18 countries worldwide in the areas of humanitarian aid, development, and volunteer cooperation. After responding to the immediate emergency, CECI implements livelihood recovery and reconstruction projects, and maintains ongoing development activities that considerably strengthen and accelerate the ability of communities to return to their regular activities. For more information visit ceci.ca.
