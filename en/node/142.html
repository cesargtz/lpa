VOX Capital: Investing in sustainable market-based solutions for Brazil’s BoP

VOX Capital: Investing in sustainable market-based solutions for Brazil’s BoP

July 15, 2016
By
Yuri Soares

Click here for full-sized image 

Founded in São Paulo, Brazil in early 2009, Vox Capital is a venture capital impact investment management company that focuses on small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) serving low-income populations.

Interestingly, Vox Capital is a “double bottom line” financial firm that seeks to create both good returns with its early stage investments, but also beneficial social impacts among the Base-of-the-Pyramid (BoP) population of Brazil (which in 2015 included more than 120 million people, 60% of Brazil’s population, but is highly underserved by both the private and public sectors). 

The company raised $35.5 million for its Fund I, and as of late 2015 it had analyzed 1,430 investment opportunities in education, healthcare, housing and access to finance, and invested in 19 promising startups, with a 3.9% (unrealized) IRR (no exits).
Vox Capital Fund I itself makes $450,000 - $4.5 million equity investments in consolidated startups, while Vox Labs, its accelerator arm, makes smaller seed investments of $150,000+ in less mature startups. 

As part of its impact investing focus, Vox Capital requires all of its portfolio companies to be rated by GIIRS (the process if funded by the MIF for one year) and, while not required, investees are also encouraged to pursue a B-Corporation (or analogous) impact investing certificate, following the example set by the fund itself. 

Moreover, Vox Capital helps its portfolio companies to develop a formal theory of change, so that they can fine-tune their business model to maximize impact. Also, with the support of the MIF the fund is exploring the possibility to carry out low-cost annual impact evaluations of some of its investments, in order to generate more robust evidence about its impact.

Therefore, when in early 2015 the MIF’s Market Innovation Lab and its Early Stage Equity Team partnered with Prof. Josh Lerner and Bella Research to carry out a detailed study of early stage VC funds in which the MIF had invested, Vox Capital was one of the three funds selected to be included in the analysis. The study explored how the MIF financial and non-financial support of Vox Capital ultimately impacted socioeconomic development in LAC:
 

  • As of late 2015 companies in Vox Capital’s portfolio had experienced $7 million revenue annual growth, creating 200 new direct jobs, and serving 150,000 people daily.
  • The research team found that the networking support that the Vox Capital team provided was highly important to its portfolio companies, particularly in terms of business development and market access for its BoP-focused entrepreneurs, who had few other options for networking support.
  • Vox Capital’s business training was also very important to its portfolio companies, not only in terms of supporting management teams as they made important strategic decisions, but also to help entrepreneurs to optimize their businesses’ social impact (with the support of the MIF, the fund is continuing to develop and test strategies to measure and increase its portfolio’s social impact, serving as a model and testing ground for other Brazilian impact investors). 
  • The capital provided by the fund was also important, as all its portfolio companies were operating in equity- and/or credit-constrained environments, and other financing sources were generally not as keen on funding impact investing entrepreneurs. 
  • As expected given Vox Capital’s double bottom-line approach, most of Vox’s portfolio companies had a substantial focus on BoP populations and the alleviation of poverty through sustainable market-based solutions (such as, for example, access to financial services in Brazilian favelas, cheaper access to credit through P2P loans, or more affordable and easier to operate emergency medical equipment.
  • As the nation’s first home-grown impact investor, Vox Capital plays a crucial role in the Brazilian entrepreneurial and VC ecosystem.

Additionally, the study highlights some of Vox Capital’ most impactful and innovative investments, such as:
 

  • Tamboro (“For All Without Exception”) is a Brazilian adaptive learning software company which has developed an affordable technology-based alternative to classroom-based teaching, with the goal of reducing the learning gap between public and private schools in the country. As of September 2015 approximately 6,000 students across 24 public schools and 1,800 students across 11 private schools were using Tamboro’s software products, and the company had plans to reach 300,000 students by 2018 through a partnership with textbook publishers.
  • ProRadis aims to decrease the cost of medical imaging diagnostics for Brazilians who do not have access to private health insurance and cannot afford the long waiting-times in the public sector (e.g. two years for an MRI), by negotiating bulk rates with providers and redirecting demand towards less popular time slots during which facilities are generally underused. In order to do so, the company has developed a proprietary technological platform which seamlessly links with existing systems, and doubles as an all-in-one facilities management tool. 

Encouraged by the positive feedback from its investors, which sought both financial performance and social impact, in 2015 the Vox Capital team started fundraising for its second fund, which has a target of R$120 million (US$32 million) and will follow a similar investment thesis.
