Indigenous Peoples & the Private Sector: Partners in Advancing Rights and Well-Being

Indigenous Peoples & the Private Sector: Partners in Advancing Rights and Well-Being

August 09, 2016
By
Ana Grigera

Photo by Jeff Cremer 

At the MIF, we believe that the private sector is central to creating solutions to the development challenges of the 21st century.

August 9th, the International Day of the World’s Indigenous Peoples, offers an opportune moment to reflect upon the role of the private sector in addressing disparities and advancing the rights of indigenous peoples in Latin America.

When we hear about the private sector and indigenous peoples, it is often in relation to situations of conflict where two sides are pitted against each other. We seldom hear of indigenous peoples' private sector activity, or of private-sector actors engaging with and creating win-win alliances with indigenous peoples.

It often takes place without much visibility, but indigenous peoples are indeed engaging with public, private, and civil society actors to develop economic development models that meet market demands as well as fundamental cultural considerations.  Indeed, it’s worth noting that one of the distinguishing factors of indigenous peoples’ economic development models and aspirations is a focus on social, environmental, financial AND cultural benefits, or the “quadruple bottom line” as coined by the Maori peoples of New Zealand.  

In the past 15 years, the MIF has had the privilege of working on more than 100 projects with indigenous peoples, helping to advance their private sector development. These projects have scanned multiple sectors, geographies, and the rich diversity of indigenous peoples throughout Latin America.

Such a rich experience provides many lessons, but two particularly notable takeaways are: 1) it is possible to generate innovate business models that meet market demands while also advancing indigenous peoples’ social, environmental, and cultural development aspirations; and 2) the limited number and visibility of successful indigenous business models should be taken as a call to action to direct more attention, resources, policy, and innovation to leveraging the human, natural, and social capital of indigenous peoples to create innovative and pertinent business models.  

Below are just a few examples of how, with MIF and IDB support, private-sector mechanisms are being used to advance rights and improve the well-being of indigenous peoples.

Kallari (Napo Province, Ecuador), a Kichwa chocolate cooperative with over 850 member families, is a bean-to-bar manufacturer of internationally awarded and exported chocolate. Kallari is committed to the principles of Sumak Kaway (Buen Vivir or Living Well):  “community viability and economic growth, through knowledge sharing, and the preservation of Kichwa cultural traditions and natural resource conservation.” Its fine aromatic cacao is cultivated through traditional Amazonian garden plots.  In addition to being a successful chocolate producer, Kallari has become a key player in the Napo region’s economic development, contributing to catalyzing its territorial identity as a unique destination for chocolate, nature, and culture. The MIF is contributing to the revalorization of Napo’s cultural and natural assets through the Ruta Cultural del Cacao y el Chocolate. 

Fruta Sa (Maranhao, Brazil) is a joint venture fruit pulp company in which the Timbira peoples are half owners and participate in the company’s governance structure. Equally notable is that the company was created with the intent of protecting the Timbira’s territory.  Without nearby markets, smallholder farmers living on the margins of Timbira territory were increasingly selling their land, thereby enabling the advancement of the agricultural frontier into Timbira territory.  By creating a company that generated market demand for smallholder products, farmers were able to remain on their land, and continue to provide a buffer zone to the Timbira territory.  The MIF contributed to strengthening the integration of smallholder farmers into the fruit value chain.

Posada Amazonas, another initiative to which the MIF was able to contribute, is an eco-lodge in the Peruvian Amazon that offers an interesting joint venture model. Rainforest Expeditions, one of the largest tour operators in Peru jointly operates the lodge in conjunction with the Ese Eja peoples of the Infierno community.  The construction of the lodge was financed by Rainforest Expeditions but is the property of the community. The community receives 60% of the earnings of the Lodge, and has also benefited from employment opportunities at the Lodge.  Rainforest Expeditions is responsible for acting as an intermediary with and positioning the lodge in the market.

Our colleagues in other parts of the IDB Group are contributing to such initiatives in other ways. For example, an IDB loan is helping the Government of Chile support entrepreneurship in indigenous communities. The  Indigenous Development and Promotion Program focuses on (i) strengthening the capacities of indigenous organizations to improve the technical and sociocultural viability of their business initiatives or projects; (ii) facilitating access to financing for indigenous business initiatives; and (iii) strengthening the institutional capacities of CORFO, the Chilean Economic Development Agency, to execute indigenous productive development programs.

As we celebrate indigenous people’s tremendous contributions to the world’s cultural and biological diversity, we are also cognizant of the disparities they continue to face in our region (as well as globally). We, from the private sector perspective, should see these discrepancies as call to action to engage with indigenous peoples as equal partners in exploring how indigenous cultural and natural assets can, through private-sector innovation, be used to advance indigenous peoples’ rights, reinforce their cultural identity, and improve their well-being.  While the answers are not self-evident, promising examples exist, not only in the region but also internationally, in places like New Zealand, Australia, Canada, and the United States, which provide valuable input for future action.
