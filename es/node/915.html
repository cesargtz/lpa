Light at Home: Access to sustainable energy in Peru

Light at Home: Access to sustainable energy in Peru

Febrero 24, 2014
By
Ignacio Fernandez Admetlla

Peru’s Department of Cajamarca, with a population of approximately 1.5 million people, is located in the northernmost region of the country’s Andes Mountains. Cajamarca is one of the poorest areas of the country with an extreme poverty rate as high as 21.3% and an overall poverty rate of 52.5%, more than double the national average which is estimated at 25.8%. Insufficient, or non-existent, access to basic services such as water and sanitation, basic healthcare and energy is common for most low-income families in the region.

Peru has significantly increased the level of household access to energy in the last decade, especially in rural areas where coverage has increased from 27.6% to 64.2%. However, the goal of achieving universal electrification still has a long way to go, since hard-to-reach regions, such as Cajamarca, can have percentages of families without electrification as high as 30%.

In order to increase access to energy in Cajamarca, ACCIONA Microenergía Perú (AMP), a Peruvian social microenterprise created with the support of the Fundación ACCIONA Microenergía in 2009, has a program called "Luz en Casa" (Light at Home). This program has been designed to increase access to electricity for off-grid communities using Domestic Photovoltaic Systems (DPSs), systems composed of individual 80Wp solar panels with the capacity to provide at least four hours of electricity a day to three low-consumption lights and outlets .

Since 2012, the MIF and its Social Entrepreneurship Program (SEP) have cofinanced the “Luz en Casa” initiative in order to support AMP in enlarging its network with  1,700 additional DPSs and strengthening its service provision model in terms of technical training and community engagement. At this time, the program has 3.000 DPSs installed, which are providing sustainable energy services to 12,600 people and have allowed AMP to reach a break-even point.

In a study carried out by the Innovation and Technology for Development Centre (itdUPM) in Spain, in collaboration with the MIF, the “Luz en Casa” program was one of five case studies chosen to showcase partnerships for innovation in access to basic services. Not only has this service provision model achieved remarkable results in a short period of time, but it has also been successful in terms of stimulating active community engagement, increasing affordability for low-income families through cross-subsidization, and institutionalizing DPSs as a certified off-grid energy solution. 

AMP uses a model based on direct community participation and promotes the formation of community Photovoltaic Electrification Committees (CEFs, which are responsible for coordinating all the different stakeholders involved in the project, providing basic DPS supervision, and collecting customer fees. AMP also trains people from the community to become DPS technicians, thereby ensuring that installation and technical maintenance are provided locally.

The business model that AMP has implemented is based on a pay-for-service model where families pay a monthly fee that includes the lease for the DPS, energy consumption, maintenance throughout the system’s lifespan, and equipment depreciation. AMP applies a cross-subsidy price scheme that allows it to offer families an affordable fee based on their economic means and consumption levels. Households pay AMP 20% of the stipulated tariff while the remaining 80% is covered by the government´s Electrical Social Compensation Fund.

AMP has maintained an ongoing dialogue with the Energy and Mining Investment Supervisory Body and the Ministry of Energy and Mines in order to promote clarity surrounding regulatory requirements surrounding DPSs. It has also played an active role in developing   rules and regulations for the expansion of new energy technologies in Peru, particularly with regards to off-grid service provision in rural communities. The “Luz en Casa” initiative has been well-accepted in the communities where it operates and so far the customer default rate has been very low. After having reached its target of operating a network of 3,000 DPSs AMP has achieved financial sustainability and is able to cover its operational costs through household fees.
