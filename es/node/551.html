SME finance – it’s not all about banks and microfinance institutions!

SME finance – it’s not all about banks and microfinance institutions!

Febrero 25, 2016
By
Tetsuro Narita

Bringing a new model to a Haitian credit union 

When we talk about access to financial products and services for small and growing businesses, we put commercial banks at center stage and try to find ways to overcome such businesses’ lack of credit history and collateral. When we emphasize the importance of so-called “vulnerable” populations building up savings for rainy days or future investment opportunities, we generally identify (regulated, therefore deposit-taking) microfinance institutions as the most appropriate counterparts, based on their social missions and capillarity.

We tend to forget, however, the other important players in the financial system: credit unions and cooperatives. According to a 2014 MIF publication  there are 1,428 regulated cooperatives and 3,124 non-regulated cooperatives in Latin America and the Caribbean region. In the Caribbean in particular, the coverage, outreach, and relevance of credit unions and cooperatives is even greater. In Jamaica, for example 60% of the adult population belongs to a credit union, one of the highest rates in the world. 

The asset size of each credit union/cooperative is, in general, significantly smaller than commercial banks, but commercial banks would envy credit unions and cooperatives’ potential competitive advantages: proximity to clients, local knowledge, and therefore capacity to financially support local businesses. Recognition and re-recognition of those edges might have started in some countries in the world. The Economist reports that “[g]lobally, the number of people in credit unions has doubled since 2000, from 108m to 217m. Savings are up by 130% in real terms,” and that “fully 39% of American adults belong to a credit union, up from 36% a decade ago – an increase of 14m.”

This trend is in alignment with the way of thinking and behaviors of today’s young adults, and might even be strengthened by them. The Millennial Disruption Index found that 71% of millennials would rather go to the dentist than listen to what banks are saying, and foresee that banks will suffer major disruptions in the coming years. Millennials who are inclined to make socially responsible investments may be more attracted to local credit unions and cooperatives, because there is compelling evidence that locally-owned businesses are the key to community prosperity.

Développement international Desjardins (DID) of Desjardins Group, one of the biggest banks in Canada and one of the top cooperative financial groups in the world with more than 7 million members and clients, recognized the competitive advantages of the first-tier cooperatives in its network, and in the 1990s introduced a very innovative model called Entrepreneurs Financial Center (EFC) to provide the most-needed financial support to small and medium enterprises (SMEs).

EFC is a specialized business unit established at the network level. Its expertise in originating SME finance is utilized by the network’s first-tier cooperatives to address the financial needs of their clients. More specifically, a member client of a cooperative in Desjardins’ network who has a specific need for financing his/her business is referred to the corresponding EFC that has specialized staff and analytical tools for assessing this need. The EFC then makes a decision or recommendation, and based on this, the cooperative ultimately grants the loan and bears the credit risk.

Based on the successful implementation of the EFC business model in Canada, in 2003 DID began transferring it to developing and emerging countries. DID has implemented the model in various African countries (Rwanda, Burkina Faso, Mali, Senegal, and Benin) and has successfully demonstrated its viability. We are delighted to be able to announce our new project to bring the model to the Latin American and Caribbean region for the first time. We are collaborating with DID to implement an EFC business unit within the network of the Haitian cooperatives Fédération des Caisses Populaires Haïtiennes (FECAPH) – Le Levier. We’ll be providing US$1M in grant financing and a US$500K loan, with additional resources coming from the Canadian government and from a previously-approved IIC operation supported by the Spain-IIC Haiti Development Fund. With these funds, DID will be able to “build, operate, and transfer” the EFC model, eventually handing over its administration to Le Levier once it has achieved financial self-sufficiency. 

We believe that the EFC model is an appropriate approach to closing the estimated US$1.9 billion financing gap for growing Haitian micro and small businesses. For any financial intermediary, developing a small business financing unit (including financing for growing microenterprises) is a process that takes considerable time and money. Therefore, while it would be ideal for each cooperative to develop its own small business financing capacity, given the time and financial constraints mentioned above, and the need to close the huge financing gap, some collective action is needed to significantly increase small business financing in Haiti. With this Le Levier project, we hope to contribute both to developing an innovative approach to financing entrepreneurship in Haiti, and to providing an example that other cooperative networks in Latin America and the Caribbean may try to follow.
