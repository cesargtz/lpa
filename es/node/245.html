In search of high-growth enterprises

In search of high-growth enterprises

Junio 22, 2016
By
Irani Arraiz

Photo by Robert Scoble

According to the World Bank’s Enterprise Surveys, about 10.5% of formal firms in Latin America have been in operation for 5 years or less.  This is bad news for innovation and growth in the region.  In a typical year in the US, startups—or businesses that are just beginning to operate—account for about 10% of firms (in a typical year, 35% of firms have been in operation for 5 years or less).  These numbers are important because from this pool of young firms spring those that drive job creation, output, and innovation.  Although we know very little about firm dynamics in Latin America, we can draw some conclusions by looking at what we know about firms in the US:

 

  • In the US the firms that grow are mostly young firms.  These are the firms that, disproportionally, create jobs—the share of jobs created is small because the share of new, young firms is small:  startups account for about 10% of firms in a typical year and generate about 20% of firm-level gross job creation.  
  • Young firms are also more likely to exit the market and in the process destroy jobs—50% of jobs generated in the US by an entering cohort of firms are lost to exits by age five.   However, the net result is positive, disproportional job creation. 
  • While much growth is due to new firms, it is not true that most new firms generate growth.  Most startups fail, and most surviving young firms do not grow. 
  • It is also true that about 34% of high-growth firms are 10 years or older.  But 5 years after being created, it is highly unlikely that a surviving firm suddenly becomes a high-growth firm.  
  • Moreover, most new US firms have little desire to grow big or to innovate (either by developing a new product or service or entering new markets with an existing product or service). 
  • When asked about their desired future business size (at inception), about 75% of US business owners answered that they wanted to keep their business small (with a median of only 3-4 employees). 
  • Less than 15% of new firms in the US (at inception) expect to develop proprietary technology, processes or procedures in the future.
  • As in developing countries, some of the reasons why US business owners start a business include non-pecuniary benefits (for example, desire to be their own bosses or have flexibility over their schedule). About 35-37% of US business owners report non-pecuniary reason as the primary driver of the business startup decision.  These businesses are very unlikely to become high-growth firms.
  • Most high-growth entrepreneurs in the US have undergraduate degrees and five or more years of experience in large multinational corporations, where they learn about the industry. (This is the case in other countries too; Saudi Arabia and South Africa, for example). 
  • A typical startup founder is in her mid to late 30s.  (Yes, yes, I know; Bill Gates, Steve Jobs, and Mark Zuckerberg are all college dropouts who started Microsoft, Apple, and Facebook in their early 20s).
  • High-growth firms are distributed across industries, from retail and wholesale trade to technology. (Yes, it is a myth; high-growth firms are not concentrated in high-tech industries.) 
  • Only 6.5% of high-growth firms in the US raise money from venture capitalists (7.7% of them raise money from angel investors).  The most common source of funding is personal savings, loans, and friends and family.
  • High-growth firms represent about 2% of firms in the US.


What can we learn from all these stylized facts?  

The pool of young firms in Latin America is proportionally smaller than in the US, so it would seem that the likelihood of high-growth firms springing up in the region is also smaller (less than 2%).  

 

The pool of knowledgeable employees that could potentially become entrepreneurs is also smaller: While in the US 34% of the labor force has tertiary education; in the LAC region this proportion is about 17%. Moreover, in the US 93% of the labor force is wage and salaried workers; in the LAC region it is only 64%. 

Once I heard somebody rhetorically ask why there was no Gates, Jobs, or Zuckerberg in Latin America and the Caribbean.  

It seems clear that you need a knowledge base to successfully become a (high-growth) entrepreneur: knowledge of an existing technology (Gates and Jobs), operational processes, or an unsatisfied customer need (Zuckerberg). That knowledge base would allow you, as an entrepreneur, to innovate and potentially become disruptive.   Employees, academics, and users are in a privileged position to exploit their knowledge base.  We may not have many employees working in large multinational corporations in the region, or large budgets for research in our academic institutions, but we sure have users of products and services who feel dissatisfied.  Maybe that’s a niche we can exploit.  After all, Netflix’s founder, Reed Hastings, came up with his innovative business model, worth about US$40 billion, after being charged $40 in late fees! As we say here at the IDB, all of our work, at its core, is about improving lives. So if you have an idea that could improve lives in Latin America and the Caribbean, it could become a disruptive business. And we at the MIF would definitely like to hear about your ideas and what you need to launch them.
