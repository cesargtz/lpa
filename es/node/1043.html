DASH Forward: Sustainable and Affordable Healthcare Solutions for Low Income People

DASH Forward: Sustainable and Affordable Healthcare Solutions for Low Income People

Octubre 08, 2013
By
Nara Meli

 

Last week the MIF approved a project to support DASH, a Haitian NGO extending healthcare to low-income people – earning $5 to $10 a day – working in the formal and informal sector. A few months ago, I mentioned some of the challenges in low-cost healthcare provision, so let’s take a look at how this project helps address them.  The objective is to pilot a business model that offers a market-based, low-cost, pre-paid health card: “Carte Avantage Santé”. The card targets the most vulnerable groups of the population, for whom healthcare is often inaccessible: pregnant and breastfeeding women, parents of children under 5, elderly persons, people with chronic illnesses including HIV/AIDS and tuberculosis, and acutely ill patients. The project aims to enroll 100,000 heads of low-income households over three years.  What are the project’s components?  1) Marketing and public outreach: to recruit and train health promoters to sell the card and provide basic health education for potential customers and community organizations; and  2) Improving the provision of high quality basic healthcare services. This includes: Upgrading the existing treatment protocols to comply with national and WHO protocols for baby and maternal health; partner with national NGO programs to offer screening, prevention and treatment of STDs including HIV, Tuberculosis, Malaria, and Diabetes; train DASH staff on health protocol implementation; establish a Preferred-Provider Organization (PPO) network to complement existing DASH health centers; contract a healthcare quality manager; train customer representatives that provide information and follow-up and monitor patient satisfaction in the 11 DASH medical centers; prepare a sustainability plan to assess the long-term profitability and value of the model. Expected results among the target population by the end of the project include: at least 90% of pregnant women will benefit from access to pre-natal care and at least 90% of female clients will have access to family planning. The vaccination rate for children 12-23 months participating in the program will be at least double the current rate of 45%. The project will also create at least 100 jobs by providing training to health promoters (micro entrepreneurs) to deliver key health messages and sell the pre-paid health services cards to the beneficiaries. How does the card work? For a set fee of $37.50, and for a period of 6 months, members are entitled to unlimited consultations with a DASH doctor, have access to reduced-cost generic brand medication ($0.59) and reduced-cost lab exams ($1.18). Card membership can be renewed every six months. Purchasing the card eliminates the need to pay for health services at the point of delivery, encouraging members to visit a doctor more frequently, and build a stronger relationship with him or her. DASH will also provide referrals to NGO, donor and government programs for those patients requiring specialized or intensive care. A web-based listing of patients will be set up and made available in all medical facilities to check the membership and medical history of patients. Where to obtain it? The card is provided through DASH’s existing facilities throughout Port-au-Prince, including Cité Soleil, Martissant, Carrefour, Delmas 18, Route de l’Aéroport, Delmas 48, downtown Port-au-Prince, Tabarre, two locations in Pétion-ville as well as inside the Industrial Park (SONAPI). Why use DASH? The short answer is that DASH fills a gap between private and public sector: On one hand, the private sector believes that the low-income population cannot pay for health care services, and that there isn’t a market opportunity. On the other hand, the public sector is unable to cover all the needs of the population, due to the limited number of trained personnel, supplies and quality of care in public facilities. DASH’s Carte Avantage Santé model therefore complements public health care provision. It does not receive public subsidies nor does provide health services to government. However, the Health Ministry will participate in disseminating results and exploring scaling up the model, once the pilot demonstrate financial sustainability and positive health outcomes.  What about the gender aspect? At least 50% of the healthcare promoters will be women. This new class of micro entrepreneurs will be able to make a living by selling the membership while simultaneously promoting healthy behaviors related to family planning, cholera and other illnesses. If you want more information about the DASH model, be sure check out this blog post on the Global Partnerships’ website!
