Guatemala: Access to Health in Rural Areas

Guatemala: Access to Health in Rural Areas

December 05, 2013
By
Guest

By Andrés Martínez, Director of the EHAS Foundation and member of the Innovation and Technology for Development Centre (itdUPM) The department of Alta Verazpaz in Guatemala has a population of close to 1.2 million, of which 78% live in rural areas and 89% belong to indigenous communities (mostly Q’eqchi’, but Poqomchi' as well). In this department only one third of births are attended by healthcare personnel and two thirds of children are affected by chronic malnutrition. This, along with the difficulty of accessing the healthcare system and other cultural factors, leads to maternal and child mortality rates in the region being amongst the highest in Latin America, with an estimated 268 women dying for every 100,000 live births and a mortality rate of 1 out of 50 for children under 5 years old. Due to this situation, the Guatemalan NGO TULASALUD (created in 2008), in collaboration with the Ministry of Public Health and the Cobán National School of Nursing, has launched a program that aimed to “strengthen healthcare, primarily related to maternal and child health, in marginalized indigenous rural communities by using Information and Communication Technologies (ITCs)”. TULASALUD’s model has focused on working closely with public institutions that have a strong commitment to institutionalize services once their efficiency has been proven. In order to do so TULASALUD has launched its programs with the financial support of the Tula Foundation, a Canadian NGO that supports TULASALUD’s programs in Guatemala and will continue to do so until their costs are fully absorbed by public institutions. The project is composed primarily of three components which are designed to impact the health levels of Alta Verapaz in the short, medium and long term. In the short term TULASALUD is working with tele-facilitators to provide health assistance in marginalized areas, while for the medium term the focus is being placed on the distance training for nursing technicians and assistants. Finally, for a long term impact the program is carrying out the “X'beil li Kawilal” initiative which promotes health and preventative habits within indigenous communities.  TULASALUD has already trained 195 Community Facilitators (FCs) from the government’s Extended Coverage Program  in order for them to become tele-FCs, equipping them with a mobile phone which allows them to consult with medical experts, coordinate emergency transfers or gather a patient’s epidemiological information. These tele-FCs have carried out over 100,000 medical consultations (approximately 7,000 were related to pregnancy or birth supervision) and over 2,000 emergency transfers, of which 298 and 235 were classified as “lives saved” from an obstetric and pediatric perspective.  The tele-education component implemented by TULASALUD, in coordination with the Cobán National School of Nursing, seeks to train people from within the different indigenous communities. It is very important that the health personnel in the communities are familiar with the local languages and customs in order to increase the confidence towards the public healthcare system and reduce the high rotation among the health workers in these communities. So far 243 nursing technicians (3 year course) and 861 nursing assistants (1 year course) have been trained using multi-videoconferences, which allow for classes to be followed by groups of approximately 25 students who connect from remote training centers located in rural health centers. These remote training centers are coordinated by an academic tutor in charge of supervising the training process (class attendance, motivation, conflict resolution, etc.)  The “X'beil li Kawilal” initiative, which in the Q’eqchi’ language means “path to health”, is aimed at raising health awareness among female adolescents. At this point in the program 750 young adolescents have been educated by female community leaders who have been trained in health promotion and prevention habits, group management techniques and leadership skills. These community leaders, aided by TULASALUD through distance audio-conferences, discuss with adolescents different issues such as self-esteem, pregnancy prevention, gender violence awareness, life projects and informed decision-making. So far a total of 98 community leaders have been trained belonging to 49 different rural communities in Alta Verapaz. The public-private partnership headed by TULASALUD, the Ministry of Public Health and the Cobán National School of Nursing has been working together for 5 years and, in the areas where the program is being implemented, has contributed to a reduction of over 40% in maternal mortality and 66% in infant mortality. This is highly significant when compared to other areas where the program does not operate; they have maintained a steady maternal mortality rate and have seen their infant mortality decrease by only 22%.
