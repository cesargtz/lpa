Local Economic Development: A valid approach to achieve the new Sustainable Development Goals

Local Economic Development: A valid approach to achieve the new Sustainable Development Goals

Noviembre 24, 2015
By
Guest

By Monica Romis and Monica Muñoz *

With the opening message from the Pope and the participation of the United Nations Secretary-General Ban Ki-moon, theThird World Forum of Local Economic Development ended on Friday, October 16, after four days of intense discussions and debates. The Forum, co-organized by the City of Turin, United Cities and Local Governments (UCLG), Andalusian Fund of Municipalities for International Solidarity (FAMSI), Organization of United Regions (ORU FOGAR), Brazilian Service of Support for Micro and Small Enterprises (SEBRAE), International Labor Organization (ILO) and United Nations Development Program (UNDP), included 3 plenary sessions, 29 panels, 26 workshops, an Arena space dedicated to the presentation of best practices and ongoing initiatives, along with other initiatives organized in parallel to the Forum’s celebration.

With more than 2,000 participants from 130 countries, the Forum offered the opportunity to compare the perspectives of national, regional and local governments, associations, entrepreneurs, academics, researchers and members of civil society on how to achieve a more inclusive, equitable and environmentally sustainable development. The Forum stressed the importance of territorial policies to create better interaction between the public sector, civil society and the private sector in response to the global economic crisis.

The great thematic richness around the LED approach was organized into three major thematic lines that were explored during panels, conferences and workshops:

1. Regional competitiveness and innovation for a more sustainable and  inclusive development.

2. Location of employment generation: towards local labor and inclusive growth.

3. Towards urban sustainable development: economic development strategies for creating links between rural and urban areas.

The Forum is part of a long-standing process that has been consolidating a global alliance for enhancing the policy debate and a shared vision on Local Economic Development (LED). This edition of the Forum was an important institutional milestone and recognition of the LED approach in the global public agenda and within the United Nations system, being aware that LED represents a valid approach to implement and achieve the new Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted on September 27, 2015 by the United Nations General Assembly. 

"Local economic development seems to be the most appropriate response to the challenges posed by an economy that is globalized and often cruel in its outcomes,” said the Pope in a message sent to the Third World Forum on LED. In order to highlight the potential contribution of LED as a strategic and operational approach to meet the challenges of Sustainable Development Goals, the Secretary General of the UN Ban Ki-moon participated in the closing ceremony of the Forum, emphasizing that local goals need to be achieved in cities, regions, and territories and that local actors should have a strong ownership over the SDGs.

The Forum’s Final Declaration identified specific challenges ahead and outlined how LED constitutes today a consolidated set of diverse and widely applied practices and tools, directed at enlarging equal opportunities for income and decent work. As fully rooted in the specificity of territorial contexts and driven by the joint vision of a diversity of local actors, local development allows contextualizing and integrating the SDGs, relying on new models of partnership in the form of organized communities joining forces to find local solutions for global challenges and achieve related universal goals.

The MIF had a strong presence at the Forum. During the second day, Claudio Cortellese, former Head of the Access to Market and Capabilities Unit, participated as a panelist at the morning plenary session, in which he mentioned that in the next decades most of economic growth will be driven by the development of mid-size and peripheral cities, for which LED is an effective strategy for the construction of local governance and the strengthening of the local institutional and multi-sectoral framework. “Strengthening local communities is not only an issue of equality and inclusion, but is also an issue for growth,” Cortellese emphasized.

Other panelists from the MIF were Francisco Alburquerque Llorens, technical advisor to the ConectaDEL program, Pablo Costamagna, coordinator of the ConectaDEL program, Karen Chapple, Associate Professor of City and Regional Planning at UC Berkeley, and Monica Romis, technical advisor to the MIF. The latter two presentations also took the opportunity to present the forthcoming MIF publication “Public-Private Management Mechanism: guidelines for practice” by showcasing some of the findings and best practices illustrated in the document. Finally, the MIF program ConectaDEL together with UNDP organized the workshop “Opportunities and Challenges for Building Territorial Economic Development Networks in Latin America”, part of a broader process that included virtual and in-person spaces both before and during the Turin Forum to allow for exchange of experiences, good practices and lessons learned in building networks. During the Workshop, the next steps to move forward in building the Network for Territorial Economic Development in Latin America were discussed to continue the process.

Monica Romis *

Monica Romis joined the Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF) in 2006 as a consultant. At the MIF she has identified, designed and evaluated projects that promote local economic development throughout Latin America and the Caribbean.  In addition, she has worked with the World Bank, the Italian government, and the NGO WaterAid in Mozambique. Ms. Romis is an economist by training and received a master’s degree in international development from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

Monica Muñoz *

Monica Muñoz, is a communication consultant for the Multilateral Investment Fund (MIF), and has since 2010 led the communication strategy for ConectaDEL, Regional Training Program for Socially Inclusive Local Economic Development. Promoting knowledge management and institutional collaboration for LED, she has been a facilitator of communication for development at workshops, conferences and educational programs in Latin America.
