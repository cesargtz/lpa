Private sector finance for the communities most impacted by climate change

Private sector finance for the communities most impacted by climate change

Junio 23, 2014
By
Carrie McKellogg

 

To date, many of the important discussions about climate change have focused on national level challenges - strengthening national government departments, building climate smart infrastructure, setting national GHG emissions targets - which are critical given the magnitude of the climate challenge. But here at the MIF we view climate change from the lens of our clients: small firms and farms, and poor and low income families. Our challenge is to translate global and national conversations to the individual communities by providing training and finance for clean and efficient energy, making communities more resilient and identifying new business opportunities.

As we gather this week in Montego Bay, Jamaica, the Donors and partners of Climate Investment Funds - a $8 billion pilot that brings together multiple national and global stakeholders in a concerted effort to drive investment in clean energy, energy efficiency, sustainable forestry, and climate adaptation - are challenged with finding innovative ways reach communities most vulnerable to climate change.

While the public sector must drive change from inside government, the biggest contributor to GHG emissions is the private sector, and more data is suggesting that small firms that supply goods and services to big corporations are responsible for most of the GHG emissions.

The Climate Investment Funds have encouraged the private sector to be part of the conversation through establishing private sector “set asides” with dedicated monies within thematic areas for the private sector to test new ways to deal with renewable energy or climate adaptation.

The MIF, which focuses on supporting the private sector, especially at the community level with micro and small businesses, is uniquely positioned to be able to think of innovative ways to deploy this funding.

From training to financing

We have already implemented a variety of tools to help private sector stakeholders address climate change: from audits to help small businesses understand their consumption of water and energy, to low-cost opportunities to increase efficiency, to financing for investments in processes and changes that will generate positive returns in terms of business competitiveness and better and reduced use of natural resources in the long term.

But we know we can't go it alone. We need partners dedicated to a common objective. In the case of sustainable forestry, based on our past experience in forestry, the government of Mexico selected the MIF/IDB as the first multilateral development bank to execute a private sector operation in the context of the Forest Investment program. The Mexican government wanted to include a role for small forestry firms and indigenous community enterprises and needed to both achieve significant impacts in terms of forest preservation and job and income generation for low-income people. With our deep network of partners on the ground, we were able to identify the right organizations to reach ejidos and forest communities, and thanks to our track record in microfinance and rural lending, we were able to create a credit product that was designed for the needs of those particular small agricultural producers.

Creating opportunities in renewable energy value chains

We also think there is a fairly significant information gap that keeps small market players from being competitive. Renewable energy is a very innovative but fast moving market, where you need up-to-date information or you are quickly out. We surveyed existing tools and finally designed a new one that specifically addressed some of those gaps, and designed it in a way that it is relevant to a range of actors in the market—public and private, and of all sizes.

The Climatescope is an index/tool/report that rates the 26 counties of Latin America and the Caribbean (and soon countries in Asia and Africa) on their ability to attract clean energy and climate-resilient investment. One of the pieces of the Climatescope designed with small firms in mind is the section on renewable energy value chains for technologies for every single country. Where there are providers, who finances those providers, where there are opportunities to create a business to serve a solar or wind energy value chain, and what are missing pieces needed to complete the chain to increase renewable energy in our hemisphere.

It has been incredibly useful. The Government of Mexico, has publicly committed to use the Climatescope value chain analysis in Mexico. That is exactly the kind of utility we hopes that our tool would have and we encourage both the private and public sectors to maximize all the information available.

In developing countries, more than 90% of all firms (outside agriculture) are SMEs and microenterprises, resulting in a significant combined environmental impact. What are your ideas on how we can work and support these stakeholders? Let me know in the comments!
