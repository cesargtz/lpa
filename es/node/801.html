How the embedded education model can improve performance of loan officers

How the embedded education model can improve performance of loan officers

Octubre 10, 2014
By
Guest

 

 

In Latin America and the Caribbean, there is growing interest in making financial education more effective and more scalable. We at the MIF share this interest. We work to ensure that people served by financial inclusion projects learn to understand financial products and services better so they can improve the way they make financial decisions. Through our savings, credit, remittances, and insurance projects, we test financial education strategies connected to products. We therefore find the proposal presented by MFO in this blog post to be very valuable. 

By María Jaramillo, MFO and Andrea Stiles, AVAL Consulting

 

Eduardo, a loan officer for Banco Solidario in Ecuador, has difficulty talking to potential clients about the microinsurance products that insurance companies offer to bank clients through the loan process. He is brief in his explanations as he fears clients could ask questions he would not know how to answer.

 

Teddy faces similar challenges. As a loan officer for VisionFund Zambia, he has been asked to train clients on the use of a new electronic payments platform for loan disbursements. However, he himself does not understand how the process works or how Zoona, the payment partner, operates.

 

At Microfinance Opportunities (MFO), we have seen that an embedded education model can help loan officers manage these challenges.

 

In the process of implementing client-centered embedded financial education programs, we learned that these interventions not only benefit clients but also have positive outcomes for the credit providing organization and its staff. Embedded education is the process of leveraging client encounters in a provider’s existing service delivery channels that exist primarily for non-educational purposes. MFO’s embedded education programs focus on solving a specific problem faced by a provider, which can help loan officers and other staff working with clients achieve the outcomes required of their jobs and have a positive impact on the core business of the organization. Our experiences in Zambia and Ecuador bear this out.

 

In Ecuador, the embedded program implemented with Banco Solidario helped loan officers better explain risk management and how insurance products work. Loan officers were also better equipped to communicate to clients the benefits of the additional coverage that the microinsurance products offered. Instead of providing brief explanations, they were able to explain microinsurance products with greater transparency.

 

One loan officer from Banco Solidario commented, “Thanks to what I learned in the financial education program, I was able to prevent losing a client because he initially did not understand the insurance products that he had.”

 

In Zambia, training provided to VisionFund loan officers increased their understanding of how Zoona’s e-payments platform works. Loan officers were then able to increase clients’ understanding and confidence in how to use this service to collect their loans. In addition, trained clients were more likely to repay their loans, easing the workload of loan officers.

 

Finally, these programs also had positive impacts on the core business of the financial institutions:

 

  • In a survey carried out by Banco Solidario one month after the training, the percentage of clients that reported having an insurance product increased from 49 percent to 75 percent due to increased knowledge and awareness of the products. Following the financial education training, the number of clients who signed up for an insurance product designed to cover Hospital Services increased, as did the number of calls to the insurance company’s call-centers and the number of claims submitted.  
  • Analysis of VisionFund MIS data found that clients who received embedded education were more likely to apply for smaller increases in follow-up loans than clients who did not participate in the training. In branches that implemented the training program, average monthly 30-day Portfolio at Risk (PAR) was 7.7 percentage points lower on average after implementation occurred.
