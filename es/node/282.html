PPP models address the region’s urban challenges

PPP models address the region’s urban challenges

Mayo 08, 2017
By
Guest

By Anita Fiori de Abreu 

Imagine if all large and medium sized cities of Latin America had good quality, sustainable, modern and affordable transportation choices: metro lines, bike sharing systems and light rails. Envision cities with ample access to green, safe, well maintained public spaces and where most of the energy used is generated by biofuels originated from the recycling of municipality waste, and where information about these services are only one click away. All that functioning via innovative, transparent, sustainable and efficient Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) models, where the private sector builds and/or manages these operations, assumes risks and its remuneration is linked to performance. Sounds like a dream? As of today, it may sound that way but the dream is getting closer to reality in various Latin American and Caribbean cities.

Almost 80% of the region’s population currently lives in urban settings and this number will grow approximately 6% in the next twenty years. This growth comes accompanied by challenges related to urban mobility, waste and water management, land use, citizen security and others.

Public Private Partnerships can be a great option for governments that need to deal with these pressing social needs in a financially and environmentally sustainable way. Throughout the region, there are various recent good and innovative PPP solutions that tackle different urbanization challenges that could have demonstration-effects for other governments. 

A 2015 study commissioned by the MIF concluded that the most successful innovative PPP projects that tackle urban mobility, for example, also carry low levels of operational risk and are economically sustainable. Countries have understood that and despite various challenges, are moving forward with emblematic urban mobility PPP projects. For example, this year the government of Colombia will award a US$4.7 billion PPP contract for the Bogota Metro; Chile continues to increase its bike sharing network and a light rail system operated via a PPP contract, integrating 32 trams, is already operational in Rio de Janeiro. 

The region is also modernizing PPPs in the so called circular economy, and its main purpose is to keep different waste materials at their maximum value for as long as possible.  In this context, the Municipality of Mexico City has awarded a PPP contract of US$ 611 million to the private sector to build and operate an incineration plant, where the waste of the city will be treated and transformed into biofuels. And in Bolivia, the municipalities of La Paz, Cochabamba  and Santa Cruz are developing a new public-private partnership model, with relevant participation of civil society, to manage the recycling of these cities’ electronic and electrical waste. 

Innovation is also present in the way PPP projects are funded by municipalities. For example, the Municipality of Rio de Janeiro used a financial certificate to raise resources to finance urban planning PPP projects in downtown Rio. The financial mechanism created, also known as CEPACs or Certificates of Potential Additional Construction, was offered by the municipality to potential developers. The funds raised by selling these certificates were used by the municipality to finance urban development projects that  prioritize a better land use of public spaces and the improvement of previously degraded areas. Thus, the project enhanced local tourism and the local economy. In Rosario (Argentina), the municipality worked with local developers of a new urban district to provide part of their capital gains as donations to public spaces and other infrastructure needs. In Chile there is a study currently being carried out to collect information on the consumers’ willingness to pay for non-conventional infrastructure assets, such as museums and parks.

However, challenges to bring these projects to fruition persist, such as: transparency and public consultation processes during the PPP project cycle; more preparedness to climate change adaptation and resilience; and access to institutional investors and long term finance – that go beyond loans. The good news is that there are more and more solutions to help countries surpass these obstacles as well, including new regulation, such as a new transparency framework in Argentina or the ability to use pension funds to invest in infrastructure in Chile. There are also readily available resources, such as the recently launched NDC Invest tool of the IDB Group, that allows national governments to access resources needed to translate their climate commitments into investment plans and bankable projects.

These and other PPP topics will be discussed in 12 different and exciting sessions at PPP Americas 2017, that will be held between the 8th and 10th of May in San Jose, Costa Rica. PPP Americas 2017 is co-sponsored by the Ministry of Finance of Costa Rica and the IDB group.
