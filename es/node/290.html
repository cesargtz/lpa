Sharing Lessons on Financial Inclusion between China and Latin America and the Caribbean

Sharing Lessons on Financial Inclusion between China and Latin America and the Caribbean

Septiembre 01, 2016
By
Tomas Miller

Recently, I and a few MIF colleagues had the pleasure of traveling to Beijing for an international forum on financial inclusion that we co-sponsored with the People’s Bank of China. This was the latest meeting in the very rewarding MIF-China relationship, which began in 2009 when China first became an IDB member and a MIF donor. We have had the opportunity to exchange experience and information many times over the years, such as when representatives from Chinese financial institutions have attended our annual Foromic conferences, and at two previous seminars we co-sponsored and co-organized in China. At this event, six experts from the Latin American and Caribbean region, including financial institution executives and representatives of bank superintendencies, also joined us to share their experience.

As a huge and rapidly-growing country, China is investing a great deal of attention and resources into building a financial system that can serve even its poorest and most remote citizens, and is looking to examples from other countries and regions to help guide its efforts. As the Latin American and Caribbean region has developed one of the world’s most stable and effective microfinance systems, and since the MIF has played a key role in supporting its growth, we have been eager to share our lessons learned with our Chinese counterparts.   In the past  10 years alone, the MIF has  dedicated nearly $300 million to promoting financial inclusion in our region: strengthening microfinance institutions (MFIs), promoting innovations in reaching new client segments and developing new products, facilitating the development of financial system infrastructure like rating agencies and credit bureaus, modernizing the regulatory framework, and investing in institutions with innovative business models.

Some of our main observations about financial inclusion in Latin America and the Caribbean, gleaned from these experiences, include:
 

  • Microfinance has become closely linked to capital markets, as its liabilities have become more diversified.
  • Microfinance has evolved since the 1990s, when most microcredit was financed by NGOs. Today, 70% of all microcredit clients in our region are served by regulated financial institutions. 
  • Specialized microfinance funds that innovate in areas such as local currency lending, emergency liquidity, equity and subordinated debt, and integrated services have been one of the most effective vehicles for mobilizing international funding and catalyzing growth in local MFIs. Our investments in microfinance funds have mobilized 7.6 times as much funding, and have created local fund management capacity as well.
  • Product innovation is key to reaching underserved population segments. For example, the MIF has helped to drive down the cost of sending remittances about 10% between 2000 and 2008, which resulted in savings of approximately $6.4 billion for migrant workers and their families. This was achieved by raising awareness among policy makers and regulators, working with financial intermediaries on new financial products, and providing information and data to better understand the industry, the costs, and the flows of funds. Another example is the EcoMicro program, which is a green energy finance program co-funded by the Nordic Development Fund that provides funding to MFIs so they can target small enterprises and low-income households to help them access clean energy, increase energy efficiency, and adapt to climate change.
  • Digital finance offers exciting new possibilities. One example from our portfolio is a project with Tigo Paraguay, that country’s largest mobile financial service provider, to offer electronic wallet services in alliance with financial institutions. The Tigo Money e-wallet has increased access to the financial system from 55% to 95% of the municipalities in Paraguay.
  • Knowledge and data support a robust financial system infrastructure. Our annual Foromic conference and Global Microscope report provide important inputs for learning from the experiences of others.
  • Microfinance has not been immune to recent financial crises, so it is crucial that MFIs are prepared to withstand challenges when they come.

It is always interesting and inspiring to see the vast scale of the Chinese landscape for financial inclusion, and to offer what lessons we can. We are proud of our 1,000 microfinance institutions in Latin America and the Caribbean, but this network seems small compared to the 10,000 MFIs in China. We definitely have much to learn from each other. As Confucius once said, “If you want to succeed, first help others to succeed.” Our partnership with China has been very much in this spirit.
