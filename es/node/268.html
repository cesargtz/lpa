When it comes to Brazil, look past the headlines

When it comes to Brazil, look past the headlines

Septiembre 20, 2016
By
Susana Garcia-Robles

I just came back from California, where the Brazilian VC/PE Association, ABVCAP, held its fourth InBrazil Forum. The idea of these fora is to introduce Brazil and its investment opportunities to Silicon Valley (SV) investors. It showcases entrepreneurs and funds from the country, as well as other actors from the ecosystem: lawyers, government agencies, family offices, corporations, Silicon Valley Bank, and the MIF; all with track records supporting the VC/PE industry in Brazil and other countries.

You may think this is not the time to be on the road marketing for investing in Brazil … but you would be wrong. When it comes to Brazil, look past the headlines.

Brazil is a country tied to my beginnings in VC in the LAC region. My first trip as a MIF Investment Officer was to Brazil to do due diligence on a seed fund, and the first fund I presented to the MIF Board was also a Brazilian fund. I’ve been a witness to this country’s ups and downs, as well as its remarkable resilience and capacity to bounce back and start all over again if needed.

Although the headlines regarding Brazil may be worrying, I take them as a positive signal to the market: corruption is no longer a viable way of doing business.  Corruption, as we know, happens in both developed and developing countries. The difference lies in a country’s capacity to live by the rule of law, uphold principles, and prosecute those who trespass them. With its latest actions, Brazil has demonstrated its willingness join the list of countries that uphold the law. As Brazilians would say: Parabens! Congratulations on that move!

But back to the VC industry in Brazil.

Since 1999, the MIF has invested in more than 15 seed and VC funds in Brazil, has had gains in many of them, and has learned a lot from building an ecosystem for innovation and entrepreneurship. Brazil became our own laboratory for learning how to build an industry, and later we replicated this approach in many other countries: Colombia, Peru, and Jamaica, to name a few.

This event provided some interesting highlights from new actors in the ecosystem:

Latest regulatory changes for VC: Rodrigo Menezes from the Derraik & Menezes law firm gave an excellent presentation on the latest regulatory changes for VC, which will allow funds incorporated in Brazil to invest 20% of their funds overseas, a welcome change. Funds incorporated locally in Brazil have not been considered by many overseas investors due to this geographic restriction, and other “customized” governance issues, like allowing investors to be voting members in their investment committees, a sure NO in best international practices (The MIF is only an observer to these committees, in order to act as a broker of knowledge and relationships given our exposure throughout the region).

The rise of corporate venture capital: Carlos Kochron from Qualcomm spoke of their strategy for investing in startups that are aligned with the company’s strategy. He reaffirmed that corporates are investing in these startups to grow their own markets for the next generation of technologies, and to learn from entrepreneurs about the mid to long term themes. Microsoft Ventures spoke of Brazil as an untapped market, with an entrepreneurial  mindset between the US and Europe, trying to go for the big win and seeking capital efficiencies.

Entrepreneurs from LAC need to understand how to go global: Santi Subotovsky, from Emergence Capital and founder of Puente Labs, an initiative that connects LAC entrepreneurs to SV, spoke candidly of the difference in expectations between SV investors and LAC entrepreneurs. To date, LAC hasn’t had impressive exits, so when an entrepreneur goes to SV and describes a successful exit as one that returned US$30 M, they lose the investors’ interest. SV investors are looking for “unicorns,” companies valued at US$ 1 B. Part of the problem, he explained, is that to create a global company, you have to have a global team. You start with a local team and then you need to expand it. Some LAC entrepreneurs are still too focused in creating companies that can serve small, niche markets.

Inclusion of women as part of the entrepreneurial and investment communities is a must for delivering financial results: Little by little, Brazil is catching up to having more women co-founders of startups, and some are making their way into funds’ management teams, and investing as angels. Still, there is a long way to go. Initiatives like Mulheres do Brasil and an IDB/SEBRAE program to foster entrepreneurship among Afro-descendants contribute to the democratization of the entrepreneurial landscape.

Impact Investment is gaining traction: Julia Profeta, from Vox Capital, spoke about some of the fund’s investees and how the application of technology to improve the lives of underserved communities is an attractive business proposition. There is an important contribution that multilateral development organizations like the MIF can make, paving the way for new investors who will feel reassured if they see that investors with solid track records are choosing to invest in these funds.

At a dinner hosted by Silicon Valley Bank at the end of the event, we were asked to share our impressions of the day. I highlighted the resilience of Brazil as a nation and in this industry. And added that the Brazil VC community had always acted as a group, watching out for each other, even if at the end of the day all are competitors. This attitude in the VC community has been a great plus for its growth: fund managers in Brazil all want to be #1, but they also know that a success for their peers is a success for the whole industry in Brazil, and a failure for one of them affects the whole community. This is why Brazil will always be back on the investors’ map.

There’s a song from an old movie that says: “from the ashes of disaster grow the roses of success.” We’ve seen it before, and we’ll see it again!
