CMF VI food for thought: Carib-Cap III and other ideas about future microfinance development in the Caribbean

CMF VI food for thought: Carib-Cap III and other ideas about future microfinance development in the Caribbean

Julio 29, 2015

Panelists share lessons learned from Carib-Cap

During the second session of the Caribbean Microfinance Forum VI (CMF VI), one of the panelists, Curven Whyte, Microfinance Coordinator of a major Jamaican credit union, cited the growth in the industry brought about by the Caribbean Microfinance Capacity Building projects (Carib-Cap) I and II and called for a Carib-Cap III. The audience applauded and I couldn’t help but smile because at the CMF last year, the donors—MIF included—had said they would continue supporting microfinance in the Caribbean but not through a regional, multi-donor Carib-Cap III project. Nevertheless, throughout the remainder of the CMF VI, calls for a Carib-Cap III resounded and it became evident that many microfinance stakeholders thought Carib-Cap I and II had accomplished a lot but that need for additional support remained in the Caribbean.

What did Carib Cap I and II accomplish?

Carib-Cap I and II is a partnership among MIF, European Commission, Caribbean Development Bank and Citi Foundation which has provided $5.7 million in technical cooperation funding for the Caribbean. It has given a strong boost to the microfinance industry in the Caribbean and has funded the creation and development of the Caribbean MicroFinance Alliance (CMFA) and the Caribbean Microfinance Forum (CMF). Given the nascent stage of microfinance in many Caribbean countries, the two phases of Carib-Cap provided direct capacity-building to microfinance institutions, some of which did not have existing microcredit portfolios. In addition to providing financial education to microentrepreneurs, microfinance institution (MFI) training and upgrades to the Management Information Systems (MIS) of participating institutions, the project also commissioned studies to provide a much needed picture of the dynamics of the industry. 

Considerations for future interventions

Carib-Cap has shown – and this is supported by the findings of the studies – that future assistance needs to take a more nuanced approach, where support is tailored to the characteristics of individual markets and microfinance ecosystems in the region. While opportunities for collaboration still exist to support the sector, after the evolution brought about by Carib-Cap, it does not seem optimal to embark on a region-wide project that provides direct capacity-building to more than a dozen MFIs. As expected, institutions in Carib-Cap demonstrated different levels of success under the project, and any future intervention will have to consider whether to support only the “stars,” or whether to include institutions that have less-established but promising microfinance operations.  As intended, the CMFA has been strengthened through Carib-Cap and  now has the capability to assume project management responsibilities for new interventions.
 

CMF VI: Opportunities for support and growth

That said, while Carib-Cap III, in the sense of a multi-donor regional capacity-building project may not be a good fit right now, the CMF VI brought to light the following potential opportunities for microfinance support and growth in the Caribbean:
 

  • Support the CMFA to continue gathering data on the industry, providing trainings on responsible microfinance (SMART Campaign, Universal Standards), financial education, regional coordination and advocacy,  and organization of the CMF.
  • Support the CMF as a gathering place for microfinance stakeholders to network and discuss opportunities for growth and collaboration.
  • Upgrade the Management Information Systems at MFIs to better understand clients.
  • Coordinate with diaspora organizations that can provide support to the Caribbean and its financial institutions, including provision of remittances services.
  • Design and expand financial services for rural enterprises.
  • Develop financial services agent networks. 
  • Design and expand financial services offered to youth enterprises.


Some of these opportunities do indeed provide room for multi-stakeholder partnerships, especially in conjunction with the CMFA as executing agency. The CMFA did an excellent job hosting the CMF VI and has proven itself to be a key player in the region for collecting data, influencing public policy and guiding strategic vision for microfinance services in the Caribbean. Additionally, there are opportunities to support individual financial institutions with targeted interventions, which can enhance regional microfinance development by tracking results and sharing lessons learned. Both the CMF and Carib-Cap have offered support, data and analysis, moving along the conversation so that we can now discuss new areas for support and development. What priorities do you now see for microfinance development in the Caribbean?
