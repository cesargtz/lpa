A Glance Backward to 2013

A Glance Backward to 2013

Febrero 03, 2014
By
Anonym

2013 was a pivotal year for the MIF—the end of our first 20 years and the beginning of discussion with our Donors and partners on the vision and funding for our next 10 years.  Over two decades, the MIF has adapted itself to respond to the evolving needs of the region--from its initial focus on private sector policy reform in the 90s, to poverty reduction in the 2000s, to our current emphasis on promoting economic mobility for the poor and vulnerable—still two thirds of the population in the region.

Earlier this month, our team collectively took stock of progress in 2013, and I thought I would share with you some highlights in six areas:  (1) access to finance, (2) access to basic services and green growth, (3) access to markets and skills, (4) women’s economic empowerment, (5) development effectiveness, and (6) knowledge sharing.  Many of you reading this are our partners in this work—in financing, in execution, in analysis, and in intellectual inspiration.  Our private and public partners number over a thousand so you are too numerous to thank here.  But please know that all of us at the MIF understand how fundamentally many of these achievements depend on you. 

Access to Finance

  • Microfinance. Our concerted efforts to boost the quality, effectiveness, and knowledge gains from our longstanding microfinance work paid off last year as CGAP awarded the MIF its highest SmartAid score ever given to a development finance institution. 
  • Financial inclusion. We took a big step forward in our ambitious work plan with others to test innovations that provide the poor and vulnerable with a broader range of financial products—particularly savings—and a broader range of options for accessing services (mobile phone, bank agents, etc.). To date, 26 of 42 planned projects have been launched.
  • SME lending.  We are encouraged by progress in cracking one of the most difficult nuts in small and medium enterprises finance—the missing middle, firms at the small end of the SME spectrum too large for microfinance and too small for most banks. Our partnership with the IDB’s Structured and Corporate Finance department has helped incentivize 10 banks in LAC to try the transformative psychometric testing credit scoring model to scale finance for these clients.
  • Equity in venture capital and microfinance.  Consistent with our Donors’ strong support for this part of our work, we boosted MIF equity investments to $28 million in 2013, leveraging $147 million from other investors. The outstanding portfolio of MIF active equity commitments reached $286 million.

Access to Basic Services and Green Growth

  • Global Environmental Facility.  Based on broadly shared interests, the MIF partnership with GEF  is growing rapidly, with $30 million committed from GEF for equity investments and $21 million for renewable energy projects for poor communities.
  • PROADAPT. The Nordic Development Fund’s generous $5.1 million commitment is funding the launch of PROADAPT, the first ever fund targeting community and private sector interventions to help small firms, farmers, and fishermen with climate resilience.
  • Climate Investment Funds.  We advanced our goal of helping to channel some of these funds to small actors in the private sector. The MIF was entrusted with $3.5 million to implement the world’s first ever private sector Forest Investment Programs in Mexico. A further $25 million from the Scaling-Up Renewable Energy in Low-Income Countries Program (SREP) to catalyze private investment in small-scale renewable energy was given provisional approval.
  • Climatescope. In the second year of this unique scoring tool for the clean energy investment environment in countries in the region, it is already being globalized.  The MIF worked with DFID and USAID to secure funding to bring it to 29 countries in Asia and Africa in 2014

Access to Markets and Skills

  • New Employment Opportunities (NEO). To date, three projects (Brazil, Mexico, Dominican Republic) have been approved under the 10 year NEO public-private partnership to train 1 million youth and employ at least 50%.  So far, 264,000 youth are to be trained, 26 % of the target.
  • Regional Initiative for Inclusive Recycling. To date, all 11 of the projects under the IRR to link informal recyclers to formal recycling markets have been approved, with 16,600 recyclers being trained, exceeding the target.
  • Youth Entrepreneurship Program (YEP). We were excited to launch this $32 million program with the globally recognized Youth Business International to develop more effective models for youth entrepreneurship, with $19.6 million to be mobilized from partners.  

Women’s Economic Empowerment Initiative

  • WEVentureScope. With the help of the Economist Intelligence Unit, the MIF developed this country scoring tool, the first ever ranking of 20 countries in the region based on objective indicators of their environments for women entrepreneurs. Chile, Peru, Colombia, Mexico, and Uruguay topped the list.
  • women entrepreneurshipBanking (weB). We are making real progress in helping banks target women SME owners as clients. To date, six projects have been approved under weB, the joint program with SCF that helps banks devise and fund the right strategies, lending methodologies, and loan portfolio.
  • WeXchange. The MIF hosted this first ever, highly successful pitching conference exclusively for the region’s women entrepreneurs seeking venture capital in December in Miami.

Development Effectiveness 

  • New Corporate Results Framework. Based on extensive consultations with experts and review of best practice, the MIF developed a new, state-of-the-art corporate results framework to make it possible for us to aggregate outputs and outcomes across our 80-90 projects per year, better assess systemic impact, and ensure alignment with strategic priorities. 
  • Poverty Focus. As measured using the MIF’s Quality for Effectiveness in Development (QED) tool, we are focusing our projects on the right beneficiaries. 80% of MIF projects approved in 2013 targeted both the poor and vulnerable; 41% targeted mostly the poor.
  • Gender Focus. We achieved our goal of incorporating gender analysis into 100% of projects.  All staff/consultants trained in integrating gender into all phases of the project cycle, and relevant results and impact indicators are sex-disaggregated. 72 % of 2013 projects targeted, or incorporated special interventions for, women.

Knowledge

  • NEXSO. We were proud to launch the beta version of NEXSO, an innovative online platform to crowdsource development solutions and link solutions seekers with solution providers. The platform so far contains 152 solutions from 34 countries.  Watch for more Challenges to be launched this year to continue to populate the platform.  
  • Knowledge Transfer Program. We financed the path-breaking Knowledge Transfer Program which transferred knowledge gained from MIF projects to 17 countries in the region and 44 institutions.  Based on the demonstrated cost effectiveness of this mode of knowledge sharing, we intend to expand it significantly going forward. 
  • Landscape Studies in Microinsurance and Informal Recycling Markets. This was a year of firsts in landscape studies. The MIF published the first landscape study on microinsurance in the region with the Microinsurance Centre, the first regional landscape study on informal recycling markets, and the first operational manual designed to help formalize and better manage recyclers’ cooperatives.
  • Foromic. The MIF held the largest ever Foromic conference in Guadalajara, Mexico, with over 2,000 attendees, focusing on the theme of “The productivity challenge:  Financing dynamic entrepreneurship.”
  • PPPAmericas. We set an attendance record at the PPPAmericas conference in Cartagena, Colombia with 500 people from 27 countries discussing social and green PPPs, PPPs in smaller economies, and subnational PPPs.
