LINGOs Trains MIF’s Haitian partners in capacity building: A Success Story

LINGOs Trains MIF’s Haitian partners in capacity building: A Success Story

Febrero 21, 2013
By
Nara Meli

As readers of this blog, you may have heard that the MIF provided assistance to its partners immediately following the January 12, 2010 earthquake. A grant of $3 million was distributed among 16 different institutions, all good performers, and with whom MIF had previously worked. What you may not know, is that as part of this emergency assistance, a 17th grant was given to LINGOs, which is a consortium of over 75 international humanitarian relief, development, conservation and health organizations. The purpose of LINGOs is to enable people working in development access learning at little or no cost. This includes project management training.

Why did MIF choose to finance this last grant? You guessed it: Capacity building. With the daily challenges the institutions faced, plus the influx of new funds to recover their capacity lost in the earthquake, the training came at a crucial time to boost the effectiveness of project management for our partners. And so in August 2010, the MIF partnered with LINGOs to conduct a project management capacity building project, to increase the developmental impact of all resources.

This is not as easy as it sounds. To train Haitian project managers, you need a Haitian-Creole-speaking trainer. LINGOs worked with four organizations to identify and develop the best long-term local partner to conduct Project Management training. There were a total of 5 face-to-face workshops, which led to face-to-face training for 120 Haitian project managers. After each workshop, participants were able to participate in individual coaching sessions with trainers. A total of 4 sets of 10 hours of coaching were performed. 40 participants regularly took part in these sessions.

An online portal was also developed for Haiti, in French and was populated with self-instructional management and project management courses to expand the skills covered in the face-to-face trainings. All learning materials including the PMD Pro Guide, the PMD Pro Syllabus, the classroom materials, the Sample examination and the two certification examinations were translated into French. PMD Pro stands for project management in development for professionals, and includes two levels of courses that cover the basic concepts, terminology, tools, and techniques of project management used by NGOs. A third level is currently being developed.

The project was, by a large account, successful – as can be seen in this video.  Participants felt they learned how to use important tools for project management, and that the training applied to their daily work. Not only that, but from our point of view working with the participants, MIF Senior Specialist Jempsy Fils-Aimé says:  “I can see a big difference between the Haitian implementing agencies that have been through the training and those that haven't.”

 However, several participants also noted that they found it difficult to apply their knowledge within organizations, which were not interested in changing. Indeed, for institutional capacity trainings such as this one to be successful, they require a commitment from the organization to make the appropriate changes, and prioritize the use of the new tools acquired. Without a clear commitment from the organization, implementation of the training results will be impossible. One of the lessons learned for the future, is therefore to secure this buy-in from the participating organization from the beginning, to ensure the uptake and the maximum impact of the capacity building.  

There is no doubt that further good results will come from this initiative. The project is now sustainable: the local facilitator that helped give the trainings for the project is now conducting PMDPro trainings in Haiti. This means more institutions will benefit from this knowledge and be better equipped with project management tools and methods, which will lead to better effectiveness of projects in the short and long term. “This is fantastic”, says LINGOs Director John Cropper. I can’t help but agree.
