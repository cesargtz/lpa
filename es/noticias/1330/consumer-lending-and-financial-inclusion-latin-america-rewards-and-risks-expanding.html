Consumer lending and financial inclusion in Latin America: Rewards and risks of expanding access

Consumer lending and financial inclusion in Latin America: Rewards and risks of expanding access

Abril 15, 2013
By
Sergio Navajas

Yolanda Luna, Mexico 2012 CGAP Photo Contest Cross-posted from CGAP's blog. By Xavier Faz (CGAP), Rudy Araujo (ASBA), Sergio Navajas (MIF)

On March 12 and 13, CGAP, the MIF, and the Association of Bank Supervisors of the Americas (ASBA) hosted a discussion with regulators in Latin America on consumer lending in the Americas, including participants from Brazil, Chile, Colombia, México, Perú and the US.

Consumer lending has evolved in different ways across markets, and has grown rapidly in recent years both in market size and outreach to low-income consumers and the growing middle class. Policymakers in the region have identified important risks resulting from this market evolution, but there are still not clear consensus views on how to measure and assess market penetration, how to separate the good aspects of it from what is potentially harmful, and the role of the regulators towards proactively controlling or reactively responding to the market.

Some of the highlights of the discussion were:

  • Most Latin American markets don't regulate many credit-only institutions since they do not intermediate people's savings and therefore are not considered subject to prudential regulation.
  • Because access to credit is highly constrained, there is significant pent-up demand for credit in middle and low income populations. In most cases, this is traditionally known as "consumer" credit but the kinds of uses are broader (i.e. cars, durable household goods).
  • The demand for credit has further increased given that most Latin American countries have enjoyed sustained economic growth over the past several years, leading to less poor and a growing middle class.
  • As a result, unregulated credit institutions as well as big-brand retailers have amassed large portfolios of unregulated credit. Chile estimates that nearly half of consumer credit in the market comes from unregulated institutions, while Brazil estimates this to be around 30% and Mexico 20%.
  • Because there are no rules for how these actors assess credit-worthiness and provide loans, aggressive credit practices are often posing other market risks (financial sector stability, economic shocks and consumer over-indebtedness).
  • At the same time, the interest in advancing financial inclusion is driving reforms that allow new non-bank players to take on roles along the financial services value chain. As a result, many mobile banking initiatives of different sorts are being launched in most markets, in most cases around mobile payments and mobile accounts. The international experience shows that it is likely that these business models eventually expand into credit.
  • Consumer protection has been a foremost priority in many markets, but as recent research shows, the traditional views on transparency as the key ingredient for sound choices in lending products are changing, since other factors seem to drive consumer behavior besides pure cost of credit and ability to compare offers. Moreover, mystery shopping studies have demonstrated that the implementation of transparency norms in practice deviates significantly and is often ineffective in getting consumers the information they need to make informed choices, or controlling for deceptive sales practices and marketing.

 

To address these issues in consumer lending, several policy approaches were discussed that could hold potential for markets across the region:

1.     Expand the scope of regulation in order to bring into the financial sector authority purview the provision of credit across all segments of population and provider types. This should help regulators achieve a full view of the market and assess changes in the offering and use of credit that might entail risks, and avoid the regulatory arbitrage that currently exists in many of these markets.

2.     Regulate use of credit bureaus to ensure a more complete and shared view of credit risk at the household level by promoting broader inclusion of institutions in the bureaus and of mandatory information-sharing across providers and bureaus. There are open questions around whether bureaus should be public or private, whether one or more should exist, the governance aspects and pricing. But the consensus is that providers in general should assess risk based on consultation to credit bureaus.

3.     Identify the data models needed to track healthy lending development. One of the common challenges heard by participants was “we don’t have the full market picture nor the data to understand all the actors providing consumer credit.” Improving the breadth and quality of information on consumer credit in a market should help identify what providers are offering what kind of products, to what consumer segments, and the performance and health of their consumer credit portfolios.

4.     Expand understanding of best practices in regulating consumer lending. Different countries have exercised different approaches to controlling consumer credit expansion and monitoring individual providers’ portfolios and lending practices, with many successful cases across the region. It would therefore be useful to have a shared understanding of what rules, policies, and market monitoring tools work best in regulating consumer lending.

5.     Understand how consumer and behavioral insights might change existing consumer protection guidelines and norms by unlocking how consumers think about consumer credit, and what leads some consumers to manage their debt responsibly, while others end up in over their heads.

6.     Down the road, as business models evolve, learn more about how innovations in branchless banking may expand access to credit and promote an early assessment of how this might change existing risks or bring new ones.
