Microfranchises: How to get buy in?

Microfranchises: How to get buy in?

Marzo 04, 2012
By
César Buenadicha

This post was prepared by Jason Fairbourne, of Fairbourne Consulting who are supporting the MIF's microfranchising project in Jamaica. In this post he shares their experiences with the project.

 
We at Fairbourne Consulting recently completed our third trip to the beautiful Caribbean nation of Jamaica the first week of February. We were hired to analyze the Kingston, Jamaica market and create sustainable microfranchises for 100 at-risk youth in poor inner-city communities.
 
In the weeks leading up to this on-site visit we had detected the private sector partners were beginning to lose momentum. We determined the problem was that we had reached a point in the project where the real commitment began and we had little personal time with them. It is one thing for a company to entertain the idea of increasing profits through market expansion by utilizing the vastly undervalued workforce that exists at the BoP, after all more profits sounds great and helping inner-city youth is a good story. However, it becomes real when these potential microfranchisors are asked to commit time and resources to build out a program requiring them to take on the risk of purchasing assets (vendor carts, bicycles, etc.), possibly hiring additional staff, and essentially taking a step into the unknown.
 
Fairbourne Consulting in conjunction with the Youth Upliftment Through Employment (YUTE) program came to the conclusion that until our potential microfranchisors took ownership in designing the models they would be apprehensive in putting too much skin in the game.
 
So what were the obstacles? What questions could we answer to win their buy in? What steps could we take to put this project back on the fast track?
  • First: face-to-face meetings with the key decision makers from each of the potential partnering organizations were vital. Much of the communication up to this point had been through email correspondence or meetings/calls with intermediary sources. Each partner allocated four hours with key decision makers and we were able to get everyone on the same page.
  • Second: there existed a lack of familiarity with Fairbourne Consulting and our Lean Start-up processes and methodologies which are backward from the traditional school of thought. Traditional thought would say one should Learn, Measure, and then Build as oppose to what we have found more successful in Building, Measuring, Learning, and then making adjustments before jumping into the loop again.
  • Third: we sensed that perhaps the reason there was a lack of ownership or “buy-in” to the current plan was because these major decision makers had had very little input in the creation process. We had internally built models and processes that we believed could work based off of our extensive knowledge of BoP markets combined with our findings in the Kingston market. To garner more confidence in the project we would take our potential partners through the same process we had internally performed.
The results were an added excitement, model ownership, and understanding of how the project could successfully be accomplished. Not only were the microfranchisors more confident in offering their time and resources to the project, but they were also able to add value to the overall design and implementation plan. The key will be to ride this momentum and maintain the communication and collaboration that is fueling this project’s success.
 
As a business innovation firm Fairbourne Consulting must often find the delicate balance of being flexible enough to adapt to market trends while also minding the boundaries and parameters that inherently exist within the nonprofit space. For-profit models as a development tool is new to most NGOs and thus we are constantly learning from each project how to best navigate this space.
