Engaging the Haitian private sector in climate change resilience: Why communication matters

Engaging the Haitian private sector in climate change resilience: Why communication matters

Julio 19, 2013
By
Jempsy Fils-Aimé

 

 

Last month, I participated in a Climate Change (CC) mission organized by the IDB’s Climate Change and Sustainability Division and the MIF. Our mission’s objective was to initiate a proactive discussion with the local private sector to explore options for their participation in two adaptation programs:

  1. The climate resilience concessional loans pilot program of the Climate Investment Funds (CIFs); and
  2. The MIF’s PRODAPT facility.

Fruitful dialogues were held with Haiti’s Comité Interministériel d’Aménagement du Territoire (CIAT) on the possibility of developing private sector proposals for both programs, with technical support from the Climate Change/Sustainability Division and the MIF. As a first step, the parties agreed that to be successful, the project proposals must align with the Haitian government’s recently developed Strategic Program for Climate Resilience (SPCR).

Adaptation measures in the construction sector in Haiti: A house more resistant to disasters associated with climate change.

Meetings were held with private sector representatives from several institutions (enterprises, NGOs, chambers of commerce associations) that have shown some interest in developing adaptation and mitigation projects. In addition, a site visit was made to the Côte des Arcadins, located approximately 75 km north of Port-au-Prince, to meet with hotels in the area. Overall, the institutional representatives expressed concerns about climate change and requested more information. Hotel managers described similar threats to their infrastructure and the tourism sector in the face of vulnerabilities to climate change. They identified energy and water management as especially vulnerable features. The hotels visited have been self-generating most of their electricity, while their water resources come from a limited supply. Furthermore, protection of the coral reefs and reforestation of the Cotes des Arcadins watershed were identified as urgently needed interventions.

While the mission lasted only two days, it was very instructive. It generated a lot of knowledge about the views of climate change stakeholders with different backgrounds, and brought to light their ideas for better managing related threats that affect their livelihoods as well as three important economic sectors in Haiti – construction, agriculture and tourism. Although the mission focused mainly on adaptation, the people we met claimed that mitigation should be also considered, due to the advanced state of degradation of the environment. Environmental degradation is one of the most important issues affecting Haiti’s development. According to UNDP, the forest cover shifted from 20% in 1980 to 2% in 2011.

Personally, I noticed four important realities during the mission that should be taken into account when communicating to private sector stakeholders about climate change.

First, communication should not be technical, and should be well directed. It is critical not to assume that people can understand climate change easily. During the mission, while there was an enthusiasm for collaborating with the IDB Group to address climate change, it seemed that the private sector did not yet understand the phenomenon and was not aware of how to implement such programs. We needed to “adapt” the language we used to include private sector-oriented jargon to make concepts more tangible. In business, facts are often expressed in mathematical fashion. Quantification matters. As one hotel manager said, “It is not easy to understand climate change. People have been talking for decades about it, but it is still challenging to grasp the concept.”

Second, to engage the private sector, it is imperative to display that climate change has an impact on private sector businesses. In fact, some professionals reason that businesspeople feel the impacts that climate change has on their assets. Indeed, businesspeople think in terms of cost-benefit. In the entrepreneur mindset, it is important to be able to react quickly to a real challenge and turn it into an opportunity that generates money in the short, medium or in a very manageable long run. Decisions are based mainly on current facts, while climate change is abstract, and its measurable impact is often calculated toward a long-run basis. In a developing country like Haiti, where everything is a priority, people tend to consider the short term more decisively than the long term. Even some development practitioners conceive climate change just as one more topic to add to the country’s “problem tree,” rather than a sustainable lens through which all topics should be looked at in order to develop development strategies that are viable in the short, medium and long term. Dominique Carvonis, an active businesswoman in the tourism industry and advisor for several hotels, said that conveying the right message about climate change is critical. Entrepreneurs need to understand the concrete impacts that the phenomenon has on their business’s bottom line, she said. This is why it is essential to conduct more research to demonstrate how it is impacting private sector assets.

Example of Adaptation measure in the agriculture sector: Irrigation efficiency

My third point is related to incentives. While business people might understand climate change and are aware of its potential impacts, it seems that they need appealing incentives, some catalytic push to adopt climate change strategies. According to the business stakeholders we met, government or the international community should promote the adoption of sustainable and affordable strategies as a first step. One way to do so is to provide comprehensive technical assistance. In fact, the expertise for dealing with climate change may not be readily available in the market. For example, it might be difficult to find an expert who can conduct a climate change diagnostic analysis of hotels in Haiti. Additionally, hotel owners do not have expertise to design terms of reference and monitor an expert assignment. Providing concessional loans to implement expert recommendations could prove another incentive for private-sector climate change mitigation efforts. In regard to these strategies, the MIF’s PROADAPT and the CIF concessional loans programs are very relevant.

The final and most significant result from our private sector meetings is the need to raise awareness more broadly. Indeed, the multiple meetings and conversations that we had during the mission have convinced me that the implementation of a comprehensive climate change management program should start with a series of awareness raising activities. However, it is critical that they are carried out, contrary to the traditional development approach, in a top-down custom to ensure their effective and sustainable implementation. I propose this approach because many in leadership positions in the private sector who could pilot climate change resilience strategies are neither knowledgeable about climate change, nor convinced that it exists. If they need to implement such strategies, it is sine qua non for them to be adequately aware of climate change. As mission leaders Gerard Alleng and Steve Wilson said: We need to ensure that on all levels the right knowledge is communicated correctly, so that everyone can be a partner and can act to promote climate change resilience.
