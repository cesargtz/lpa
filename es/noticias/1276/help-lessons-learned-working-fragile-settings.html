HELP: Lessons learned from working in fragile settings

HELP: Lessons learned from working in fragile settings

By
Nara Meli

The MIF recently hosted two workshops, one in Washington and another in Port-au-Prince  (as part of a microfinance seminar), to discuss the lessons learned from the Haitian Emergency Liquidity Program (HELP), a $2.1 million dollar program designed to buy earthquake-affected loans from microfinance institutions, providing them fresh funds to maintain their lending operations and preserve their capital base.  MIF and other funders conceived the project in the aftermath of the 2010 earthquake, to provide support to microfinance institutions and their clients. Omtrix implemented HELP and the Emergency Liquidity Fund (ELF).  ELF provides liquidity and risk management training to prequalified MFIs and private investors in case of disasters. Omtrix is a fund manager and financial consulting firm that focuses on the microfinance sector.  

HELP closed in December 2012, and the workshop presented the results of the external evaluation, which was conducted by Terracor Financial Services.

The goal of HELP was facilitate the access of microenterprises and low-income people to credit and financial services, by supporting the expansion of the local microfinance sector. This support to microfinance institutions (three received support from the program, serving some 14,300 micro entrepreneurs) would mitigate the impact of the earthquake and contribute to the recovery efforts. In light of the earthquake, HELP would help the microfinance institutions restore the working capital of their low-income clients, and address the deterioration of their loan portfolios.

Here are the lessons learned outlined in the evaluation report. While they were specifically drawn from the HELP project, they can also be adapted to other projects designed in post-disaster contexts.  I’ve altered the language a bit.

 

  1. Act quickly. Right after a disaster, “early money is far more valuable than late money, so move with agility” said Ira Lieberman, the Chairman of the Board of ELF.
  2. Be there: Involve and work with the partner on the ground to ensure quick action.
  3. Simple design: the simpler it is, the quicker the uptake and implementation. Also, simplify legal requirements and procedures.
  4. Think prevention: identify and pre-qualify beneficiaries before emergencies, not after (longer, more difficult, and costlier).
  5. Pick adequate criteria: criteria for assistance should be appropriate to the country and to the situation.
  6. Provide technical assistance, and build capacity along the way.
  7. Think of what’s next? As you design the intervention, think about mechanisms to make it self-sustaining or to implement a “phase-out” approach.
  8. Be flexible. Rules, information requirements, measurements may need changing along the way to adapt to a crisis situation. “You do not need 50 metrics to track impact” says Caroline Bressan, Investment Officer, Calvert Foundation.

I also gleaned a few more from the presentations in the workshop itself:

Be Proactive: In terms of prevention, also, prepare your instruments/tools/methods before-hand, so that they are ready when disaster strikes (and you aren’t left trying to design a whole new operation during a crisis).

Mitigate moral hazard: (ie. Risky behavior arising from not incurring the cost of consequences) by continuing to raise awareness among beneficiaries, and setting up compliance mechanisms.

Build strong relationships with the partners. Establish trust through regular communication.

Canvass: Draw on sector assessments or market studies to learn about the context of the country so that you know the lay of the land when disaster strikes.

So, what about you? What has been your experience in designing projects in or after a major disaster? How do you design successful projects in fragile settings? What are the greatest challenges, and how did you go about facing them? What are some of the lessons you learned?
