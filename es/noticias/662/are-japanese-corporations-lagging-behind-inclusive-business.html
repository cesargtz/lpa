Are Japanese corporations lagging behind in inclusive business?

Are Japanese corporations lagging behind in inclusive business?

Marzo 23, 2015
By
Masato Okumura

“Why are Japanese corporations behind in inclusive business? Honestly, I am not sure if the question is valid in the first place.”

This was a comment made by a panelist during a February workshop about inclusive business, held in Tokyo and co-organized by the MIF and Japan International Cooperation Agency (JICA). It was the first joint MIF-JICA event, and the venue was filled with 50 people from both the private and public sectors, reflecting a high level of interest by Japanese corporations in inclusive business in Latin America and the Caribbean. To our great surprise, during the workshop, many questions were raised by the participants. As you may know, this rarely happens in Japan, where many people still live with a “silence is golden” mindset. This active interaction was another indication of high and deep levels of interest from the participants. 

The original issue prepared by us to be addressed in the workshop was “Why are Japanese corporations behind Western ones?” In his opening remarks, Toru Shikibu, the representative of the IDB’s Asia Office, pointed out that there is a large space for Japanese corporations to explore and set out the main question for the workshop: “What needs to be done to move forward?” 

What comes to your mind when you hear the phrase, “Japanese corporations actively engaged in inclusive business?” I assume that many of you would say, “Sorry, nothing…” It is natural, since in many of the books (at least in English) about inclusive business, you can find many examples of Western corporations, such as DANONE, Coca-Cola and SAP. But very few Japanese ones are there.

The Japanese way of inclusive business

Then came the comment I shared earlier on the validity of the question itself. The commenter continued, “On the contrary, we can be proud of what Japanese corporations have been doing.” His argument was based on two reasons. Firstly, there are a lot of Japanese corporations engaging in exciting inclusive business projects, mainly in Asia and Africa. The leading players include Yakult, Sumitomo Chemical, Euglena, and Yamaha Motors. In fact, the MIF is working with Yamaha on a microfranchising project for mechanics who repair engines in remote areas of Suriname. Many have developed inclusive business on their own. In addition, since 2010, JICA has supported more than 90 feasibility studies for “base of the pyramid (BOP)” business all over the world, and is helping many of them actually launch businesses in a profitable way. 

The second point of his argument is that Japanese companies embrace a longstanding culture of inclusive business, which is vividly illustrated in a well-known saying of the traditional Japanese commercial business world, “San-po-yoshi,” which means “Satisfaction of all three parts; vendors, buyers, and society.” This concept is deeply engraved in the missions of many Japanese corporations, so their businesses naturally include aspects of Inclusive Business. The mission of Panasonic, for example, “to contribute to the progress and development of society and the well-being of people worldwide,” was stated by the founder Konosuke Matsushita in 1929, long before terms such as “corporate social responsibility” were born.

Now, there seems to be some truth in his argument. So why haven’t Japanese companies’ contributions been recognized widely enough?


One answer could be that Japanese companies are not good at promoting their achievements. Or that they haven’t thought to do so, since “inclusive business or corporate social value is nothing special to them,” in the words of my colleague Nobu Otsuka, a MIF specialist who participated in the workshop as a panelist. The second possible answer is that Japanese companies are actually behind in terms of scaling through partnering with NGOs and foundations with which they might be able to leverage their own resources. Japanese companies may have to work on both these aspects to compete globally.

It is true that Japanese companies currently have little presence in the context of inclusive business in Latin America and the Caribbean. But now that we know there is strong interest, we at the MIF can work on changing that. Our project with Yamaha shows there are opportunities and that there is much that Japan can offer the region—not only funds and technologies, but also the concepts and business models of Japanese-style inclusive business.
