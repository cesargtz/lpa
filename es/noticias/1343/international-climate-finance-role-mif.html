International climate finance: the role of the MIF

International climate finance: the role of the MIF

Mayo 02, 2013
By
Filippo Berardi

The Copenhagen and Cancun accords of 2009-10 represent key milestones in the international discussion on climate finance, as they set quantitative targets for the volume of financial resources developed countries will need to make available to fund the needed shift to a lower carbon economy. With those agreements, developed countries jointly committed to mobilize $30 bn in ‘fast-start’ finance for the three year period 2010 to 2012, and $100 bn/year thereafter, to finance climate mitigation and adaptation actions in developing countries. With the period to pledge fast-start finance now closed, and as advanced economies start focusing on how to mobilize $100 bn/year by 2020, we at the MIF have been working to ensure that finite resources are spent in an effective, yet timely, manner.

Urgent action is needed to transform the way both developed and middle income developing countries fuel their growth. Investments done today in more carbon-intensive energy generation units will lock societies into high-carbon emission paths for the next 25-40 years. In fact, the latest data on carbon emissions continue to show that we are not departing from the high end trajectory of emission scenarios. If we are to limit the global warming below 2o Celsius over pre-industrial levels, the International Energy Agency (IEA) estimates that investments at a massive scale are required over the next few years. In particular, the IEA projects that the incremental investment in the energy sector will need to reach $36 trillion over the period of 2012-2050, i.e. approximately $1 trillion each year up to mid-century.

A first step to understand what that means in practice is to look at current scale and trends of climate finance. Recent research indicates a wide range of estimates. This is in part due to the lack of a generally accepted definition of climate finance and what this concept should include. The Climate Policy Initiative’s Landscape of Climate Finance 2012, estimates the level of financing available to the transformation of the energy systems at around $360 bn in 2010-2011. The figure includes investments from the private sector (65%), public sources (5%) and financial intermediaries, including national and multilateral development banks (30%). However, given the uncertainties about how to estimate the incremental costs of cleaner technologies over more polluting ones, the methodology used by the CPI to build the $360 bn figure considers the overall capital costs of green assets, and does not clarify the magnitude of net climate finance flows. OECD figures estimate the size of current flows of climate finance at $70-120 bn year. In both cases, comparing such figures with the IEA forecasted $1 trillion/year in incremental costs - rather than net climate-related costs - the magnitude of the challenge to scale-up becomes evident.

The bulk of the climate financing has come from the private sector, mostly from developed countries. The public sector has a role to play as a catalyst to leverage additional private sector resources and to create an environment conducive for private investments. Financial intermediaries and development banks should continue to play a pivotal role in channeling resources on the ground as well as leveraging additional lower-cost financing through their high credit rating. Within this context, the MIF has been an early mover, acting as IDB Group private sector implementing agency for the Climate Investment Funds (CIFs). The recent approval of the first ever private sector operation of the CIFs’ Forest Investment Program (FIP), which is the result of a fruitful collaboration with the IDB’ Climate Change and Sustainability division, is a step in this direction. The $3.5 million grant will contribute to climate change mitigation by improving the social and financial sustainability of 60 Mexican community forest enterprises (CFEs) that work to preserve the natural capital of forest lands. In addition to an expected 100,000+ tCO2e reduction in GHG emissions, the operation will increase the profit of the beneficiaries CFEs and providing income to an estimated 4,900 women, men and children living in ejidos and other rural communities in the states of Oaxaca, Yucatan, Quintana Roo, Jalisco and Campeche.

As the importance of ensuring scale, timeliness and effectiveness of international climate finance remains high on governments’ agendas, the MIF will continue to play a key role in channeling resources on the ground as quickly and effectively as possible. Following the approval of the FIP operation, the next initiative on the MIF pipeline will be an Energy Efficiency Fund, the first ever in Colombia, specifically intended to increase access to credit for Energy Service Companies (ESCOs), which will be seeded with a combination of MIF, IDB and CIFs’ Clean Technology Fund resources. Designed in close collaboration with other IDB’s units, such as Capital Markets and Structured Corporate Finance, the project will finance capacity development programs for stakeholders involved in the assessment, design and financing of energy efficiency projects, allowing them to access the financing options available through the Fund and positioning them as relevant market players in the nascent energy efficiency market.
