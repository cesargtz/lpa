The MIF in Haiti: Five Years Post-Quake

The MIF in Haiti: Five Years Post-Quake

Enero 12, 2015
By
Guest

By the MIF Haiti Team

Today marks the fifth anniversary of the devastating earthquake that shook Haiti and its partners in an unprecedented way. We’re taking the opportunity reflect on the work the MIF has done in Haiti since that day, and looking ahead, we are thinking about how we can better respond to the country’s needs.

The MIF has been active in Haiti since 1995 and there were a number of active projects at the time of the earthquake. We immediately provided support to our current partners, and then focused on contributing to rebuilding the Haitian economy as a whole. 

2010: Emergency Assistance 

Haiti Emergency Spending Allocation Request (HESAR): The MIF quickly created a US$3 million budget line to provide fast-track financial support to 16 MIF partners. These local partners served a total of over 300,000 clients and beneficiaries. HESAR also leveraged an additional US$2.2 million from other organizations and stakeholders. 
 



Credit for housing: We provided US$1.6 million to Le Levier, a major local credit union federation, which was able to provide housing credit to over 154 households.

“Learning by doing”: Through a partnership with Habitat for Humanity, we supported the affected population in Santo-Leogane with shelter and construction assistance so that families could reconstruct their own homes and acquire new income-generating skills. Over 600 families received training, 400 people were hired in construction, 194 construction workers gained specialized skills through training and supervision, and 455 households have rebuilt their own homes. 

Long-term growth with mangoes: Haiti Hope, a five-year, US$9.5 million public-private partnership between the MIF-IDB, the Coca-Cola Company, USAID and TechnoServe, was designed to create sustainable economic opportunities for Haitian mango farmers. To date, more than 21,000 farmers have been trained in agricultural and business skills. Over 229 participating producer business groups generated over US$425,000 in revenue in 2014 alone. 

2011: Rebuilding the Economy

Remittances for sustainable energy:  An innovative project demonstrated the potential of using remittances to finance small-scale clean energy products. More than 15,500 solar lamps were sold through a Miami-based remittance platform and at pick-up locations in Haiti. A second phase is now being contemplated, which will expand a nascent street agent network launched in the project. 

Youth employment skills: 4,620 people between the ages of 16 and 26 are increasing their livelihood skills and opportunities for employment and self–employment through training in financial education, entrepreneurship, workforce readiness, and leadership. In an environment where jobs creation is very difficult, this project has placed nearly 40% of its trained beneficiaries.

Access to higher education: To date, this project has recruited and signed Alumni Contribution Model contracts with 170 talented students who would otherwise not be part of the University system in Haiti. 100% of participating graduates have found employment. The model is still in its early stage, but is very promising, as the graduates are constantly being called by the best employers in the country.

Strengthening the coffee value chain: This project hopes to reintroduce Haitian coffee to the international market, tackling critical problems with production, quality, financing, and the network of cooperatives. So far, the results have exceeded expectations, as the cooperatives have exported 19 containers in 2014 compared to 5-7 in the previous two years.  




2012: Access to Finance for MSMEs and Value Chains

Venture Capital for SMEs: With an investment of US$3 million, we joined the International Finance Corporation (IFC), the Dutch development bank (FMO), and Leopard Capital in launching Leopard Haiti Fund, Haiti's first private equity fund.  Since the launch, LHF has made three investments:

  •  DloHaiti -- a start-up venture aiming to provide safe drinking water through decentralized water production kiosks that provide the purified water at an affordable price.
  • NUtv -- a local provider of television services, which has also received support from Haiti’s largest corporation and telecommunications operator, Digicel, Banque Nationale de Crédit (BNC), and a few local entrepreneurs.
  • Veerhouse Voda -- a manufacturer of disaster-resilient, environmentally sustainable structures using a technology licensed through Dutch company Veerhuis Bouwsystemen B.V. Its prefabricated system can be built five times faster than traditional construction methods.

Equity Investment in ACME: We took a US$1 million minority equity stake in Association pour la Coopération avec la Micro Entreprise (ACME), a key microfinance institution in the country, enabling ACME to expand its financing services to micro and small enterprises.

Psychometric tools for SME finance: In partnership with EFL Global Ltd and Sogesol, the microfinance arm of Sogebank, we are introducing a unique loan evaluation approach that uses psychometric assessments of entrepreneurs’ ability and honesty as a viable, low-cost screening tool. So far, Sogesol has assessed over 4,300 credit applicants and a fully calibrated model is expected in 2016. 

Artisan supply chains: This project seeks to develop a sustainable business platform for Haitian artisans by improving their competitiveness and facilitating their access to finance and markets.

2013: Health, agriculture

Health services: The DASH Carte Avantage model offers basic health care services through a market-based, low-cost prepaid health card. DASH has already sold over 7,000 cards, and by the end of the project expects to sell 100,000.

Cocoa alliances: This project focuses on rehabilitating degraded lands, upgrading quality systems, and strengthening producer groups. New production models, infrastructure, and links to international markets will benefit some 7,000 producers. 

2014:  A new MIF approach in Haiti

Savings for remittance recipients: Our project increases access to and use of savings by Haitian international remittance clients through financial products and services tailored to their needs.

An  Agenda for Change: We spent much of last year developing a new action plan for our future work in Haiti. Aiming to be more strategic in responding to challenges and opportunities, we have consulted with stakeholders, MIF partners, and donors to set our priorities in Haiti for the next five years. In our next blog post, we will share more details about this “Agenda for Change.” 

In the meantime, we look back on the last five years feeling both pride for the breadth of projects and sectors we have been able to support, and humility knowing the scope of the challenges that remain in the complicated and inspirational country of Haiti. We thank all the MIF staff, partner organizations, and project beneficiaries who have been part of this story, and look forward to many more years working together.
