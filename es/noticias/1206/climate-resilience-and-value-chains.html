Climate Resilience and Value Chains

Climate Resilience and Value Chains

December 07, 2012
By
Steve Wilson

Managing climate change risks in the food supply chain. Here, a still buzzing local market

An increasing number of the world’s largest companies are recognizing their vulnerability to climate change and are acting to improve the climate resilience of their firms and value chains. In addition, companies are beginning to see business opportunities related to the need for greater climate resilience.  

A recent publication of the Partnership for Resilience and Environmental Preparedness (PREP), “Value Chain Climate Resilience: A Guide to Managing Climate Impacts in Companies and Communities,” outlines a “value chain approach” to building climate resilience in firms and local communities. The PREP group is comprised of large global companies with extended value chains or with a compelling business interest in value chains, such as the reinsurance industry. Many of the firms in their value chains are small companies. The report was prepared by Acclimatise, Oxfam America, and BSR, on behalf of PREP members.  

A value chain approach to climate resilience recognizes that climate change affects more than a company’s assets, but also impacts local communities and natural environments. Communities provide key resources to companies and a “social license to operate,” while local ecosystems offer flood protection and water treatment, among other ecosystem services. The value chain approach examines climate vulnerabilities and business opportunities from three perspectives: the business environment (access to finance, the policy environment, stakeholder expectations), activities beyond business boundaries (community and ecosystem resilience, raw materials sourcing, distribution, sales), and activities within business boundaries (assets and infrastructure, and production and operations).  

The PREP report provides case studies of firms in the following sectors: food, beverage agriculture, water, energy utilities, and general manufacturing. While about 90% of the companies studied recognized that they faced climate related threats, only 30% are actively responding to these threats. The paper suggests that the gap between problem recognition and response is due in part to a widespread lack of practical climate data to inform business decision, and an overall degree of uncertainty regarding the timing and magnitude of climate impacts.  

However, the report points out that some companies have used the best climate data available to better inform management of likely climate scenarios going forward. Other firms have worked closely with climate research centers to develop higher resolution climate projections that would inform their strategic planning. While uncertainty over the timing and magnitude of climate impacts is a leading reason for inaction, the paper notes that climate risk management is an extension of the risk management models that have been in use for a long time, and can be an important tool in stimulating action – even in the face of uncertainty.  

While many businesses view community resilience as the responsibility of governments, a growing number equate community risks with their own company’s risks, particularly given that communities provide employees and supplies and buy the firm’s products and services. Extreme weather can also create local instability that disrupts business operations. In addition, communities can be a source of reputational risks when companies are seen (rightly or wrongly) to be causing or aggravating climate risks.   

In another recent report, the Carbon Disclosure Project’s “Business Resilience in an uncertain, resource constrained world” 81% of CDP members (almost 700 large organizations) report that they currently identify specific physical risks from climate change. These companies are starting define climate threats in immediate and long-term timeframes as well, with the percentage of companies seeing current physical climate risks jumping from 10% in 2010 to 37% in 2012. A growing number of companies identify benefits from climate resilience that transcend financial returns or savings, with almost seven out of ten companies seeing in climate resilience a source of business opportunities and a way to enhance their company’s reputation.  

These publications stress the growing recognition of companies for the need to increase climate resilience, and the potential business opportunities in this area. Firms with large value chains, in particular, recognize that climate risk is different from conventional disaster risk management, and that communities are a vital part of business value chains.   Most multilateral meetings on adaptation still focus on the role of the public sector, and frame the private sector as a source of finance rather than of innovative solutions. But at the COP 18 climate meetings in Doha the specific issue of business climate resilience is surfacing. Groups such as the Center for Climate and Energy Solutions are hosting side events to discuss specific mechanisms and strategies fostering enterprise resilience. While small in comparison to the large public-oriented discussions on adaptation, current trends will only increase the interest in enterprise resilience and the role of businesses as a source of many new products and services to help households, businesses, and the public sector to become more climate resilient.

These trends will inform much of the work of the Multilateral Investment Fund in climate resilience going forward. We will continue to help small firms and communities to build resilience, to work with anchor firms to raise the resilience of their value chains, and to broker new models to help small enterprises seek business opportunities associated with this threat.
