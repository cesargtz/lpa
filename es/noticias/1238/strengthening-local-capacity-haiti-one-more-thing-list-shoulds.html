Strengthening local capacity in Haiti: one more thing on the list of “shoulds”

Strengthening local capacity in Haiti: one more thing on the list of “shoulds”

Octubre 26, 2012
By
Nara Meli

With the recent uncovering of alleged mismanagement at Yélé Haiti, the need to regulate the NGOs active in Haiti has been the talk of the town. In the view of many, Haiti is a “republic of NGOs,” each with its own mission to fulfill. There have been examples of cooperation (as I pointed out in a previous blog entry), but too often, despite best intentions, an uncoordinated system that disperses aid resources according to institutional development goals prevails.

In the Huffington Post Mark Schuller, an anthropologist working in Haiti and professor of NGO development, lists a number of policy recommendations for international NGOs and other agencies working in Haiti. His key point, in my view, is addressing the need for greater involvement of the local, beneficiary population. More participation will provide more ownership by those benefiting from the project. It also means the local population will have a voice and a stake in the process, and a greater chance for control over what is happening.

I would like to add one more to the list of “shoulds.” We, as international organizations, whether public, private or non-profit, should strive to strengthen our local partners. Low institutional capacity and low financial, business and management skills are an endemic theme when working in Haiti.

The MIF has been supporting and strengthening its local partners since it began working in Haiti, about 20 years ago. How do we go about this?

One way to pass on technical expertise, project experience and knowledge directly is to hire local consultants to work with international project staff.

Another is to reserve part of the budget for any project in Haiti to train the local partner in financial, business and management skills, as the MIF has done through a project supporting ACME, a microfinance institution based in Port-au-Prince. The project provided human resource training, financial education, and integrated online communication services to support ACME in opening new branches in secondary cities and expanding its microcredit portfolio.

Of all the things we should be doing in Haiti, this is one I believe can have a great impact in the long term: working with stronger partners means better project execution and better results.
